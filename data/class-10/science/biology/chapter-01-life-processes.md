# Chapter 1: Life Processes

## Chapter Overview

Life processes are the basic maintenance processes that keep living organisms alive. Even when an organism is not visibly moving, its body keeps carrying out internal activities such as nutrition, respiration, transport, and excretion.

Visible movement alone is not enough to decide whether something is living. Living organisms show continuous molecular movements inside their cells. These movements help maintain organized body structures, repair damage, obtain energy, remove waste, and support growth.

Main life processes covered in this chapter:

- Nutrition
- Respiration
- Transportation
- Excretion

---

## 1. What Are Life Processes?

Life processes are the biological processes that perform maintenance work in living organisms.

Organisms need continuous maintenance because:

- Body structures are made of molecules.
- Molecules and cells are constantly undergoing changes.
- Cells and tissues need repair.
- Energy is required for all body activities.
- Waste products must be removed.

Important point:

> The processes that together perform the maintenance function in living organisms are called life processes.

Main examples:

- Nutrition: obtaining food and raw materials.
- Respiration: breaking down food to release energy.
- Transportation: moving materials inside the body.
- Excretion: removing harmful metabolic wastes.

---

## 2. Nutrition

Nutrition is the process by which organisms take in food and use it for energy, growth, repair, and maintenance.

Food provides:

- Energy
- Carbon-based raw materials
- Proteins and other building materials
- Minerals and nutrients needed by cells

Organisms use different methods of nutrition depending on their body structure, food source, and environment.

There are two main types of nutrition:

1. Autotrophic nutrition
2. Heterotrophic nutrition

---

## 3. Autotrophic Nutrition

Autotrophic nutrition is the mode of nutrition in which organisms prepare their own food from simple inorganic substances.

Examples:

- Green plants
- Some bacteria

Green plants prepare food by photosynthesis.

### Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants use carbon dioxide and water in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll to make carbohydrates.

General equation:

```txt
6CO2 + 6H2O  --sunlight/chlorophyll-->  C6H12O6 + 6O2
```

In words:

```txt
Carbon dioxide + Water  --sunlight/chlorophyll-->  Glucose + Oxygen
```

Carbohydrates formed during photosynthesis are used by plants for energy. Extra carbohydrates are stored as starch.

### Main Events During Photosynthesis

During photosynthesis, three major events occur:

1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll.
2. Conversion of light energy into chemical energy and splitting of water into hydrogen and oxygen.
3. Reduction of carbon dioxide into carbohydrates.

These steps may not happen immediately one after another. In desert plants, carbon dioxide may be taken in at night and used during the day when sunlight is available.

---

## 4. Raw Materials Required for Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis requires:

| Raw Material / Condition | Source / Role |
|---|---|
| Carbon dioxide | Enters leaves mainly through stomata |
| Water | Absorbed by roots from soil |
| Sunlight | Provides energy |
| Chlorophyll | Absorbs light energy |
| Minerals | Absorbed from soil and used for making proteins and other compounds |

### Chlorophyll

Chlorophyll is the green pigment present in chloroplasts. It traps sunlight required for photosynthesis.

### Stomata

Stomata are tiny pores present on the surface of leaves. They allow exchange of gases.

Functions of stomata:

- Carbon dioxide enters the leaf.
- Oxygen exits the leaf.
- Water vapour is lost during transpiration.

Each stoma is controlled by guard cells. When guard cells swell, the pore opens. When guard cells shrink, the pore closes.

## 6. Heterotrophic Nutrition

Heterotrophic nutrition is the mode of nutrition in which organisms depend directly or indirectly on other organisms for food.

Examples:

- Animals
- Fungi
- Most bacteria
- Humans

Heterotrophs use complex food materials prepared by autotrophs. These complex substances must be broken down into simpler molecules by enzymes before absorption.

### Types / Examples of Heterotrophic Nutrition

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Saprophytic nutrition | Food is broken down outside the body and then absorbed | Fungi, yeast, mushrooms |
| Holozoic nutrition | Food is ingested and digested inside the body | Humans, amoeba, animals |
| Parasitic nutrition | Food is obtained from a living host without killing it immediately | Cuscuta, ticks, lice, leech, tapeworm |

---

## 7. Nutrition in Amoeba

Amoeba is a unicellular organism.

Steps of nutrition in Amoeba:

1. Amoeba extends finger-like projections called pseudopodia.
2. Pseudopodia surround the food particle.
3. Food is enclosed in a food vacuole.
4. Digestive enzymes break complex food into simpler substances.
5. Digested food diffuses into the cytoplasm.
6. Undigested food moves to the cell surface and is thrown out.

Important term:

> Pseudopodia are temporary finger-like projections used by Amoeba for movement and food capture.

## 8. Nutrition in Humans

Humans have a complex digestive system. The alimentary canal is a long tube extending from the mouth to the anus.

Main organs of the human digestive system:

- Mouth
- Oesophagus
- Stomach
- Small intestine
- Large intestine
- Rectum
- Anus

Associated glands:

- Salivary glands
- Liver
- Pancreas

---

## 9. Human Digestive System

### 9.1 Mouth

Digestion begins in the mouth.

Functions:

- Teeth break food into smaller pieces.
- Saliva moistens food.
- Tongue mixes food with saliva.
- Salivary amylase digests starch into sugar.

Important enzyme:

```txt
Salivary amylase: starch → sugar
```

### 9.2 Oesophagus

The oesophagus carries food from the mouth to the stomach.

Food moves through the alimentary canal by rhythmic contraction and relaxation of muscles. This movement is called peristalsis.

### 9.3 Stomach

The stomach is a muscular organ that stores and mixes food with gastric juice.

Gastric glands secrete:

- Hydrochloric acid
- Pepsin
- Mucus

Functions:

| Secretion | Function |
|---|---|
| Hydrochloric acid | Makes the medium acidic and helps pepsin work |
| Pepsin | Digests proteins |
| Mucus | Protects stomach lining from acid |

Hydrochloric acid also helps kill many harmful microbes present in food.

### 9.4 Small Intestine

The small intestine is the main site for complete digestion and absorption of food.

It receives secretions from:

- Liver
- Pancreas
- Intestinal glands

Functions:

- Digestion of carbohydrates
- Digestion of proteins
- Digestion of fats
- Absorption of digested food

### 9.5 Liver and Bile

The liver secretes bile juice. Bile is stored in the gall bladder.

Functions of bile:

- Makes acidic food from the stomach alkaline.
- Breaks large fat globules into smaller droplets.
- Helps enzymes act efficiently on fats.

Breaking fat into tiny droplets is called emulsification.

### 9.6 Pancreas

The pancreas secretes pancreatic juice.

Important pancreatic enzymes:

| Enzyme | Function |
|---|---|
| Trypsin | Digests proteins |
| Lipase | Digests emulsified fats |

### 9.7 Intestinal Juice

The glands in the wall of the small intestine secrete intestinal juice.

It completes digestion:

```txt
Proteins → Amino acids
Complex carbohydrates → Glucose
Fats → Fatty acids + Glycerol
```

### 9.8 Villi

The inner lining of the small intestine has many finger-like projections called villi.

Functions of villi:

- Increase surface area for absorption.
- Absorb digested food.
- Pass absorbed food into blood vessels.

### 9.9 Large Intestine

The large intestine absorbs water from undigested food.

The remaining waste is removed through the anus. This removal is controlled by the anal sphincter.

---

## 10. Respiration

Respiration is the process by which food is broken down inside cells to release energy.

The energy released during respiration is used to make ATP.

ATP is the energy currency of the cell.

### Cellular Respiration

The first step of respiration is the breakdown of glucose into pyruvate.

This occurs in the cytoplasm:

```txt
Glucose (6-carbon molecule) → Pyruvate (3-carbon molecule) + Energy
```

After this, pyruvate can follow different pathways depending on the presence or absence of oxygen.

---

## 11. Pathways of Glucose Breakdown

### 11.1 Anaerobic Respiration in Yeast

In the absence of oxygen, pyruvate is converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide.

```txt
Glucose → Pyruvate → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy
```

This process occurs in yeast during fermentation.

### 11.2 Anaerobic Respiration in Muscles

When oxygen is insufficient in muscle cells, pyruvate is converted into lactic acid.

```txt
Glucose → Pyruvate → Lactic acid + Energy
```

Accumulation of lactic acid in muscles can cause cramps.

### 11.3 Aerobic Respiration

In the presence of oxygen, pyruvate is broken down completely into carbon dioxide and water.

```txt
Glucose → Pyruvate → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
```

This process occurs in mitochondria.

Aerobic respiration releases much more energy than anaerobic respiration.

---

## 12. ATP

ATP stands for adenosine triphosphate.

ATP is used by cells for:

- Muscle contraction
- Protein synthesis
- Nerve impulse transmission
- Active transport
- Other cellular activities

Energy is released when ATP breaks down:

```txt
ATP → ADP + Pi + Energy
```

---

## 13. Respiration in Plants

Plants exchange gases through stomata and intercellular spaces.

Gas exchange in plants happens by diffusion.

During the day:

- Photosynthesis uses carbon dioxide.
- Oxygen is released as the main product.
- Carbon dioxide produced by respiration is often used in photosynthesis.

At night:

- Photosynthesis does not occur.
- Carbon dioxide is released due to respiration.

---

## 14. Respiration in Aquatic and Terrestrial Animals

Aquatic animals use oxygen dissolved in water.

Since the amount of dissolved oxygen in water is lower than oxygen in air, aquatic animals generally breathe faster than terrestrial animals.

Fish take water through the mouth and force it over the gills. Dissolved oxygen diffuses into blood at the gills.

Terrestrial animals use atmospheric oxygen. Different animals have different respiratory organs, but the respiratory surface must be:

- Thin
- Moist
- Large in surface area
- Richly supplied with blood vessels

---

## 15. Human Respiratory System

Main parts of the human respiratory system:

- Nostrils
- Nasal passage
- Pharynx
- Larynx
- Trachea
- Bronchi
- Bronchioles
- Lungs
- Alveoli
- Diaphragm

### Path of Air

```txt
Nostrils → Nasal passage → Throat → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli
```

### Function of Nasal Passage

The nasal passage contains fine hair and mucus. These help filter dust and other impurities from inhaled air.

### Trachea

The trachea has rings of cartilage. These rings prevent the air passage from collapsing.

### Alveoli

Alveoli are balloon-like structures in the lungs where exchange of gases takes place.

Features of alveoli:

- Large surface area
- Thin walls
- Rich network of blood capillaries
- Moist surface for diffusion

Gas exchange:

- Oxygen diffuses from alveolar air into blood.
- Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli.

Human lungs always contain residual air, which gives enough time for oxygen absorption and carbon dioxide release.

---

## 16. Transport of Gases in Humans

Oxygen is transported by haemoglobin present in red blood cells.

Carbon dioxide is more soluble in water, so it is mostly transported in dissolved form in blood plasma.

Important points:

- Haemoglobin has high affinity for oxygen.
- Deficiency of haemoglobin reduces oxygen supply to body cells.
- Reduced oxygen supply may cause tiredness, weakness, and poor body functioning.

---

## 17. Transportation

Transportation is the movement of substances from one part of the body to another.

Substances transported in humans include:

- Oxygen
- Carbon dioxide
- Digested food
- Nitrogenous wastes
- Hormones
- Salts
- Water

In humans, transport occurs through the circulatory system.

---

## 18. Human Circulatory System

Main components:

- Heart
- Blood
- Blood vessels

### 18.1 Blood

Blood is a fluid connective tissue.

Main components:

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Plasma | Transports food, carbon dioxide, nitrogenous wastes, salts |
| Red blood cells | Transport oxygen |
| White blood cells | Help in defense |
| Platelets | Help in blood clotting |

### 18.2 Heart

The human heart is a muscular pumping organ about the size of a fist.

It has four chambers:

- Right atrium
- Right ventricle
- Left atrium
- Left ventricle

The heart keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood separate.

### 18.3 Flow of Blood Through the Heart

1. Oxygenated blood from the lungs enters the left atrium.
2. Left atrium contracts and sends blood to the left ventricle.
3. Left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood to the body.
4. Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the right atrium.
5. Right atrium sends blood to the right ventricle.
6. Right ventricle pumps blood to the lungs for oxygenation.

Valves prevent the backward flow of blood.

### 18.4 Double Circulation

In humans, blood passes through the heart twice in one complete cycle.

This is called double circulation.

Two circuits:

1. Pulmonary circulation: heart → lungs → heart
2. Systemic circulation: heart → body → heart

Double circulation is necessary because it keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood separate and supports efficient oxygen supply.

---

## 19. Blood Vessels

### Arteries

Arteries carry blood away from the heart.

Features:

- Thick walls
- Elastic walls
- Blood flows under high pressure

Usually arteries carry oxygenated blood, except the pulmonary artery.

### Veins

Veins carry blood from body organs back to the heart.

Features:

- Thinner walls than arteries
- Lower blood pressure
- Valves prevent backflow

Usually veins carry deoxygenated blood, except the pulmonary vein.

### Capillaries

Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels.

Features:

- One-cell-thick walls
- Allow exchange of materials between blood and surrounding cells
- Connect arteries and veins

---

## 20. Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is the force exerted by blood against the walls of blood vessels.

Normal blood pressure is about:

```txt
120/80 mm Hg
```

Where:

- 120 mm Hg = systolic pressure
- 80 mm Hg = diastolic pressure

High blood pressure is also called hypertension. It may damage arteries and cause internal bleeding.

---

## 21. Platelets and Blood Clotting

Platelets help prevent blood loss.

When a blood vessel is injured, platelets help form a clot at the site of injury. This blocks the leak and reduces blood loss.

---

## 22. Lymph

Lymph is also called tissue fluid.

Formation:

- Some plasma, proteins, and blood cells pass out through capillary walls into intercellular spaces.
- This fluid forms tissue fluid or lymph.

Features:

- Colourless
- Similar to plasma
- Contains less protein than plasma

Functions:

- Transports absorbed fat from the small intestine.
- Drains excess fluid from intercellular spaces back into blood.
- Helps in transport and immunity.

---

## 23. Transportation in Plants

Plants need transport systems because roots absorb water and minerals, while leaves prepare food.

Main transport tissues:

| Tissue | Function |
|---|---|
| Xylem | Transports water and minerals from roots to other parts |
| Phloem | Transports food from leaves to other parts |

---

## 24. Transport of Water and Minerals in Plants

Xylem transports water and minerals.

Process:

1. Root cells actively absorb ions from soil.
2. This creates a concentration difference between root and soil.
3. Water enters roots from soil.
4. A continuous column of water is formed in xylem.
5. Water moves upward through xylem.

### Transpiration

Transpiration is the loss of water in the form of vapour from aerial parts of the plant, mainly through stomata.

Functions of transpiration:

- Helps upward movement of water.
- Helps absorption and transport of minerals.
- Produces transpiration pull.
- Helps in temperature regulation.

During the day, when stomata are open, transpiration pull is the major force for movement of water in xylem.

---

## 25. Translocation of Food in Plants

Transport of soluble products of photosynthesis is called translocation.

Translocation occurs through phloem.

Phloem transports:

- Sucrose
- Amino acids
- Other soluble substances

Direction of transport:

- Upward
- Downward

Food is transported to:

- Roots
- Fruits
- Seeds
- Growing regions
- Storage organs

Translocation in phloem requires energy from ATP.

Process:

1. Sucrose is transferred into phloem tissue using ATP.
2. Osmotic pressure increases.
3. Water enters the phloem.
4. Pressure moves food materials to tissues where pressure is lower.

---

## 26. Excretion

Excretion is the biological process by which harmful metabolic wastes are removed from the body.

Wastes are produced during metabolic activities.

Examples:

- Carbon dioxide
- Urea
- Uric acid
- Excess salts
- Excess water

Unicellular organisms remove wastes by diffusion through the body surface.

Multicellular organisms use specialized organs for excretion.

---

## 27. Excretion in Humans

The human excretory system includes:

- A pair of kidneys
- Ureters
- Urinary bladder
- Urethra

### Path of Urine

```txt
Kidneys → Ureters → Urinary bladder → Urethra → Outside body
```

### Kidneys

Kidneys are located in the abdomen, one on each side of the backbone.

Functions:

- Filter blood.
- Remove nitrogenous wastes.
- Regulate water balance.
- Form urine.

Nitrogenous wastes such as urea and uric acid are removed from blood in the kidneys.

---

## 28. Nephron

Nephron is the basic filtration unit of the kidney.

Each kidney has many nephrons.

Main parts:

- Glomerulus
- Bowman's capsule
- Tubule
- Collecting duct

### Urine Formation

Urine formation involves:

1. Filtration
2. Selective reabsorption
3. Collection of urine

### 28.1 Filtration

Blood enters the glomerulus. Small molecules such as glucose, amino acids, salts, urea, and water are filtered into Bowman's capsule.

### 28.2 Selective Reabsorption

As the filtrate passes through the tubule, useful substances are reabsorbed into blood.

Reabsorbed substances include:

- Glucose
- Amino acids
- Required salts
- Required amount of water

The amount of water reabsorbed depends on:

- How much excess water is present in the body.
- How much dissolved waste has to be excreted.

### 28.3 Urine Storage and Removal

Urine from each kidney enters the ureter and reaches the urinary bladder.

The bladder stores urine until pressure causes the urge to urinate.

Urination is under nervous control because the bladder is muscular.

---

## 29. Artificial Kidney / Dialysis

Kidneys are vital organs. If kidneys fail, wastes accumulate in the body and may cause death.

An artificial kidney removes nitrogenous wastes from blood by dialysis.

Principle:

- Blood is passed through tubes made of selectively permeable membrane.
- These tubes are surrounded by dialysis fluid.
- Dialysis fluid has the same osmotic pressure as blood but no nitrogenous waste.
- Waste products diffuse from blood into dialysis fluid.
- Purified blood is pumped back into the body.

Difference from normal kidney:

> Dialysis removes wastes by diffusion, but it does not perform selective reabsorption like a real nephron.

---

## 30. Excretion in Plants

Plants use different methods for excretion.

Methods:

- Oxygen produced during photosynthesis is released.
- Excess water is removed by transpiration.
- Waste products are stored in vacuoles.
- Waste products are stored in old xylem as resins and gums.
- Wastes may be stored in leaves that later fall off.
- Some wastes are excreted into the surrounding soil.

Diffusion is too slow to transport oxygen, food, and wastes across long distances in large multicellular bodies. Therefore, specialized transport systems are needed.

Chlorophyll absorbs light energy from the sun and helps convert it into chemical energy.

Salivary amylase breaks starch into sugar.

## Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain the process of photosynthesis.

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants prepare food from carbon dioxide and water using sunlight and chlorophyll. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy. Light energy is converted into chemical energy, water is split into hydrogen and oxygen, and carbon dioxide is reduced to carbohydrates. Oxygen is released as a by-product. The carbohydrate formed is used for energy or stored as starch.

### 2. Explain human digestion.

Human digestion begins in the mouth, where food is chewed and mixed with saliva. Salivary amylase breaks starch into sugar. Food passes through the oesophagus into the stomach by peristalsis. In the stomach, HCl creates acidic medium, pepsin digests proteins, and mucus protects the stomach wall. Food then enters the small intestine, where bile emulsifies fats, pancreatic enzymes digest proteins and fats, and intestinal juice completes digestion. Digested food is absorbed by villi. Undigested food goes to the large intestine, where water is absorbed, and waste is removed through the anus.

### 3. Explain the different pathways of glucose breakdown.

Glucose first breaks down into pyruvate in the cytoplasm. In yeast, without oxygen, pyruvate forms ethanol and carbon dioxide. In muscles, when oxygen is insufficient, pyruvate forms lactic acid. In the presence of oxygen, pyruvate enters mitochondria and breaks down completely into carbon dioxide and water, releasing a large amount of energy.

### 4. Explain gas exchange in human lungs.

Air enters through nostrils and reaches alveoli through the trachea, bronchi, and bronchioles. Alveoli have thin walls and a rich network of blood capillaries. Oxygen diffuses from alveolar air into blood, while carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli. Haemoglobin in red blood cells carries oxygen to body tissues.

### 5. Explain double circulation in humans.

In double circulation, blood passes through the heart twice in one complete cycle. Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the right atrium and then the right ventricle, which pumps it to the lungs. Oxygenated blood from the lungs enters the left atrium and then the left ventricle, which pumps it to the body. This separation improves oxygen supply and prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

### 6. Explain transport of water in plants.

Roots absorb ions from soil, creating a concentration difference. Water enters root cells and moves into xylem. Xylem vessels form a continuous pathway from roots to leaves. Transpiration from leaves creates a suction force called transpiration pull, which helps pull water upward. Root pressure also contributes, especially at night.

### 7. Explain transport of food in plants.

Food prepared in leaves is transported through phloem. This process is called translocation. Sucrose is loaded into phloem using energy from ATP. This increases osmotic pressure, causing water to enter the phloem. The pressure moves food material to regions where it is needed or stored, such as roots, fruits, seeds, and growing parts.

### 8. Explain urine formation in humans.

Urine formation occurs in nephrons. Blood is filtered in the glomerulus, and small molecules enter Bowman's capsule. Useful substances such as glucose, amino acids, salts, and required water are selectively reabsorbed in the tubule. The remaining fluid containing urea and excess salts forms urine. Urine passes through the ureter to the urinary bladder and exits through the urethra.

### 9. Explain excretion in plants.

Plants remove waste in different ways. Oxygen from photosynthesis is released through stomata. Excess water is lost by transpiration. Some wastes are stored in vacuoles, old xylem, leaves, resins, and gums. Leaves containing wastes may fall off. Some wastes are also released into the surrounding soil.
