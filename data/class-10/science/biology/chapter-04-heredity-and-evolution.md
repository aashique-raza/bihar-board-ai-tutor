# Chapter 4: Heredity and Evolution

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains how variations arise during reproduction, how traits are inherited from parents to offspring, and how these inherited variations can lead to evolution over many generations.

Main topics covered in this chapter:

- Accumulation of variations during reproduction

- Mendel's experiments on pea plants

- Monohybrid and dihybrid inheritance

## 1. Variation During Reproduction

Reproduction produces new individuals that resemble their parents but are not always exactly identical to them.

During reproduction, DNA is copied and passed from one generation to the next.

DNA copying is not always perfectly accurate. Small errors can occur during copying. These errors create variations.

These variations may be:

- Very small and harmless
- Harmful to the organism
- Useful in a particular environment
- Passed from parents to offspring if present in reproductive cells

Variation is the basis of heredity and evolution.

Simple flow:

```txt
DNA copying
↓
Small copying errors
↓
Variations
↓
Inheritance of some variations
↓
Evolution over many generations
```

---

## 2. Accumulation of Variations During Reproduction

Every generation receives:

- Basic body design from the previous generation
- Some variations from parents
- Some new variations produced during reproduction

In asexual reproduction, offspring are very similar to the parent because only one parent is involved. Still, minor variations can occur due to DNA copying errors.

In sexual reproduction, variations are greater because genetic material from two parents combines.

Therefore:

```txt
Asexual reproduction → less variation
Sexual reproduction → more variation
```

Variation accumulates over generations.

Example:

If one bacterium divides into two bacteria, the two offspring are almost identical. When these bacteria divide again, small new differences may appear. After many generations, the population may contain several slightly different individuals.

---

## 3. Importance of Variation

Variation is useful for the survival of a species.

If all organisms of a species are exactly identical, a sudden environmental change may destroy the entire population.

If some organisms have variations, a few may survive under changed conditions.

Example:

A population of bacteria lives in temperate water. If global warming increases the temperature of water, most bacteria may die. But if some bacteria have heat-resistant variation, they may survive and multiply.

Thus, variation helps species survive environmental changes.

Important point:

Variation is more important for the survival of a species than for the survival of a single individual.

---

## 4. Heredity

Heredity is the transfer of traits from parents to offspring.

Traits are characters or features of an organism.

Examples of traits:

- Height of plant
- Shape of seed
- Colour of flower
- Eye colour
- Ear lobe type
- Blood group

Heredity explains why children resemble their parents but are not exact copies.

In humans, a child receives genetic material from both parents. Therefore, traits can be influenced by both the mother and the father.

---

## 5. Inherited Traits

Inherited traits are traits passed from parents to offspring through genes.

Examples:

- Free or attached ear lobes
- Blood group
- Natural eye colour
- Natural hair texture
- Height tendency

Traits that are acquired during the lifetime of an organism are generally not inherited.

Example:

If a person develops strong muscles by exercise, the children will not automatically inherit those strong muscles.

---

## 6. Ear Lobe Example

In humans, ear lobes may be of two types:

1. Free ear lobe
2. Attached ear lobe

This trait varies among individuals.

By observing children and their parents, we can study how such traits are inherited.

This type of observation helps us understand that some traits pass from one generation to the next.

---

## 7. Mendel's Contribution to Heredity

Gregor Johann Mendel studied inheritance using pea plants.

He is called the father of genetics.

Mendel selected pea plants because they had clear contrasting traits.

Examples of contrasting traits in pea plants:

| Trait | Variant 1 | Variant 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Plant height | Tall | Dwarf |
| Seed shape | Round | Wrinkled |
| Flower colour | Purple | White |
| Seed colour | Yellow | Green |

Mendel crossed pea plants with different traits and studied the traits of their offspring.

His experiments showed that traits are inherited according to definite rules.

---

## 8. Mendel's Monohybrid Cross

A monohybrid cross studies the inheritance of one pair of contrasting traits.

Example:

Mendel crossed a tall pea plant with a dwarf pea plant.

```txt
Tall plant × Dwarf plant
↓
F1 generation: All tall plants
```

Observation:

All plants in the F1 generation were tall. No plant had intermediate height.

This showed that only one of the two contrasting traits appeared in the F1 generation.

Then Mendel allowed F1 plants to self-pollinate.

```txt
F1 tall plant × F1 tall plant
↓
F2 generation: Tall and dwarf plants
```

In the F2 generation:

```txt
Tall plants : Dwarf plants = 3 : 1
```

This showed that the dwarf trait was present in the F1 generation but was hidden.

---

## 9. Dominant and Recessive Traits

A dominant trait is a trait that appears in the offspring even when only one copy of the gene is present.

A recessive trait is a trait that appears only when both copies of the gene are recessive.

In Mendel's pea plant experiment:

- Tallness was dominant.
- Dwarfness was recessive.

Symbolically:

```txt
T = gene for tallness
 t = gene for dwarfness
```

Possible combinations:

| Gene Combination | Trait Shown |
|---|---|
| TT | Tall |
| Tt | Tall |
| tt | Dwarf |

Only `tt` plants are dwarf.

`TT` and `Tt` plants are tall because `T` is dominant.

---

## 10. Genotype and Phenotype

Genotype means the genetic constitution of an organism.

Phenotype means the visible expression of a trait.

Example:

| Genotype | Phenotype |
|---|---|
| TT | Tall plant |
| Tt | Tall plant |
| tt | Dwarf plant |

Two plants may look tall, but their genotypes may be different.

```txt
TT → Tall
Tt → Tall
```

Thus, the same visible trait may come from different genetic combinations.

---

## 11. F1 and F2 Generations

F1 generation means the first filial generation obtained from parental cross.

F2 generation means the second filial generation obtained by selfing or crossing F1 individuals.

Example:

```txt
Parent generation:
TT × tt
↓
F1 generation:
All Tt → Tall
↓
F2 generation:
TT, Tt, Tt, tt
↓
Phenotype ratio:
3 Tall : 1 Dwarf
```

---

## 12. Mendel's Dihybrid Cross

A dihybrid cross studies inheritance of two pairs of contrasting traits.

Example:

Mendel crossed pea plants with:

- Round yellow seeds
- Wrinkled green seeds

In the F1 generation, all seeds were round and yellow.

This showed:

- Round seed shape is dominant over wrinkled seed shape.
- Yellow seed colour is dominant over green seed colour.

When F1 plants were self-pollinated, the F2 generation showed new combinations:

- Round yellow
- Round green
- Wrinkled yellow
- Wrinkled green

This proved that different traits are inherited independently.

---

## 13. Independent Inheritance of Traits

Mendel's dihybrid cross showed that different traits are inherited independently.

For example:

Seed shape and seed colour are inherited independently.

A plant can be:

- Round yellow
- Round green
- Wrinkled yellow
- Wrinkled green

This means inheritance of seed shape does not necessarily control inheritance of seed colour.

This is called independent assortment.

---

## 14. Genes

A gene is a segment of DNA that carries information for making a specific protein.

Genes control traits by controlling protein synthesis.

A change in a gene can change the protein formed. This can change the trait.

Example:

Plant height depends on hormones that control growth.

Hormone production depends on enzymes.

Enzymes are proteins.

Proteins are made using information present in genes.

So:

```txt
Gene
↓
Protein / enzyme
↓
Hormone production
↓
Trait expression
↓
Tall or dwarf plant
```

If a gene produces an efficient enzyme, more growth hormone may be produced and the plant becomes tall.

If the gene produces a less efficient enzyme, less hormone may be produced and the plant becomes dwarf.

---

## 15. Chromosomes and Heredity

Chromosomes are thread-like structures present in the nucleus.

They contain DNA and genes.

In sexually reproducing organisms, each cell usually has two copies of each chromosome:

- One copy from the mother
- One copy from the father

During formation of gametes, chromosome number becomes half.

During fertilisation, male and female gametes fuse and restore the normal chromosome number.

Flow:

```txt
Parent cells have paired chromosomes
↓
Gametes receive one chromosome from each pair
↓
Male gamete + female gamete
↓
Zygote has normal chromosome number again
```

This maintains chromosome number from generation to generation.

---

## 16. Sex Determination

Sex determination means the process by which the sex of an offspring is decided.

Different species use different methods of sex determination.

In some animals, sex may depend on environmental conditions such as temperature.

In humans, sex is determined genetically by sex chromosomes.

---

## 17. Sex Determination in Humans

Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes.

Out of these:

- 22 pairs are autosomes.
- 1 pair is sex chromosomes.

In females:

```txt
XX
```

In males:

```txt
XY
```

Female gametes always carry an X chromosome.

Male gametes may carry either:

- X chromosome
- Y chromosome

During fertilisation:

| Egg from Mother | Sperm from Father | Child |
|---|---|---|
| X | X | XX = Girl |
| X | Y | XY = Boy |

Therefore, the sex of a child depends on whether the sperm from the father carries X or Y chromosome.

Important point:

The mother always contributes X chromosome. The father contributes either X or Y chromosome.

## 18. Evolution

Evolution is the gradual change in inherited traits of a population over many generations.

Evolution occurs because variations arise and some variations become common in a population.

The basic idea of evolution:

```txt
Variation arises
↓
Some variations are inherited
↓
Some variations help survival or reproduction
↓
Such variations become common
↓
Population changes over generations
```

Evolution does not happen in a single individual. It happens in populations over generations.

---

## 19. Beetle Population Example

The textbook explains evolution using a population of beetles living in green bushes.

Suppose most beetles are red, and crows eat beetles.

### Case 1: Green Beetle Variation

A green beetle appears due to variation.

Green beetles are less visible on green leaves, so crows eat fewer green beetles.

Green beetles survive better and reproduce more.

Over generations, the number of green beetles increases.

This is natural selection.

### Case 2: Blue Beetle Variation and Accident

A blue beetle appears due to variation.

Blue beetles do not get a survival advantage because crows can still see them.

But by chance, an elephant crushes the bushes and kills many red beetles. Some blue beetles survive.

Then the blue beetle population increases by chance.

This is genetic drift.

### Case 3: Starvation and Body Weight

If plant disease reduces available leaves, beetles may become undernourished and lose body weight.

But this decrease in body weight is not inherited because it does not change the DNA of reproductive cells.

When food becomes available again, beetles can regain normal body weight.

This is an acquired trait, not an inherited trait.

---

## 20. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process in which nature selects organisms with useful variations.

Organisms with useful variations survive and reproduce better in a particular environment.

Over generations, these useful traits become more common in the population.

Example:

Green beetles survive better than red beetles in green bushes because crows cannot see them easily.

Therefore, the green colour becomes common in the beetle population.

Important point:

Natural selection depends on environment.

A trait useful in one environment may not be useful in another environment.

---

## 21. Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is a change in gene frequency in a small population due to chance events.

It does not necessarily provide any survival advantage.

Example:

In a small beetle population, an accident kills many beetles. If by chance mostly blue beetles survive, the blue trait may become common even though it gives no survival advantage.

Genetic drift is stronger in small populations.

---

## 22. Acquired Traits

Acquired traits are traits developed during the lifetime of an organism due to environmental influence, use, disuse, or experience.

Examples:

- Strong muscles developed by exercise
- Low body weight due to starvation
- Loss of tail by cutting
- Learning a skill

Acquired traits are generally not inherited because they do not change the DNA of reproductive cells.

---

## 23. Inherited Traits

Inherited traits are traits passed from parents to offspring through genes.

Examples:

- Eye colour
- Blood group
- Natural hair colour
- Natural skin colour
- Ear lobe type
- Genetic diseases

Only inherited traits can directly contribute to evolution.

---

## 24. Difference Between Acquired and Inherited Traits

| Acquired Traits | Inherited Traits |
|---|---|
| Developed during lifetime | Passed from parents to offspring |
| Caused by environment or experience | Caused by genes |
| Not usually passed to next generation | Passed to next generation |
| Do not directly cause evolution | Can contribute to evolution |
| Example: muscles from exercise | Example: blood group |

## 25. Origin of Life

The chapter discusses how life may have originated from non-living matter.

J.B.S. Haldane suggested in 1929 that life may have originated from simple inorganic molecules present on early Earth.

According to this idea:

- Early Earth had a different atmosphere from today.
- The early atmosphere probably lacked oxygen.
- It contained gases such as methane, ammonia, hydrogen sulphide, and hydrogen.
- Energy from lightning or heat may have helped form complex organic molecules.
- These organic molecules may have led to the first primitive life forms.

---

## 26. Miller and Urey Experiment

Stanley L. Miller and Harold C. Urey performed an experiment in 1953 to test the possibility of chemical origin of life.

They created an artificial atmosphere similar to the early Earth atmosphere.

The setup contained:

- Methane
- Ammonia
- Hydrogen sulphide
- Hydrogen
- Water vapour
- No oxygen

They passed electric sparks through the gas mixture to simulate lightning.

After about one week, simple organic compounds were formed.

Some amino acids were also formed.

Amino acids are building blocks of proteins.

This experiment supported the idea that life could have originated from non-living chemical substances under early Earth conditions.

---

## 27. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of a new species from an existing species.

A species is a group of organisms that can reproduce among themselves and produce fertile offspring.

Speciation may occur when populations of the same species become separated and gradually accumulate differences.

Main factors causing speciation:

- Geographical isolation
- Genetic drift
- Natural selection
- Accumulation of variations
- Reduced gene flow
- Changes in chromosomes or DNA
- Reproductive isolation

---

## 28. Geographical Isolation

Geographical isolation occurs when a population is separated by physical barriers.

Examples of barriers:

- Mountain ranges
- Rivers
- Seas
- Deserts
- Long distances

If two populations are separated, they may stop breeding with each other.

Over many generations, they may accumulate different variations.

Eventually, they may become so different that they can no longer reproduce with each other.

Then they become separate species.

---

## 29. Gene Flow

Gene flow is the movement of genes from one population to another through breeding.

If individuals from two populations interbreed, genes move between the populations.

Gene flow keeps populations similar.

If gene flow stops because of geographical isolation, populations may become different.

---

## 30. Reproductive Isolation

Reproductive isolation occurs when members of two populations can no longer reproduce with each other.

This can happen due to:

- Different mating behaviour
- Different reproductive organs
- Different breeding seasons
- Chromosomal changes
- Gametes unable to fuse
- Offspring unable to survive or reproduce

Reproductive isolation is a key step in speciation.

---

## 31. Evolution and Classification

Classification groups organisms based on similarities and differences.

Evolution explains why organisms show similarities and differences.

Organisms with more similarities are more closely related and likely share a more recent common ancestor.

Organisms with fewer similarities are more distantly related.

Therefore, classification reflects evolutionary relationships.

Simple idea:

```txt
More similarities
↓
Closer evolutionary relationship
↓
More recent common ancestor
```

---

## 32. Characteristics Used in Classification

A characteristic is a visible or functional feature of an organism.

Examples:

- Presence or absence of nucleus
- Unicellular or multicellular body
- Ability to photosynthesise
- Presence of skeleton
- Body symmetry
- Presence of backbone
- Type of limbs

Basic characteristics are more important for classification than superficial characteristics.

Example:

Presence of a nucleus is more basic than body colour.

---

## 33. Common Ancestor

A common ancestor is an earlier organism from which two or more groups evolved.

Example:

A brother and sister have the same parents. Their cousins share grandparents but not the same parents.

Similarly, species that share more characteristics may have a recent common ancestor.

Species that share fewer characteristics may have a distant common ancestor.

---

## 34. Homologous Organs

Homologous organs have the same basic structure and origin but perform different functions.

Examples:

- Forelimb of human
- Forelimb of frog
- Forelimb of lizard
- Wing of bird

These organs have similar bone arrangement but are used differently.

| Organism | Forelimb Function |
|---|---|
| Human | Grasping |
| Frog | Support and movement |
| Lizard | Walking |
| Bird | Flying |

Homologous organs show common ancestry.

They provide evidence for evolution.

---

## 35. Analogous Organs

Analogous organs perform similar functions but have different basic structure and origin.

Examples:

- Wing of bird
- Wing of bat

Both are used for flying, but their structures are different.

In bats, the wing is mainly a fold of skin stretched between elongated fingers.

In birds, the wing is formed by the forelimb covered with feathers.

Thus, bird wings and bat wings are analogous organs.

Analogous organs show similar adaptation but not necessarily close common ancestry.

---

## 36. Fossils

Fossils are preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms that lived in the past.

Examples:

- Fossilised tree trunk
- Fossilised fish
- Fossilised dinosaur skull
- Fossilised invertebrates such as ammonites and trilobites

Fossils help us understand extinct organisms and evolutionary history.

They provide evidence that many organisms existed in the past but are now extinct.

---

## 37. Formation of Fossils

Usually, after death, the body of an organism decomposes.

Sometimes, the body or body parts get buried in mud, sand, or sediment.

If decomposition is prevented or slowed, the remains may get preserved.

Over time, layers of sediment harden into rock.

The preserved remains or impressions become fossils.

Flow:

```txt
Organism dies
↓
Body gets buried in sediment
↓
Decomposition is reduced
↓
Sediment hardens into rock
↓
Fossil forms
```

---

## 38. Fossil Dating

Fossil dating means determining the age of fossils.

Two broad methods are discussed:

### 1. Relative Dating

Fossils found in deeper layers of rocks are usually older than fossils found in upper layers.

This is because lower layers were formed earlier.

### 2. Isotope Dating

The age of fossils can be estimated by comparing the ratio of different isotopes of certain elements present in the fossil or surrounding rock.

This gives a more direct estimate of fossil age.

---

## 39. Evolution by Stages

Complex organs do not appear suddenly in one generation.

They evolve gradually through small useful stages.

Each intermediate stage may provide some advantage.

Example:

A simple eye spot that detects light can be useful even if it cannot form a clear image.

Over generations, such simple structures may become more complex.

This explains how complex organs like eyes can evolve step by step.

---

## 40. Evolution of Eye

The eye is a complex organ, but it likely evolved gradually.

A simple light-sensitive spot can help an organism detect light.

This provides survival advantage.

Later, more complex structures may evolve to detect direction, shape, and images.

Different organisms have different types of eyes.

The eye has evolved independently in different groups such as insects, octopus, and vertebrates.

---

## 41. Evolution of Feathers

Feathers may not have originally evolved for flight.

They may have first evolved for insulation and keeping the body warm.

Later, feathers were used for flight in birds.

Fossils of some dinosaurs show feather impressions.

This suggests that birds are closely related to reptiles.

Important point:

A structure that evolves for one function may later be used for another function.

---

## 42. Artificial Selection

Artificial selection is selection done by humans to produce desired traits in plants or animals.

Example:

Humans selected wild cabbage for different traits and produced several vegetables.

From wild cabbage, humans developed:

| Selected Trait | Vegetable Produced |
|---|---|
| Short distance between leaves | Cabbage |
| Arrested flower development | Broccoli |
| Sterile flowers | Cauliflower |
| Swollen stem | Kohlrabi |
| Broad leaves | Kale |
| Different colour forms | Red cabbage |

This shows that very different-looking plants can develop from a common ancestor through selection.

---

## 43. Molecular Phylogeny

Molecular phylogeny studies evolutionary relationships by comparing DNA or protein sequences of different organisms.

The basic idea:

- DNA changes accumulate over generations.
- More closely related organisms have fewer DNA differences.
- Distantly related organisms have more DNA differences.

Molecular studies often support classification based on visible characteristics.

---

## 44. Evolution Is Not Equal to Progress

Evolution should not be understood as a ladder of progress from lower to higher organisms.

Evolution produces diversity.

New species can arise without older species disappearing.

A newer species is not necessarily better than an older species.

Simple organisms such as bacteria still survive successfully in harsh environments.

Therefore, humans are not the final goal or highest point of evolution.

Humans are only one species among many species produced by evolution.

---

## 45. Human Evolution

Human evolution is studied using:

- Fossils
- Excavation
- Dating methods
- Comparative anatomy
- DNA sequence analysis

Modern humans belong to the species Homo sapiens.

All humans belong to the same species.

Differences in skin colour, body shape, or appearance do not form separate biological races.

Evidence shows that modern humans originated in Africa.

Some human groups remained in Africa, while others migrated to different parts of the world.

Humans spread from Africa to:

- West Asia
- Central Asia
- Eurasia
- South Asia
- East Asia
- Indonesia
- Philippines
- Australia
- Americas through the Bering land bridge

Human groups separated, migrated, mixed, and spread across the world.

Important point:

All humans are members of one species and share common ancestry.

## Important Comparisons

### Asexual Reproduction vs Sexual Reproduction

| Asexual Reproduction | Sexual Reproduction |
|---|---|
| One parent involved | Two parents usually involved |
| Less variation | More variation |
| Offspring are very similar | Offspring show more differences |
| DNA copying errors create small variation | Mixing of genes creates greater variation |

### Dominant vs Recessive Trait

| Dominant Trait | Recessive Trait |
|---|---|
| Expressed with one copy | Expressed only with two copies |
| Represented by capital letter | Represented by small letter |
| Example: Tallness in pea plant | Example: Dwarfness in pea plant |

### Homologous vs Analogous Organs

| Homologous Organs | Analogous Organs |
|---|---|
| Same basic structure | Different basic structure |
| Different functions | Same function |
| Same origin | Different origin |
| Show common ancestry | Show similar adaptation |
| Example: human arm and bird wing | Example: bird wing and bat wing |

### Natural Selection vs Genetic Drift

| Natural Selection | Genetic Drift |
|---|---|
| Based on survival advantage | Based on chance |
| Works strongly in all population sizes | Stronger in small populations |
| Produces adaptation | May not produce adaptation |
| Example: green beetles survive better | Example: random survival after accident |

### Acquired Trait vs Inherited Trait

| Acquired Trait | Inherited Trait |
|---|---|
| Developed during lifetime | Present due to genes |
| Not passed to offspring | Passed to offspring |
| Example: muscles from exercise | Example: blood group |
| Does not directly cause evolution | Can cause evolution |

## Short Answer Questions

### 1. What is heredity?

Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring.

### 2. What is variation?

Variation means differences among individuals of the same species.

### 3. Why are variations important?

Variations help a species survive environmental changes. Some variations may provide survival advantage.

### 4. Why does sexual reproduction produce more variation than asexual reproduction?

Sexual reproduction combines genetic material from two parents, creating new combinations of genes.

### 5. What is a gene?

A gene is a segment of DNA that carries information for making a specific protein.

### 6. What is a dominant trait?

A dominant trait is expressed even when only one copy of the gene is present.

### 7. What is a recessive trait?

A recessive trait is expressed only when both copies of the gene are recessive.

### 8. What was the result of Mendel's monohybrid cross?

The F1 generation showed only the dominant trait, and the F2 generation showed a 3:1 ratio of dominant to recessive traits.

### 9. What does Mendel's dihybrid cross prove?

It proves that different traits are inherited independently.

### 10. How is sex determined in humans?

A child receives X chromosome from the mother and either X or Y chromosome from the father. XX results in a girl, and XY results in a boy.

### 11. What is natural selection?

Natural selection is the process in which organisms with useful variations survive and reproduce better.

### 12. What is genetic drift?

Genetic drift is random change in gene frequency in a small population due to chance events.

### 13. Why are acquired traits not inherited?

Acquired traits do not change the DNA of reproductive cells, so they are not passed to offspring.

### 14. What is speciation?

Speciation is the formation of a new species from an existing species.

### 15. What is geographical isolation?

Geographical isolation is separation of populations by barriers such as rivers, mountains, or distance.

### 16. What are homologous organs?

Homologous organs have the same basic structure and origin but different functions.

### 17. What are analogous organs?

Analogous organs perform the same function but have different basic structures and origins.

### 18. What are fossils?

Fossils are preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms that lived in the past.

### 19. What is artificial selection?

Artificial selection is selection by humans to develop organisms with desired traits.

### 20. Why is evolution not equal to progress?

Evolution produces diversity and adaptation, not a fixed ladder from lower to higher organisms. Simple organisms can be highly successful.

## Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain how variations accumulate during reproduction.

During reproduction, DNA is copied and passed to offspring. DNA copying is not perfectly accurate, so small errors can occur. These errors create variations. Some variations are inherited by the next generation. In asexual reproduction, variation is smaller because only one parent is involved. In sexual reproduction, variation is greater because genetic material from two parents combines. Over many generations, inherited variations accumulate in a population.

### 2. Explain Mendel's monohybrid cross.

Mendel crossed tall pea plants with dwarf pea plants. In the F1 generation, all plants were tall. This showed that tallness is dominant over dwarfness. When F1 plants were self-pollinated, the F2 generation showed tall and dwarf plants in a 3:1 ratio. This showed that the dwarf trait was hidden in F1 but reappeared in F2. Therefore, each trait is controlled by two copies of a gene, one from each parent.

### 3. Explain dominant and recessive traits using pea plant height.

In pea plants, tallness is dominant and dwarfness is recessive. If `T` represents tallness and `t` represents dwarfness, then `TT` and `Tt` plants are tall because the dominant `T` expresses itself. Only `tt` plants are dwarf because both copies are recessive. This explains why dwarfness disappears in F1 generation but reappears in F2 generation.

### 4. Explain how traits are expressed.

Traits are controlled by genes. A gene is a segment of DNA that contains information for making a protein. Proteins influence body structure and function. For example, plant height depends on growth hormones, and hormone production depends on enzymes. Enzymes are proteins made according to gene instructions. If the gene produces an efficient enzyme, the plant may become tall. If the enzyme is less efficient, the plant may become dwarf.

### 5. Explain sex determination in humans.

Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes. Females have two X chromosomes, and males have one X and one Y chromosome. The mother always contributes an X chromosome through the egg. The father contributes either an X chromosome or a Y chromosome through sperm. If the sperm carrying X fertilises the egg, the child is XX and becomes a girl. If the sperm carrying Y fertilises the egg, the child is XY and becomes a boy. Therefore, the father's sperm determines the sex of the child.

### 6. Explain natural selection using the beetle example.

In a population of red beetles living on green bushes, a green beetle may appear due to variation. Since green beetles are less visible to crows, they are eaten less often. They survive better and reproduce more. Their offspring inherit the green colour. Over generations, the number of green beetles increases. This is natural selection because the environment favours a useful variation.

### 7. Explain genetic drift using the beetle example.

Genetic drift is random change in gene frequency due to chance. Suppose a blue beetle appears in a population, but its colour gives no survival advantage. If an accident, such as an elephant crushing bushes, kills many red beetles and only a few blue beetles survive by chance, the blue beetle population may increase. This change is not due to adaptation but due to chance, so it is genetic drift.

### 8. Why are acquired traits not inherited?

Acquired traits develop during the lifetime of an organism due to environment or experience. These changes usually occur in body cells and do not change DNA in reproductive cells. Since only changes in reproductive cell DNA can be passed to offspring, acquired traits are not inherited. For example, cutting the tails of mice for many generations will not produce tailless offspring.

### 9. Explain speciation.

Speciation is the formation of a new species. It can occur when a population is divided into separated groups by geographical barriers such as rivers or mountains. Gene flow between groups decreases. Each group accumulates different variations. Natural selection and genetic drift act differently in each group. Over many generations, the groups may become so different that they cannot reproduce with each other. Then they become separate species.

### 10. Explain the relation between evolution and classification.

Classification groups organisms based on similarities and differences. Evolution explains these similarities and differences through common ancestry. Organisms with more similarities are likely to have a recent common ancestor. Organisms with fewer similarities are more distantly related. Therefore, classification helps us understand evolutionary relationships among organisms.

### 11. Differentiate between homologous and analogous organs.

Homologous organs have the same basic structure and origin but different functions. For example, forelimbs of humans, frogs, lizards, and birds have similar bone arrangement but perform different functions. Analogous organs have similar functions but different structures and origins. For example, wings of birds and bats are used for flying but have different structures. Homologous organs show common ancestry, while analogous organs show similar adaptation.

### 12. Explain how fossils provide evidence for evolution.

Fossils are preserved remains or impressions of organisms that lived in the past. They show that many organisms existed earlier but are now extinct. Fossils found in deeper rock layers are generally older than fossils found in upper layers. Fossils help us compare past organisms with present organisms and understand evolutionary changes. They also provide evidence of transitional forms and the history of life on Earth.

### 13. Explain the origin of life according to Haldane and Miller-Urey experiment.

Haldane suggested that life may have originated from simple inorganic molecules present on early Earth. The early atmosphere was different from today's atmosphere and probably lacked oxygen. Miller and Urey tested this idea by creating an artificial early atmosphere containing gases like methane, ammonia, hydrogen sulphide, hydrogen, and water vapour. They passed electric sparks through the mixture. After about a week, simple organic compounds including amino acids were formed. This supported the idea that life could have originated from non-living chemicals.

### 14. Explain evolution by stages.

Complex organs do not evolve suddenly. They evolve gradually through small useful intermediate stages. For example, a simple light-sensitive spot can help an organism detect light and survive better. Over generations, such simple structures can become more complex and develop into eyes. Similarly, feathers may have first evolved for insulation and later became useful for flight. Thus, complex structures evolve step by step.

### 15. Why should evolution not be considered progress?

Evolution does not mean progress from lower organisms to higher organisms. It means change in inherited traits of populations over generations. New species can arise without older species disappearing. Simple organisms like bacteria are still successful and survive in harsh environments. Humans are not the final goal of evolution. Humans are only one branch in the diversity produced by evolution.

### 16. Explain human evolution briefly.

Human evolution is studied using fossils, excavation, dating, anatomy, and DNA analysis. Modern humans belong to one species, Homo sapiens. Differences in skin colour and appearance do not mean separate biological races. Evidence suggests that modern humans originated in Africa. Some groups stayed in Africa while others migrated to different parts of the world. Human groups separated, mixed, and spread across the planet.
