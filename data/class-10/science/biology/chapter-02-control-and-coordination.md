# Chapter 2: Control and Coordination

## Chapter Overview

Living organisms respond to changes in their surroundings. These responses may be quick, slow, voluntary, involuntary, growth-related, or hormone-controlled.

Control and coordination are needed because different parts of the body must work together in a proper order. In animals, control and coordination are mainly performed by:

- Nervous system
- Muscles
- Hormones

In plants, control and coordination are mainly performed by:

- Chemical signals
- Plant hormones
- Growth movements
- Non-growth movements

Main topics covered in this chapter:

- Nervous system in animals
- Neuron and nerve impulse
- Synapse
- Reflex action and reflex arc
- Human brain
- Coordination in plants
- Tropic movements
- Plant hormones
- Hormones in animals
- Endocrine glands
- Feedback mechanism

---

## 1. Need for Control and Coordination

Organisms constantly receive information from their environment and respond to it.

Examples:

- A cat runs after seeing a mouse.
- A child moves while playing on a swing.
- A buffalo chews cud to help digestion.
- We pull our hand away after touching a hot object.
- Plants grow towards sunlight.

These actions are not random. They are controlled responses to a stimulus.

A stimulus is any change in the environment that causes a response.

Examples of stimuli:

- Light
- Heat
- Touch
- Sound
- Smell
- Taste
- Gravity
- Water
- Chemicals

Control and coordination help organisms:

- Detect changes in the environment.
- Send information to the correct body part.
- Produce a suitable response.
- Coordinate different organs and tissues.
- Maintain body functions.

---

## 2. Control and Coordination in Animals

In animals, control and coordination are mainly carried out by the nervous system and the endocrine system.

The nervous system gives fast responses using electrical impulses.

The endocrine system gives slower but longer-lasting responses using hormones.

Animal coordination involves:

- Receptors that detect stimuli.
- Nerves that carry information.
- Brain and spinal cord that process information.
- Muscles or glands that produce responses.

---

## 3. Animal Nervous System

The nervous system is made of nerve cells called neurons.

The nervous system helps animals:

- Receive information from the environment.
- Transfer information inside the body.
- Process information.
- Send instructions to muscles and glands.
- Coordinate voluntary and involuntary actions.

Examples of sensory receptors:

- Gustatory receptors: detect taste.
- Olfactory receptors: detect smell.
- Photoreceptors: detect light.
- Thermoreceptors: detect temperature.
- Pain receptors: detect pain.

---

## 4. Neuron / Nerve Cell

A neuron is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system.

Main parts of a neuron:

- Dendrites
- Cell body
- Nucleus
- Axon
- Nerve ending

### 4.1 Dendrites

Dendrites receive information from receptors or other neurons.

They convert the information into electrical impulses.

### 4.2 Cell Body

The cell body contains the nucleus and cytoplasm.

It processes the information received from dendrites.

### 4.3 Axon

The axon is a long fibre that carries electrical impulses away from the cell body.

### 4.4 Nerve Ending

The nerve ending passes the message to the next neuron, muscle cell, or gland.

---

## 5. Transmission of Nerve Impulse

A nerve impulse travels in this order:

```txt
Dendrite
↓
Cell body
↓
Axon
↓
Nerve ending
↓
Synapse
↓
Next neuron / muscle / gland
```

The information is first received at the dendritic end of a neuron. This causes a chemical reaction that creates an electrical impulse.

The electrical impulse travels from dendrite to cell body, then along the axon to the nerve ending.

At the nerve ending, the electrical impulse causes the release of chemicals.

These chemicals cross the gap called synapse and start a similar electrical impulse in the next neuron.

---

## 6. Synapse

A synapse is the small gap between two neurons.

At the synapse:

- Electrical impulse reaches the nerve ending.
- Chemicals are released.
- Chemicals cross the gap.
- The next neuron receives the signal.
- A new electrical impulse starts.

Important point:

> A nerve impulse passes from one neuron to another through chemicals released at the synapse.

Synapse helps in one-way transmission of nerve impulses.

---

## 7. Nerve-Muscle Junction

A nerve impulse can also be passed from a neuron to a muscle cell.

This connection is called a nerve-muscle junction.

When the impulse reaches the muscle cell:

- Special chemicals are released.
- Muscle proteins change their arrangement.
- Muscle cells change shape.
- The muscle contracts or relaxes.
- Movement occurs.

This is how nervous information finally produces physical movement.

---

## 8. Reflex Action

A reflex action is a sudden, automatic, and quick response to a stimulus.

It happens without conscious thinking.

Examples:

- Pulling hand away from a hot object.
- Blinking when bright light falls on the eyes.
- Salivation when we see tasty food.
- Knee jerk response.
- Moving away from a sharp object.

Important point:

> Reflex actions are quick responses that protect the body from harm.

Reflex action is useful because waiting for the brain to think and decide may take too much time.

---

## 9. Reflex Arc

The path followed by nerve impulses during a reflex action is called a reflex arc.

A reflex arc usually involves:

- Receptor
- Sensory neuron
- Spinal cord
- Motor neuron
- Effector muscle

Flow of reflex arc:

```txt
Stimulus
↓
Receptor
↓
Sensory neuron
↓
Spinal cord
↓
Motor neuron
↓
Effector muscle
↓
Response
```

Example: touching a hot object

```txt
Heat/pain receptor in skin
↓
Sensory neuron carries impulse
↓
Spinal cord receives impulse
↓
Motor neuron carries command
↓
Arm muscle contracts
↓
Hand is pulled back
```

Reflex arcs are mostly formed in the spinal cord, although the information also reaches the brain later.

---

## 10. Why Reflex Arc Is Important

Reflex arc is important because:

- It saves time.
- It protects the body from injury.
- It does not require conscious thinking.
- It gives quick automatic response.
- It works even before the brain fully processes the event.

The spinal cord plays a major role in reflex action.

---

## 11. Human Nervous System

The human nervous system is divided into:

```txt
Human Nervous System
├── Central Nervous System
│   ├── Brain
│   └── Spinal cord
└── Peripheral Nervous System
    ├── Cranial nerves
    └── Spinal nerves
```

### Central Nervous System

The central nervous system includes:

- Brain
- Spinal cord

It receives information, processes it, and sends commands.

### Peripheral Nervous System

The peripheral nervous system includes nerves arising from the brain and spinal cord.

It connects the central nervous system with the rest of the body.

## 12. Human Brain

The brain is the main coordinating centre of the body.

Functions of the brain:

- Receives information from sensory organs.
- Processes information.
- Makes decisions.
- Controls voluntary actions.
- Controls many involuntary actions.
- Maintains balance and posture.
- Controls thinking, memory, emotions, and learning.

The brain has three main regions:

- Forebrain
- Midbrain
- Hindbrain

---

## 13. Forebrain

The forebrain is the main thinking part of the brain.

It receives sensory impulses from different receptors.

Functions of forebrain:

- Thinking
- Learning
- Memory
- Intelligence
- Voluntary actions
- Interpretation of sensory information
- Hunger-related control
- Smell
- Hearing
- Vision

Different areas of the forebrain are specialized for different functions such as hearing, smell, sight, and movement.

The forebrain also has association areas where sensory information is interpreted using previous memory and stored information.

---

## 14. Midbrain

The midbrain controls some involuntary actions and acts as a connection between different parts of the brain.

It is involved in responses related to sight and sound.

---

## 15. Hindbrain

The hindbrain includes:

- Cerebellum
- Pons
- Medulla

### 15.1 Cerebellum

The cerebellum controls:

- Balance
- Posture
- Coordination of voluntary movements
- Precision of movement

Examples of activities controlled with the help of cerebellum:

- Walking in a straight line
- Riding a bicycle
- Picking up a pencil
- Maintaining body posture

### 15.2 Pons

Pons helps in the regulation of breathing and acts as a bridge between different parts of the brain.

### 15.3 Medulla

Medulla controls involuntary actions such as:

- Heartbeat
- Blood pressure
- Salivation
- Vomiting
- Breathing-related control

---

## 16. Voluntary Actions

Voluntary actions are actions that are under conscious control.

Examples:

- Writing
- Speaking
- Walking
- Clapping
- Moving a chair
- Picking up a book

These actions are decided by the brain and carried out by muscles.

---

## 17. Involuntary Actions

Involuntary actions are actions that are not under conscious control.

Examples:

- Heartbeat
- Breathing
- Digestion
- Salivation
- Vomiting
- Change in pupil size
- Blood pressure control

Many involuntary actions are controlled by the midbrain and hindbrain.

---

| Point | Reflex Action | Voluntary Action |
|---|---|---|
| Control | Automatic | Under conscious control |
| Speed | Very fast | Comparatively slower |
| Thinking | Not required before action | Required |
| Main control centre | Spinal cord | Brain |
| Example | Pulling hand from hot object | Picking up a pen |

---

## 19. Difference Between Reflex Action and Involuntary Action

| Point | Reflex Action | Involuntary Action |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Sudden response to stimulus | Regular body function |
| Example | Pulling hand back from flame | Heartbeat |
| Control | Mainly spinal cord | Brain stem / autonomic control |
| Purpose | Protection / quick response | Maintenance of body functions |

## 20. Protection of Brain and Spinal Cord

The brain and spinal cord are soft and delicate organs.

They are protected because damage to them can affect body control and coordination.

### Brain Protection

The brain is protected by:

- Skull
- Fluid-filled space
- Meninges

The skull acts like a hard box.

The fluid around the brain acts as a shock absorber.

### Spinal Cord Protection

The spinal cord is protected by the vertebral column or backbone.

## 21. Coordination in Plants

Plants do not have a nervous system or muscles.

Still, they respond to stimuli.

Plant responses are controlled by:

- Chemical signals
- Hormones
- Growth changes
- Water movement inside cells

Examples:

- Touch-me-not leaves fold when touched.
- Shoot grows towards light.
- Roots grow downwards.
- Tendrils coil around a support.
- Pollen tube grows towards the ovule.

---

## 22. Immediate Response to Stimulus in Plants

Some plant movements are not related to growth.

Example:

- Leaves of touch-me-not plant fold quickly when touched.

This response happens because plant cells change their shape by changing the amount of water inside them.

Unlike animals, plants do not use specialized nervous tissue or muscle tissue for this response.

Important point:

> In touch-me-not plant, movement occurs due to changes in water amount in cells, not due to growth.

---

## 23. Growth-Dependent Movements in Plants

Some plant movements are caused by growth.

These movements are slow and directional.

Examples:

- Shoot grows towards light.
- Root grows towards gravity.
- Tendril grows around support.
- Pollen tube grows towards ovule.

Such movements are called tropic movements.

---

## 24. Tropic Movements

Tropic movement is a directional growth movement of a plant part in response to a stimulus.

If growth is towards the stimulus, it is positive tropism.

If growth is away from the stimulus, it is negative tropism.

Types of tropic movement:

- Phototropism
- Geotropism / gravitropism
- Hydrotropism
- Chemotropism
- Thigmotropism

---

## 25. Phototropism

Phototropism is the movement of a plant part in response to light.

Example:

- Shoot bends towards light.
- Root usually bends away from light.

In shoots, auxin moves to the shaded side. Cells on the shaded side grow faster, so the shoot bends towards light.

Flow:

```txt
Light comes from one side
↓
Auxin moves to shaded side of shoot
↓
Cells on shaded side elongate more
↓
Shoot bends towards light
```

---

## 26. Geotropism / Gravitropism

Geotropism is the growth movement of plant parts in response to gravity.

Examples:

- Roots grow towards gravity: positive geotropism.
- Shoots grow away from gravity: negative geotropism.

This helps roots go into the soil and shoots grow upwards.

---

## 27. Hydrotropism

Hydrotropism is the movement of plant parts in response to water.

Example:

- Roots grow towards water.

---

## 28. Chemotropism

Chemotropism is the movement of plant parts in response to chemicals.

Example:

- Growth of pollen tube towards the ovule.

The ovule releases chemical substances that guide the pollen tube.

---

## 29. Thigmotropism

Thigmotropism is the movement of plant parts in response to touch.

Example:

- Tendrils coil around a support.

In tendrils, the part touching the support grows slower than the part away from the support. This causes the tendril to bend around the object.

---

## 30. Plant Hormones

Plant hormones are chemical substances that control and coordinate growth, development, and responses to the environment in plants.

Important plant hormones:

- Auxin
- Gibberellin
- Cytokinin
- Abscisic acid

---

## 31. Auxin

Auxin is produced at the tip of the shoot.

Functions of auxin:

- Promotes cell elongation.
- Helps shoot bend towards light.
- Helps in growth movements.
- Helps tendrils coil around support.

In phototropism, auxin moves to the shaded side of the shoot and causes faster growth on that side.

---

## 32. Gibberellin

Gibberellin helps in stem growth.

Functions:

- Promotes growth of stem.
- Helps in growth and development of plants.

---

## 33. Cytokinin

Cytokinin promotes cell division.

It is found in higher concentration in regions where rapid cell division occurs.

Examples:

- Fruits
- Seeds
- Growing regions

Functions:

- Promotes cell division.
- Supports growth of new tissues.

---

## 34. Abscisic Acid

Abscisic acid is a growth-inhibiting hormone.

Functions:

- Inhibits growth.
- Causes wilting of leaves.
- Helps plants respond to stress.

Important point:

> Abscisic acid is an example of a hormone that inhibits plant growth.

---

## 35. Chemical Coordination in Animals

Animals use hormones for chemical coordination.

Hormones are chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands.

They are released directly into blood and transported to target organs.

Characteristics of hormones:

- Produced in small amounts.
- Secreted by endocrine glands.
- Travel through blood.
- Act on specific target organs.
- Produce slow but long-lasting effects.

---

## 36. Endocrine Glands

Endocrine glands are ductless glands.

They release hormones directly into the blood.

Important endocrine glands in humans:

- Pituitary gland
- Thyroid gland
- Adrenal gland
- Pancreas
- Testes
- Ovaries
- Hypothalamus
- Pineal gland
- Thymus gland
- Parathyroid gland

---

## 37. Adrenaline

Adrenaline is secreted by adrenal glands.

It prepares the body for emergency situations such as fear, anger, stress, fight, or flight.

Effects of adrenaline:

- Heartbeat increases.
- Breathing rate increases.
- Blood pressure increases.
- More blood goes to skeletal muscles.
- Less blood goes to digestive system and skin.
- More oxygen is supplied to muscles.
- Body becomes ready for quick action.

Flow:

```txt
Fear / danger / stress
↓
Adrenal gland secretes adrenaline
↓
Adrenaline enters blood
↓
Heart beats faster
↓
Breathing rate increases
↓
More blood reaches muscles
↓
Body is ready for fight or flight
```

---

## 38. Thyroxine

Thyroxine is secreted by the thyroid gland.

Iodine is necessary for the synthesis of thyroxine.

Functions of thyroxine:

- Controls metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats.
- Helps maintain growth and development.
- Keeps body functions balanced.

Deficiency of iodine may reduce thyroxine production and cause goitre.

Goitre is characterized by swelling in the neck region due to enlargement of the thyroid gland.

Important point:

> Iodised salt is advised because iodine is needed for the thyroid gland to make thyroxine.

---

## 39. Growth Hormone

Growth hormone is secreted by the pituitary gland.

Function:

- Controls growth and development of the body.

Deficiency of growth hormone during childhood may cause dwarfism.

Excess growth hormone may cause abnormal increase in height.

---

## 40. Sex Hormones

Sex hormones control changes during puberty and reproductive functions.

### Testosterone

Testosterone is secreted in males by testes.

It controls male secondary sexual characters.

### Estrogen

Estrogen is secreted in females by ovaries.

It controls female secondary sexual characters.

---

## 41. Insulin

Insulin is secreted by the pancreas.

Function:

- Controls blood sugar level.

If insulin is not secreted in proper amount, blood sugar level rises.

This condition may lead to diabetes.

Some diabetic patients are treated with insulin injections.

---

## 42. Feedback Mechanism

Hormone secretion must be controlled in correct quantity.

The body uses feedback mechanism to regulate hormone levels.

Example: insulin regulation

```txt
Blood sugar rises
↓
Pancreas detects high sugar level
↓
More insulin is secreted
↓
Blood sugar level decreases
↓
Insulin secretion reduces
```

Feedback mechanism prevents too much or too little hormone secretion.

---

## 43. Nervous Coordination vs Hormonal Coordination

| Point | Nervous Coordination | Hormonal Coordination |
|---|---|---|
| Signal type | Electrical impulse and chemicals at synapse | Chemical hormones |
| Speed | Very fast | Slower |
| Pathway | Through neurons | Through blood |
| Effect | Usually short-lived | Usually long-lasting |
| Target | Specific connected cells | Target organs with receptors |
| Example | Reflex action | Adrenaline response |

## 49. Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain the structure and function of a neuron.

A neuron has dendrites, cell body, axon, and nerve endings. Dendrites receive information and create electrical impulses. The impulse passes through the cell body and axon to the nerve ending. At the nerve ending, chemicals are released. These chemicals cross the synapse and transfer the message to the next neuron, muscle, or gland.

### 2. Explain reflex action with an example.

Reflex action is a sudden automatic response to a stimulus. When we touch a hot object, heat and pain receptors in the skin detect the stimulus. Sensory neurons carry the impulse to the spinal cord. The spinal cord sends a command through motor neurons to the arm muscles. The muscles contract and the hand is pulled back. This pathway is called reflex arc.

### 3. Explain the main parts of the human brain and their functions.

The brain has three main regions: forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain. The forebrain controls thinking, memory, learning, sensory interpretation, and voluntary actions. The midbrain controls some involuntary responses related to sight and sound. The hindbrain includes cerebellum, pons, and medulla. Cerebellum controls balance and posture. Pons helps in breathing regulation. Medulla controls involuntary actions such as heartbeat, blood pressure, salivation, and vomiting.

### 4. Explain how nervous tissue causes muscle movement.

When a nerve impulse reaches a muscle cell, chemicals are released at the nerve-muscle junction. These chemicals cause special proteins in the muscle cell to change their arrangement. Due to this change, the muscle cell changes shape and contracts. This produces movement.

### 5. Explain coordination in plants.

Plants do not have nervous tissue or muscles. They use chemical signals and hormones for coordination. Some plant movements are quick and not related to growth, such as folding of touch-me-not leaves due to changes in water amount inside cells. Other plant movements are slow and growth-dependent, such as shoots growing towards light and roots growing towards gravity. Plant hormones like auxin, gibberellin, cytokinin, and abscisic acid coordinate growth and responses.

### 6. Explain phototropism.

Phototropism is the growth movement of plant parts in response to light. In shoots, auxin is produced at the tip. When light comes from one side, auxin moves to the shaded side. Cells on the shaded side elongate more than cells on the lighted side. As a result, the shoot bends towards light.

### 7. Explain different plant hormones and their functions.

Auxin promotes cell elongation and helps in phototropism. Gibberellin promotes stem growth. Cytokinin promotes cell division and is found in regions of active cell division. Abscisic acid inhibits growth and causes wilting of leaves.

### 8. Explain chemical coordination in animals.

Animals use hormones for chemical coordination. Hormones are secreted by endocrine glands directly into blood. Blood carries hormones to target organs. Hormones control processes such as growth, metabolism, emergency response, puberty, and blood sugar level. Examples include adrenaline, thyroxine, growth hormone, insulin, testosterone, and estrogen.

### 9. Explain the role of adrenaline.

Adrenaline is secreted by adrenal glands during fear, stress, anger, or danger. It increases heartbeat and breathing rate, sends more blood to skeletal muscles, reduces blood supply to digestive system and skin, and prepares the body for fight or flight.

### 10. Explain why iodised salt is advised.

Iodised salt is advised because iodine is required by the thyroid gland to make thyroxine. Thyroxine controls the metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Deficiency of iodine may cause goitre, which leads to swelling in the neck.

### 11. Explain how insulin controls blood sugar.

Insulin is secreted by the pancreas. When blood sugar level rises, pancreas secretes more insulin. Insulin helps reduce blood sugar level. When blood sugar becomes normal or low, insulin secretion decreases. This is an example of feedback mechanism.

### 12. Compare nervous coordination and hormonal coordination.

Nervous coordination is fast and uses electrical impulses through neurons. Its effect is usually short-lived and specific. Hormonal coordination is slower and uses chemical hormones transported by blood. Its effect is usually longer-lasting and may affect several target tissues.
