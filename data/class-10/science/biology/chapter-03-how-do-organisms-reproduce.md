# Chapter 3: How Do Organisms Reproduce?

## Chapter Overview

Reproduction is the biological process by which living organisms produce new individuals of their own kind.

Reproduction is not essential for the survival of an individual organism, but it is essential for the continuation of a species.

Main topics covered in this chapter:

- Need for reproduction
- DNA copying and variation
- Importance of variation
- Asexual reproduction
- Fission
- Fragmentation
- Regeneration
- Budding
- Vegetative propagation
- Spore formation
- Tissue culture
- Sexual reproduction
- Sexual reproduction in flowering plants
- Pollination and fertilisation
- Seed formation and germination
- Sexual reproduction in humans
- Puberty
- Male reproductive system
- Female reproductive system
- Menstruation
- Reproductive health
- Sexually transmitted diseases
- Contraception

---

## 1. Why Do Organisms Reproduce?

Reproduction produces new individuals similar to the parent organism.

An individual organism does not need reproduction to stay alive. Processes such as nutrition, respiration, transport, and excretion are required for individual survival.

Still, reproduction is important because:

- It maintains the continuity of a species.
- It increases the number of individuals in a species.
- It transfers hereditary information from parents to offspring.
- It produces variations that help species survive environmental changes.

If organisms did not reproduce, their species would disappear after the death of existing individuals.

---

## 2. Do Organisms Create Exact Copies of Themselves?

Organisms usually produce offspring that look similar to themselves.

This similarity is because the body design of offspring is based on hereditary information inherited from the parent.

This hereditary information is stored in DNA.

DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid.

DNA is present in chromosomes inside the nucleus of a cell.

DNA contains information for making proteins. Proteins control body structure, function, and development.

Therefore:

```txt
DNA information
↓
Protein formation
↓
Cell structure and function
↓
Body design
```

During reproduction, DNA is copied and passed to the next generation.

---

## 3. DNA Copying in Reproduction

The basic event in reproduction is DNA copying.

During reproduction:

1. DNA makes a copy of itself.
2. Two copies of DNA are formed.
3. Other cellular structures are also copied.
4. The cell divides.
5. Each new cell receives one DNA copy.

Simple flow:

```txt
Parent cell
↓
DNA replication
↓
Two DNA copies
↓
Cell division
↓
Two daughter cells
```

The daughter cells are similar to the parent cell, but they are not always exactly identical.

DNA copying is a biochemical process. No biochemical process is perfectly accurate. Therefore, small changes may occur during DNA copying.

These small changes produce variations.

---

## 4. Variation

Variation means differences among individuals of the same species.

Examples:

- Two human beings are not exactly identical.
- Plants of the same species may differ in height, size, and colour.
- Bacteria in the same population may show small differences.

Variations arise mainly because DNA copying is not always perfectly accurate.

Some variations may be harmful and may kill the new cell or organism.

Some variations may be neutral.

Some variations may be useful for survival.

---

## 5. Importance of Variation

Variation is important for the survival of species.

A population lives in a particular environment or niche. If the environment changes suddenly, all individuals may not survive.

But if some individuals have useful variations, they may survive and reproduce.

Example:

- A population of bacteria lives in cool water.
- Due to global warming, water temperature increases.
- Most bacteria may die.
- Some bacteria with heat-resistant variation may survive.
- These surviving bacteria reproduce and continue the population.

Therefore:

> Variation helps species survive environmental changes.

Variation is more important for the survival of a species than for the survival of an individual.

---

## 6. Types of Reproduction

There are two main types of reproduction:

1. Asexual reproduction
2. Sexual reproduction

### 6.1 Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction is the type of reproduction in which only one parent is involved.

Features:

- Only one organism participates.
- Gametes are not formed.
- Fertilisation does not occur.
- Offspring are usually genetically similar to the parent.
- It is common in unicellular organisms and some simple multicellular organisms.

Main methods of asexual reproduction:

- Fission
- Fragmentation
- Regeneration
- Budding
- Vegetative propagation
- Spore formation

### 6.2 Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction is the type of reproduction in which two parents usually participate and male and female gametes fuse.

Features:

- Usually two parents participate.
- Gametes are formed.
- Fertilisation occurs.
- Offspring show more variation.
- It is common in higher plants and animals.

---

## 7. Asexual Reproduction in Unicellular Organisms

Unicellular organisms reproduce mainly by cell division.

In many unicellular organisms, one cell divides to form two or more daughter cells.

---

## 8. Fission

Fission is a method of asexual reproduction in which a unicellular organism divides into two or more new individuals.

There are two main types:

1. Binary fission
2. Multiple fission

---

## 9. Binary Fission

Binary fission is a method of asexual reproduction in which one organism divides into two daughter organisms.

Examples:

- Amoeba
- Bacteria
- Leishmania

General steps:

```txt
Parent cell
↓
Nucleus divides
↓
Cytoplasm divides
↓
Two daughter cells form
```

### 9.1 Binary Fission in Amoeba

In Amoeba, binary fission can occur in any plane because Amoeba has an irregular shape.

Steps:

1. Amoeba grows to a suitable size.
2. Its nucleus divides into two nuclei.
3. Cytoplasm divides.
4. Two daughter Amoebae are formed.

### 9.2 Binary Fission in Leishmania

Leishmania has a definite body organisation and a whip-like structure at one end.

Therefore, binary fission in Leishmania occurs in a definite plane.

Important point:

> In organisms with a definite body structure, fission usually occurs in a definite orientation.

---

## 10. Multiple Fission

Multiple fission is a method of asexual reproduction in which one organism divides into many daughter organisms at the same time.

Example:

- Plasmodium, the malaria parasite

Steps:

1. Nucleus divides repeatedly.
2. Many daughter nuclei are formed.
3. Cytoplasm divides around each nucleus.
4. Many daughter cells are released.

Important point:

> Plasmodium reproduces by multiple fission.

---

## 11. Budding in Yeast

Yeast reproduces by budding.

In budding, a small outgrowth called a bud develops on the parent body.

Steps:

1. A small bud appears on the yeast cell.
2. The nucleus divides.
3. One nucleus moves into the bud.
4. The bud grows.
5. The bud separates from the parent and becomes a new yeast cell.

Important point:

> Yeast reproduces asexually by budding.

---

## 12. Fragmentation

Fragmentation is a method of asexual reproduction in which the body of a simple multicellular organism breaks into fragments, and each fragment develops into a new organism.

Example:

- Spirogyra

Spirogyra is a filamentous green alga.

In suitable conditions, mature Spirogyra breaks into small pieces. Each piece grows into a new Spirogyra.

Simple flow:

```txt
Spirogyra filament
↓
Breaks into fragments
↓
Each fragment grows
↓
New Spirogyra organisms
```

Fragmentation is possible in simple multicellular organisms because their body organisation is not highly complex.

---

## 13. Why Fragmentation Does Not Work in Complex Organisms

Complex multicellular organisms are not just random groups of cells.

They have:

- Specialised cells
- Tissues
- Organs
- Organ systems
- Fixed body organisation

In such organisms, each body part has a specific function and position.

Therefore, a piece of a complex organism cannot usually grow into a complete organism.

Example:

- A piece of human skin cannot grow into a complete human being.
- A cut part of a dog cannot grow into a new dog.

Complex organisms need more specialised methods of reproduction.

---

## 14. Regeneration

Regeneration is the ability of an organism to regrow lost body parts.

In some simple organisms, regeneration can produce a complete new organism from body pieces.

Examples:

- Hydra
- Planaria

If Planaria is cut into many pieces, each piece can grow into a complete Planaria.

Steps in regeneration:

1. The body is cut or broken into pieces.
2. Specialised cells at the cut region divide rapidly.
3. A mass of cells is formed.
4. These cells develop into different tissues and organs.
5. A complete organism is formed.

Important point:

> Regeneration is carried out by specialised cells that can divide and differentiate.

Regeneration is not the same as reproduction in all cases.

Reason:

- Every body part of every organism cannot produce a new organism.
- Regeneration is usually seen in simple organisms.

---

## 15. Budding in Hydra

Hydra reproduces by budding.

Steps:

1. Repeated cell division occurs at one specific point on the Hydra body.
2. A small outgrowth called a bud develops.
3. The bud grows and develops tentacles.
4. The bud becomes a small Hydra.
5. It separates from the parent body.
6. It lives as an independent organism.

Simple flow:

```txt
Parent Hydra
↓
Bud formation
↓
Bud grows
↓
Young Hydra
↓
Separation
↓
Independent Hydra
```

---

## 16. Vegetative Propagation

Vegetative propagation is a method of asexual reproduction in plants in which new plants develop from vegetative parts such as root, stem, or leaf.

Vegetative parts include:

- Root
- Stem
- Leaf
- Bud

Examples:

- Sugarcane
- Rose
- Grapes
- Banana
- Orange
- Jasmine
- Potato
- Bryophyllum

---

## 17. Vegetative Propagation in Potato

Potato has small depressions called eyes.

These eyes contain buds.

When pieces of potato containing eyes are placed in suitable conditions, they develop into new plants.

Important point:

> Potato pieces without eyes do not usually produce new plants.

---

## 18. Vegetative Propagation in Bryophyllum

Bryophyllum leaves have buds on their margins.

When these buds fall on moist soil, they can develop into new plants.

Important point:

> Bryophyllum reproduces vegetatively through leaf buds.

---

## 19. Advantages of Vegetative Propagation

Vegetative propagation is useful because:

- It produces plants genetically similar to the parent plant.
- It helps grow plants that do not produce viable seeds.
- It helps grow plants faster than seed propagation.
- Flowering and fruiting occur earlier.
- Desirable traits are preserved.
- It is useful in agriculture and horticulture.

Examples of plants grown by vegetative propagation:

- Sugarcane
- Rose
- Grapes
- Banana
- Orange
- Jasmine

---

## 20. Artificial Methods of Vegetative Propagation

Common artificial methods are:

- Cutting
- Layering
- Grafting
- Tissue culture

### 20.1 Cutting

A part of stem, root, or leaf is cut and planted in soil. It grows into a new plant.

Example:

- Rose
- Sugarcane

### 20.2 Layering

A branch is bent and covered with soil. Roots develop from the covered part. Later, it is separated and grown as a new plant.

### 20.3 Grafting

Parts of two plants are joined so that they grow as one plant.

### 20.4 Tissue Culture

Tissue culture is a technique in which new plants are grown from plant cells or tissues in an artificial nutrient medium.

Steps:

1. Cells or tissues are taken from the growing tip of a plant.
2. They are placed in a sterile nutrient medium.
3. Cells divide and form a small mass called callus.
4. Callus is transferred to a medium containing growth and differentiation hormones.
5. Small plantlets develop.
6. Plantlets are transferred to soil.

Advantages of tissue culture:

- Many plants can be produced from a single plant.
- Disease-free plants can be produced.
- Useful for ornamental plants.
- Useful for plants that are difficult to grow by seeds.

---

## 21. Spore Formation

Spore formation is a method of asexual reproduction in which spores are produced inside sporangia.

Example:

- Rhizopus, the bread mould

Rhizopus grows as thread-like structures called hyphae.

Some upright hyphae develop sporangia at their tips.

Sporangia contain spores.

When sporangia burst, spores are released. Under suitable conditions, spores germinate and grow into new Rhizopus.

Important features of spores:

- They are small and light.
- They can spread through air.
- They have thick protective walls.
- They can survive unfavourable conditions.
- They germinate under favourable conditions.

Simple flow:

```txt
Rhizopus
↓
Sporangia formation
↓
Spore production
↓
Spore dispersal
↓
Spore germination
↓
New Rhizopus
```

## 23. Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of male and female gametes.

The fusion of gametes is called fertilisation.

The cell formed after fertilisation is called zygote.

Simple flow:

```txt
Male gamete + Female gamete
↓
Fertilisation
↓
Zygote
↓
Embryo
↓
New organism
```

Sexual reproduction produces greater variation than asexual reproduction.

---

## 24. Why Sexual Reproduction Is Important

Sexual reproduction is important because it creates new combinations of variations.

In asexual reproduction, variation occurs mainly due to small errors in DNA copying.

In sexual reproduction, DNA from two different individuals combines. This produces more variation.

Advantages:

- Produces genetic variation.
- Helps species survive environmental changes.
- Combines useful traits from two parents.
- Supports evolution.

---

## 25. Gametes

Gametes are specialised reproductive cells.

There are two types:

- Male gamete
- Female gamete

The chromosome number in gametes is half of the chromosome number in normal body cells.

This is necessary because during fertilisation, male and female gametes fuse and restore the normal chromosome number.

If chromosome number were not halved in gametes, it would double in every generation.

---

## 26. Male and Female Gametes

In simple organisms, male and female gametes may look similar.

In complex organisms, gametes are specialised.

### 26.1 Male Gamete

The male gamete is usually:

- Smaller
- Motile
- Designed to travel towards the female gamete

Example:

- Sperm in animals
- Male gamete inside pollen grain in flowering plants

### 26.2 Female Gamete

The female gamete is usually:

- Larger
- Non-motile
- Contains stored food

Example:

- Egg cell in animals
- Egg cell in ovule of flowering plants

---

## 27. Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants

The reproductive organ of flowering plants is the flower.

Main parts of a flower:

- Sepal
- Petal
- Stamen
- Carpel / Pistil

Stamen and carpel are the reproductive parts.

---

## 28. Parts of a Flower

### 28.1 Sepals

Sepals are the outer green parts of a flower.

They protect the flower in the bud stage.

### 28.2 Petals

Petals are usually colourful.

They help attract insects and other pollinators.

### 28.3 Stamen

Stamen is the male reproductive part of a flower.

It has two parts:

- Anther
- Filament

The anther produces pollen grains.

Pollen grains contain male gametes.

### 28.4 Carpel / Pistil

Carpel is the female reproductive part of a flower.

It has three parts:

- Stigma
- Style
- Ovary

The ovary contains ovules.

Each ovule contains an egg cell.

---

## 29. Unisexual and Bisexual Flowers

### 29.1 Unisexual Flower

A flower that contains either stamen or carpel is called a unisexual flower.

Examples:

- Papaya
- Watermelon

### 29.2 Bisexual Flower

A flower that contains both stamen and carpel is called a bisexual flower.

Examples:

- Hibiscus
- Mustard

---

## 30. Pollination

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma.

There are two types:

1. Self-pollination
2. Cross-pollination

### 30.1 Self-Pollination

Self-pollination occurs when pollen grains are transferred from the anther to the stigma of the same flower or another flower of the same plant.

### 30.2 Cross-Pollination

Cross-pollination occurs when pollen grains are transferred from the anther of one flower to the stigma of another flower of the same species on a different plant.

Agents of pollination:

- Wind
- Water
- Insects
- Animals

Important point:

> Pollination is only the transfer of pollen grains. It is not the same as fertilisation.

---

## 31. Fertilisation in Flowering Plants

After pollen grains reach the stigma, the male gamete must reach the female gamete inside the ovule.

Steps:

1. Pollen grain lands on a suitable stigma.
2. A pollen tube grows from the pollen grain.
3. The pollen tube passes through the style.
4. It reaches the ovule inside the ovary.
5. The male gamete travels through the pollen tube.
6. The male gamete fuses with the egg cell.
7. A zygote is formed.

This fusion of male and female gametes is called fertilisation.

Simple flow:

```txt
Pollen grain on stigma
↓
Pollen tube formation
↓
Male gamete reaches ovule
↓
Male gamete + Egg cell
↓
Fertilisation
↓
Zygote
```

---

## 32. Formation of Seed and Fruit

After fertilisation:

- The zygote divides many times.
- The embryo develops inside the ovule.
- The ovule becomes the seed.
- The ovary grows and becomes the fruit.
- Sepals, petals, stamens, style, and stigma usually dry and fall off.

Important transformations:

| Before Fertilisation | After Fertilisation |
|---|---|
| Ovule | Seed |
| Ovary | Fruit |
| Zygote | Embryo |

---

## 33. Seed Germination

A seed contains an embryo or future plant.

Under suitable conditions, the embryo grows into a seedling.

This process is called germination.

Conditions required for germination:

- Water
- Air / oxygen
- Suitable temperature

Main parts of a germinating seed:

- Plumule: future shoot
- Radicle: future root
- Cotyledons: stored food

---

## 34. Sexual Reproduction in Humans

Humans reproduce by sexual reproduction.

Male and female reproductive systems produce gametes.

- Male gamete: sperm
- Female gamete: egg / ovum

Fertilisation occurs when sperm fuses with egg.

The zygote develops into an embryo and later into a baby.

---

## 35. Puberty

Puberty is the stage at which the reproductive system becomes functional and the body becomes sexually mature.

During puberty, the body shows physical, emotional, and reproductive changes.

Puberty does not occur at exactly the same age in all individuals.

---

## 36. Common Changes During Puberty

Common changes in boys and girls:

- Increase in height and weight
- Growth of hair in armpits and genital region
- Growth of fine hair on arms, legs, and face
- Skin may become oily
- Pimples may appear
- Increased awareness of self and others
- Emotional changes

---

## 37. Changes in Girls During Puberty

Changes in girls include:

- Breast development
- Darkening of skin around nipples
- Beginning of menstruation
- Maturation of ovaries
- Production of ova
- Hormonal changes

---

## 38. Changes in Boys During Puberty

Changes in boys include:

- Growth of beard and moustache
- Voice becomes deeper or cracks
- Enlargement and maturation of testes and penis
- Production of sperms begins
- Increased secretion of testosterone

---

## 39. Male Reproductive System

The human male reproductive system includes:

- Testes
- Scrotum
- Vas deferens / sperm duct
- Seminal vesicles
- Prostate gland
- Urethra
- Penis

---

## 40. Testes

Testes are the male reproductive organs that produce sperms.

They also produce the hormone testosterone.

Testes are located outside the abdominal cavity in the scrotum.

Reason:

> Sperm formation requires a temperature lower than normal body temperature.

Functions of testes:

- Produce sperms
- Produce testosterone
- Control male puberty changes

---

## 41. Sperm

Sperm is the male gamete.

Features:

- It is microscopic.
- It contains genetic material.
- It has a long tail.
- The tail helps it swim towards the female gamete.

---

## 42. Vas Deferens / Sperm Duct

The vas deferens carries sperms from testes towards the urethra.

It joins with the tube coming from the urinary bladder.

---

## 43. Seminal Vesicles and Prostate Gland

Seminal vesicles and prostate gland add fluid secretions to sperms.

These secretions:

- Provide a fluid medium for sperm transport
- Provide nourishment to sperms
- Help sperms move easily

The mixture of sperms and gland secretions is called semen.

---

## 44. Urethra and Penis

The urethra is a common passage for urine and semen.

The penis transfers semen into the female reproductive tract during sexual reproduction.

---

## 45. Female Reproductive System

The human female reproductive system includes:

- Ovaries
- Oviducts / Fallopian tubes
- Uterus
- Cervix
- Vagina

---

## 46. Ovaries

Ovaries are the female reproductive organs that produce ova or egg cells.

They also produce female hormones.

At birth, a girl has thousands of immature eggs in the ovaries.

At puberty, some eggs start maturing.

Usually, one egg is released every month by one of the ovaries.

---

## 47. Oviduct / Fallopian Tube

The oviduct carries the egg from the ovary to the uterus.

Fertilisation usually occurs in the oviduct.

---

## 48. Uterus

The uterus is a muscular, elastic organ where the embryo develops.

It prepares every month to receive a fertilised egg.

The inner lining of the uterus becomes thick and rich in blood supply to nourish the embryo.

---

## 49. Cervix and Vagina

The cervix is the narrow opening of the uterus.

The vagina receives sperms during sexual reproduction and also acts as the birth canal.

---

## 50. Fertilisation in Humans

During sexual reproduction, sperms are deposited in the vagina.

Sperms travel upward through the female reproductive tract and may reach the oviduct.

If an egg is present in the oviduct, one sperm may fuse with it.

This fusion is fertilisation.

The fertilised egg is called zygote.

Simple flow:

```txt
Sperm deposited in vagina
↓
Sperm travels to oviduct
↓
Sperm fuses with egg
↓
Zygote forms
↓
Zygote moves to uterus
↓
Embryo develops
```

---

## 51. Pregnancy and Embryo Development

After fertilisation:

1. The zygote divides repeatedly.
2. It forms an embryo.
3. The embryo gets implanted in the uterus wall.
4. The uterus provides protection and nourishment.
5. The embryo grows into a foetus.
6. After about nine months, the baby is born.

The birth occurs due to rhythmic contractions of uterine muscles.

---

## 52. Placenta

The placenta is a special tissue that connects the developing embryo with the mother's blood supply.

It is embedded in the wall of the uterus.

Functions of placenta:

- Transfers glucose from mother to embryo.
- Transfers oxygen from mother to embryo.
- Transfers other nutrients from mother to embryo.
- Removes wastes from embryo to mother's blood.
- Provides a large surface area for exchange of materials.

Important point:

> The embryo receives nutrition and oxygen from the mother through the placenta.

---

## 53. Menstruation

If the egg is not fertilised, it survives for about one day.

Since the ovary releases an egg every month, the uterus also prepares every month to receive a fertilised egg.

The uterus lining becomes thick and spongy with rich blood supply.

If fertilisation does not occur, this lining is not needed.

The lining slowly breaks down and comes out through the vagina as blood and mucus.

This process is called menstruation.

The menstrual cycle takes about one month.

Menstrual bleeding usually lasts about 2 to 8 days.

---

## 54. Reproductive Health

Reproductive health means physical, mental, and social well-being related to the reproductive system.

Sexual maturity does not mean that the body and mind are ready for pregnancy or parenthood.

Reproductive health requires:

- Correct knowledge
- Responsible behaviour
- Hygiene
- Prevention of sexually transmitted diseases
- Prevention of unwanted pregnancy
- Respect for law and health

---

## 55. Sexually Transmitted Diseases

Some diseases can spread through sexual contact.

These are called sexually transmitted diseases or STDs.

Examples:

- Gonorrhoea
- Syphilis
- Warts
- HIV-AIDS

STDs may be caused by bacteria or viruses.

A condom can reduce the risk of many sexually transmitted diseases because it acts as a barrier during sexual contact.

---

## 56. Contraception

Contraception means methods used to prevent pregnancy.

Main types of contraceptive methods:

- Barrier methods
- Hormonal methods
- Intrauterine devices
- Surgical methods

---

## 57. Barrier Methods

Barrier methods prevent sperms from reaching the egg.

Examples:

- Condom used on penis
- Devices placed in vagina

Condoms also help reduce the spread of many sexually transmitted diseases.

---

## 58. Hormonal Methods

Hormonal methods change the hormone balance in the female body.

They prevent the release of egg from the ovary.

They are usually taken as pills.

Possible issue:

- Since they change hormone balance, they may have side effects.

---

## 59. Intrauterine Devices

Intrauterine devices are placed in the uterus to prevent pregnancy.

Example:

- Loop
- Copper-T

Possible issue:

- They may cause side effects due to irritation of the uterus.

---

## 60. Surgical Methods

Surgical methods block the transport of gametes.

In males:

- Vas deferens is blocked.
- Sperms cannot be transferred.

In females:

- Oviducts / Fallopian tubes are blocked.
- Egg cannot reach the uterus.
- Fertilisation does not occur.

Important point:

> Surgical methods prevent fertilisation by blocking the movement of gametes.

---

## 61. Sex Ratio and Legal Protection

The sex ratio means the number of females compared to males in a population.

Misuse of medical technology for sex-selective abortion is harmful to society.

Foetal sex determination for sex selection is illegal.

A healthy society needs a balanced male-female sex ratio.

## Short Answer Questions

### 1. Why is reproduction important?

Reproduction is important because it produces new individuals and maintains the continuity of a species.

### 2. Is reproduction necessary for the survival of an individual organism?

No. Reproduction is not necessary for individual survival, but it is necessary for the survival of the species.

### 3. What is the importance of DNA copying in reproduction?

DNA copying transfers hereditary information from parent to offspring and helps maintain body design across generations.

### 4. Why are offspring not exactly identical to parents?

DNA copying is not perfectly accurate. Small changes occur during copying, producing variations.

### 5. Why is variation useful for a species?

Variation helps some individuals survive when environmental conditions change.

### 6. What is binary fission?

Binary fission is asexual reproduction in which one organism divides into two daughter organisms.

### 7. How is binary fission different from multiple fission?

In binary fission, one organism divides into two daughter organisms. In multiple fission, one organism divides into many daughter organisms at the same time.

### 8. What is budding?

Budding is asexual reproduction in which a small bud grows on the parent body and later separates to form a new organism.

### 9. What is fragmentation?

Fragmentation is asexual reproduction in which the body of an organism breaks into pieces, and each piece grows into a new organism.

### 10. Why can complex organisms not reproduce by simple fragmentation?

Complex organisms have specialised tissues, organs, and organ systems. Their body parts cannot usually grow into complete organisms.

### 11. What is regeneration?

Regeneration is the ability to regrow lost body parts. In some simple organisms, body pieces can regenerate into complete organisms.

### 12. What is vegetative propagation?

Vegetative propagation is asexual reproduction in plants where new plants develop from roots, stems, leaves, or buds.

### 13. Why is vegetative propagation useful?

It produces plants faster, preserves desirable traits, helps grow seedless plants, and produces genetically similar plants.

### 14. What is spore formation?

Spore formation is asexual reproduction in which spores are produced inside sporangia and germinate into new organisms under favourable conditions.

### 15. Why are spores able to survive unfavourable conditions?

Spores have thick protective walls that protect them from unfavourable conditions.

### 16. What is pollination?

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma.

### 17. How is pollination different from fertilisation?

Pollination is transfer of pollen grains. Fertilisation is fusion of male and female gametes.

### 18. What happens after fertilisation in a flower?

The zygote develops into an embryo, the ovule becomes seed, and the ovary becomes fruit.

### 19. What is puberty?

Puberty is the stage when reproductive organs mature and the body becomes capable of sexual reproduction.

### 20. Why are testes located outside the abdominal cavity?

Testes are located in the scrotum outside the abdominal cavity because sperm formation requires a temperature lower than body temperature.

### 21. What is the role of seminal vesicles and prostate gland?

They add fluid secretions to sperms, providing nourishment and helping sperm transport.

### 22. Where does fertilisation occur in humans?

Fertilisation usually occurs in the oviduct or Fallopian tube.

### 23. What is the function of placenta?

The placenta transfers nutrients and oxygen from mother to embryo and removes wastes from embryo to mother's blood.

### 24. Why does menstruation occur?

Menstruation occurs when the egg is not fertilised. The thick lining of the uterus breaks down and comes out through the vagina.

### 25. Does Copper-T protect from sexually transmitted diseases?

No. Copper-T helps prevent pregnancy but does not protect from sexually transmitted diseases.

## Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain the importance of DNA copying and variation in reproduction.

DNA copying is the basic event in reproduction. DNA contains hereditary information for body design and protein formation. During reproduction, DNA makes a copy of itself and passes this information to the next generation. However, DNA copying is not perfectly accurate, so small variations are produced. Some variations may be harmful, but some may help organisms survive environmental changes. Therefore, variation is important for the survival and evolution of species.

### 2. Explain different methods of asexual reproduction.

Asexual reproduction involves only one parent. In binary fission, one organism divides into two, as in Amoeba. In multiple fission, one organism divides into many, as in Plasmodium. In budding, a bud grows on the parent body and separates, as in yeast and Hydra. In fragmentation, the body breaks into pieces and each piece grows into a new organism, as in Spirogyra. In regeneration, body pieces can grow into complete organisms, as in Planaria. In vegetative propagation, new plants grow from root, stem, leaf, or bud. In spore formation, spores are produced inside sporangia and germinate into new organisms.

### 3. Explain vegetative propagation and its advantages.

Vegetative propagation is a method of asexual reproduction in plants where new plants develop from vegetative parts such as roots, stems, leaves, or buds. Potato reproduces through eyes, and Bryophyllum reproduces through leaf buds. Artificial methods include cutting, layering, grafting, and tissue culture. It is useful because it produces plants faster, preserves desirable characters, helps grow plants that do not produce seeds, and produces genetically identical plants.

### 4. Explain sexual reproduction in flowering plants.

The flower is the reproductive organ of flowering plants. Stamen is the male reproductive part and produces pollen grains. Carpel is the female reproductive part and contains stigma, style, and ovary. The ovary contains ovules, and each ovule has an egg cell. Pollination transfers pollen grains from anther to stigma. After pollination, a pollen tube grows through the style and reaches the ovule. The male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form a zygote. This is fertilisation. The zygote develops into an embryo, the ovule becomes seed, and the ovary becomes fruit.

### 5. Explain the male reproductive system in humans.

The male reproductive system includes testes, scrotum, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate gland, urethra, and penis. Testes produce sperms and testosterone. They are located in the scrotum because sperm production needs a temperature lower than body temperature. Sperms pass through vas deferens. Seminal vesicles and prostate gland add fluid secretions that nourish sperms and help their transport. The urethra acts as a common passage for urine and semen. The penis transfers semen into the female reproductive tract.

### 6. Explain the female reproductive system in humans.

The female reproductive system includes ovaries, oviducts, uterus, cervix, and vagina. Ovaries produce ova and female hormones. Usually, one ovum is released every month. The ovum is carried by the oviduct to the uterus. Fertilisation usually occurs in the oviduct. The uterus is a muscular organ where the embryo develops. The cervix connects the uterus to the vagina. The vagina receives sperms and also acts as the birth canal.

### 7. Explain fertilisation, pregnancy, and placenta in humans.

During sexual reproduction, sperms are deposited in the vagina and travel to the oviduct. If an ovum is present, one sperm fuses with it to form a zygote. The zygote divides repeatedly and forms an embryo. The embryo gets implanted in the uterus wall. The placenta develops between the embryo and the uterus. It transfers nutrients and oxygen from the mother to the embryo and removes wastes from the embryo to the mother's blood. The baby develops for about nine months before birth.

### 8. Explain menstruation.

Every month, one ovum is released by an ovary, and the uterus prepares itself to receive a fertilised egg. The inner lining of the uterus becomes thick, spongy, and rich in blood supply. If fertilisation does not occur, this lining is not needed. It breaks down and comes out through the vagina as blood and mucus. This process is called menstruation. It usually lasts about 2 to 8 days and occurs in a cycle of about one month.

### 9. Explain different methods of contraception.

Contraception means preventing pregnancy. Barrier methods such as condoms prevent sperms from reaching the egg and also reduce the risk of many sexually transmitted diseases. Hormonal pills change the hormone balance and prevent egg release. Intrauterine devices such as loop or Copper-T are placed in the uterus to prevent pregnancy. Surgical methods block the vas deferens in males or oviducts in females, preventing fertilisation.

### 10. Why is reproductive health important?

Reproductive health is important because sexual maturity does not always mean physical and mental readiness for reproduction or parenthood. Reproductive health includes correct knowledge, hygiene, prevention of sexually transmitted diseases, prevention of unwanted pregnancy, and responsible decisions. It also includes awareness that foetal sex determination for sex selection is illegal and harmful to society.
