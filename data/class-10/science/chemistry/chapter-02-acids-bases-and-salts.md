# Chapter 2: Acids, Bases and Salts

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains acids, bases and salts through their properties, chemical reactions, pH, neutralisation, and daily-life uses.

Acids usually taste sour and turn blue litmus red. Bases usually taste bitter, feel soapy, and turn red litmus blue. Acids and bases can neutralise each other to form salt and water.

Important ideas in this chapter:

- Acid-base indicators
- Chemical properties of acids and bases
- Reaction of acids with metals, metal carbonates and metal hydrogen carbonates
- Neutralisation reaction
- Common similarity among all acids and bases
- Role of water in showing acidic/basic behaviour
- pH scale and its daily-life importance
- Salts and their pH
- Chemicals obtained from common salt
- Bleaching powder, baking soda, washing soda, water of crystallisation and plaster of Paris

---

## 1. Acids and Bases

### 1.1 Acids

An acid is a substance that produces hydrogen ions `H+` or hydronium ions `H3O+` in aqueous solution.

Common properties of acids:

- Sour in taste
- Turn blue litmus red
- Conduct electricity in aqueous solution
- React with metals to produce hydrogen gas
- React with metal carbonates and metal hydrogen carbonates to produce carbon dioxide
- React with bases to form salt and water

Examples:

| Acid | Formula |
|---|---|
| Hydrochloric acid | `HCl` |
| Sulphuric acid | `H2SO4` |
| Nitric acid | `HNO3` |
| Acetic acid | `CH3COOH` |
| Citric acid | Found in lemon and orange |
| Tartaric acid | Found in tamarind |
| Lactic acid | Found in curd |

### 1.2 Bases

A base is a substance that produces hydroxide ions `OH-` in aqueous solution.

Common properties of bases:

- Bitter in taste
- Feel soapy
- Turn red litmus blue
- Conduct electricity in aqueous solution
- React with acids to form salt and water
- Some bases react with metals to produce hydrogen gas

Examples:

| Base | Formula |
|---|---|
| Sodium hydroxide | `NaOH` |
| Potassium hydroxide | `KOH` |
| Calcium hydroxide | `Ca(OH)2` |
| Magnesium hydroxide | `Mg(OH)2` |
| Ammonium hydroxide | `NH4OH` |

### 1.3 Alkalis

Bases that dissolve in water are called **alkalis**.

Examples:

```txt
NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)2, NH4OH
```

Important point:

- All alkalis are bases.
- All bases are not alkalis because all bases are not soluble in water.

## 2. Acid-Base Indicators

An indicator is a substance that shows whether a solution is acidic or basic by changing colour or smell.

### 2.1 Natural Indicators

| Indicator | In Acid | In Base |
|---|---|---|
| Litmus | Blue litmus turns red | Red litmus turns blue |
| Turmeric | Yellow | Reddish brown |
| Red cabbage extract | Colour changes depending on acidic/basic nature | Colour changes depending on acidic/basic nature |

Litmus is a natural indicator obtained from lichens. Neutral litmus solution is purple.

### 2.2 Synthetic Indicators

| Indicator | In Acid | In Base |
|---|---|---|
| Methyl orange | Red | Yellow |
| Phenolphthalein | Colourless | Pink |

### 2.3 Olfactory Indicators

Olfactory indicators are substances whose smell changes in acidic or basic medium.

Examples:

- Onion
- Vanilla essence
- Clove oil

In basic medium, the smell of some olfactory indicators may disappear or change.

## 3. Chemical Properties of Acids and Bases

## 3.1 Reaction of Acids with Metals

Acids react with many metals to form salt and hydrogen gas.

General reaction:

```txt
Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas
```

Example with zinc and dilute sulphuric acid:

```txt
Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g)
```

Example with zinc and dilute hydrochloric acid:

```txt
Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)
```

### Test for Hydrogen Gas

Hydrogen gas burns with a **pop sound** when a burning candle or matchstick is brought near it.

### Activity-Based Observation

When granulated zinc is added to dilute acid:

- Bubbles are produced.
- The gas produced is hydrogen.
- Soap bubbles filled with hydrogen burn with a pop sound.

---

## 3.2 Reaction of Bases with Metals

Some bases react with metals to form salt and hydrogen gas.

Example:

```txt
2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na2ZnO2(aq) + H2(g)
```

Here, sodium zincate and hydrogen gas are formed.

Important point:

Not all metals react with bases.

---

## 3.3 Reaction of Acids with Metal Carbonates and Metal Hydrogen Carbonates

Acids react with metal carbonates and metal hydrogen carbonates to form salt, carbon dioxide and water.

General reaction with metal carbonate:

```txt
Metal carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
```

General reaction with metal hydrogen carbonate:

```txt
Metal hydrogen carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
```

### Sodium Carbonate with Hydrochloric Acid

```txt
Na2CO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)
```

### Sodium Hydrogen Carbonate with Hydrochloric Acid

```txt
NaHCO3(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)
```

### Test for Carbon Dioxide Gas

Carbon dioxide turns lime water milky.

```txt
Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)
```

The milky appearance is due to the formation of calcium carbonate precipitate.

If excess carbon dioxide is passed, the milkiness disappears because soluble calcium hydrogen carbonate is formed.

```txt
CaCO3(s) + H2O(l) + CO2(g) → Ca(HCO3)2(aq)
```

Important examples of calcium carbonate:

- Limestone
- Chalk
- Marble

---

## 3.4 Reaction of Acids and Bases: Neutralisation

When an acid reacts with a base, salt and water are formed. This reaction is called **neutralisation reaction**.

General reaction:

```txt
Acid + Base → Salt + Water
```

Example:

```txt
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)
```

Ionic form:

```txt
H+(aq) + OH-(aq) → H2O(l)
```

Activity idea:

- Phenolphthalein is pink in sodium hydroxide solution.
- When hydrochloric acid is added drop by drop, the pink colour disappears.
- This happens because the base is neutralised by the acid.
- If sodium hydroxide is added again, the pink colour returns.

---

## 3.5 Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids

Metallic oxides react with acids to form salt and water.

General reaction:

```txt
Metal oxide + Acid → Salt + Water
```

Example:

```txt
CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CuCl2(aq) + H2O(l)
```

Observation:

- Black copper oxide dissolves in dilute hydrochloric acid.
- A blue-green solution of copper(II) chloride is formed.

Conclusion:

Metallic oxides are basic in nature because they react with acids to give salt and water.

---

## 3.6 Reaction of Non-Metallic Oxides with Bases

Non-metallic oxides react with bases to form salt and water.

Example:

```txt
CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)
```

Conclusion:

Non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature because they react with bases to give salt and water.

## 4. What Do All Acids and Bases Have in Common?

## 4.1 Acids Produce Hydrogen Ions in Water

All acids show acidic behaviour because they produce hydrogen ions in water.

Example:

```txt
HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl-
```

Hydrogen ions cannot exist freely in water. They combine with water molecules to form hydronium ions.

```txt
H+ + H2O → H3O+
```

Important point:

Acids show acidic properties only in the presence of water.

## 4.2 Bases Produce Hydroxide Ions in Water

Bases show basic behaviour because they produce hydroxide ions in water.

Examples:

```txt
NaOH(s) --water--> Na+(aq) + OH-(aq)
```

```txt
KOH(s) --water--> K+(aq) + OH-(aq)
```

```txt
Mg(OH)2(s) --water--> Mg2+(aq) + 2OH-(aq)
```

## 5. Acids, Bases and Electrical Conductivity

Aqueous solutions of acids and bases conduct electricity because they produce ions in water.

### Acid Conductivity

Acids produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions in water. These ions carry electric current.

Example:

- Dilute HCl conducts electricity.
- Dilute H2SO4 conducts electricity.

### Why Glucose and Alcohol Are Not Acids

Glucose and alcohol contain hydrogen, but their aqueous solutions do not produce `H+` ions. Therefore, they do not show acidic behaviour and do not conduct electricity like acids.

### Dry HCl Gas and Litmus

Dry hydrogen chloride gas does not change the colour of dry litmus paper because no `H+` ions are produced without water.

Moist blue litmus turns red in HCl gas because water helps HCl produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions.

---

## 6. Dilution of Acids and Bases

Dilution means mixing an acid or base with water to decrease the concentration of ions per unit volume.

When an acid or base is dissolved in water, the process is highly exothermic.

### Safety Rule

Always add acid slowly to water while stirring continuously.

Do not add water to concentrated acid.

Reason:

- Mixing concentrated acid with water releases a large amount of heat.
- If water is added to acid, the mixture may splash out and cause burns.
- The glass container may also break due to local heating.

### Effect of Dilution

When acid is diluted:

```txt
H3O+ ion concentration decreases per unit volume.
```

When base is diluted:

```txt
OH- ion concentration decreases per unit volume.
```

## 7. Strength of Acids and Bases: pH Scale

The pH scale is used to measure how acidic or basic a solution is.

Range:

```txt
0 to 14
```

Meaning:

| pH Value | Nature of Solution |
|---|---|
| Less than 7 | Acidic |
| 7 | Neutral |
| Greater than 7 | Basic/alkaline |

Important points:

- Lower pH means stronger acidic nature.
- Higher pH means stronger basic nature.
- Neutral solution has pH 7.
- Pure water has pH 7.
- Sodium hydroxide solution may have pH close to 14.
- Strong acids may have pH close to 1.

### Relation with Hydrogen Ion Concentration

If hydrogen ion concentration increases, pH decreases.

```txt
More H+ ions → Lower pH → More acidic
```

If hydroxide ion concentration increases, pH increases.

```txt
More OH- ions → Higher pH → More basic
```

### Strong and Weak Acids

A strong acid produces more `H+` ions in water.

A weak acid produces fewer `H+` ions in water.

Example:

- Hydrochloric acid is stronger than acetic acid at the same concentration.
- HCl reacts faster with magnesium than acetic acid because it produces more `H+` ions.

## 8. Importance of pH in Daily Life

## 8.1 pH in Living Organisms

The human body works properly in a narrow pH range of about `7.0` to `7.8`.

Living organisms are sensitive to pH changes.

### Acid Rain

If the pH of rainwater becomes less than about `5.6`, it is called acid rain.

Effect:

- Acid rain lowers the pH of river water.
- Aquatic life becomes difficult in such water.

## 8.2 pH of Soil

Plants need a specific soil pH for healthy growth.

If soil is too acidic, farmers may add:

- Quicklime: `CaO`
- Slaked lime: `Ca(OH)2`
- Chalk: `CaCO3`

These substances neutralise excess acidity in soil.

## 8.3 pH in the Digestive System

The stomach produces hydrochloric acid, which helps in digestion.

During indigestion, the stomach produces too much acid. This causes pain and irritation.

Antacids are weak bases that neutralise excess stomach acid.

Example:

```txt
Magnesium hydroxide, Mg(OH)2
```

Milk of magnesia is commonly used as an antacid.

## 8.4 pH and Tooth Decay

Tooth decay starts when the pH of the mouth falls below about `5.5`.

Reason:

- Bacteria in the mouth break down sugar and food particles.
- Acids are produced.
- These acids corrode tooth enamel.

Tooth enamel is mainly made of calcium phosphate and is the hardest substance in the body.

Prevention:

- Clean mouth after eating.
- Use basic toothpaste to neutralise excess acid.

## 8.5 Self-Defence by Animals and Plants

Bee sting and nettle leaves release acids that cause pain and irritation.

Examples:

| Source | Acid |
|---|---|
| Ant sting | Methanoic acid |
| Nettle sting | Methanoic acid |

Relief:

A weak base like baking soda can neutralise the acid and reduce pain.

## 10. Salts

A salt is an ionic compound formed when an acid reacts with a base, metal, metal carbonate or metal hydrogen carbonate.

Examples:

| Salt | Formula |
|---|---|
| Sodium chloride | `NaCl` |
| Potassium nitrate | `KNO3` |
| Sodium sulphate | `Na2SO4` |
| Calcium sulphate | `CaSO4` |
| Copper sulphate | `CuSO4` |
| Sodium carbonate | `Na2CO3` |
| Sodium hydrogen carbonate | `NaHCO3` |

## 10.1 Salt Families

Salts having the same positive ion or same negative ion belong to the same family.

Examples:

- `NaCl` and `Na2SO4` belong to the sodium salt family because both contain `Na+`.
- `NaCl` and `KCl` belong to the chloride salt family because both contain `Cl-`.

## 10.2 pH of Salts

The pH of a salt depends on the acid and base from which it is formed.

| Salt Made From | Nature of Salt Solution | pH |
|---|---|---|
| Strong acid + Strong base | Neutral | Around 7 |
| Strong acid + Weak base | Acidic | Less than 7 |
| Weak acid + Strong base | Basic | Greater than 7 |

Example:

- Sodium chloride is made from strong acid `HCl` and strong base `NaOH`, so it is neutral.
- Sodium carbonate is basic in nature.

Common salt is sodium chloride, `NaCl`.

Sources:

- Sodium hydroxide

## 12. Sodium Hydroxide and the Chlor-Alkali Process

When electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of sodium chloride, sodium hydroxide, chlorine gas and hydrogen gas are produced.

This process is called the **chlor-alkali process** because the products include chlorine and an alkali, sodium hydroxide.

Equation:

```txt
2NaCl(aq) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl2(g) + H2(g)
```

Products formed:

| Product | Place of Formation |
|---|---|
| Chlorine gas | Anode |
| Hydrogen gas | Cathode |
| Sodium hydroxide solution | Near cathode |

### Uses of Hydrogen

- Fuels
- Margarine production
- Ammonia production for fertilisers

### Uses of Chlorine

- Water treatment
- Swimming pool disinfection
- PVC production
- Disinfectants
- Pesticides
- CFCs in older applications

### Uses of Sodium Hydroxide

- Soap and detergent manufacture
- Paper making
- Artificial fibres
- Removing grease from metals

## 13. Bleaching Powder

Bleaching powder is commonly represented as `CaOCl2`.

It is prepared by passing chlorine gas over dry slaked lime.

Equation:

```txt
Ca(OH)2 + Cl2 → CaOCl2 + H2O
```

### Uses of Bleaching Powder

- Bleaching cotton and linen in textile industry
- Bleaching wood pulp in paper factories
- Bleaching washed clothes in laundry
- As an oxidising agent in chemical industries
- Disinfecting drinking water

## 14. Baking Soda

Baking soda is sodium hydrogen carbonate.

Formula:

```txt
NaHCO3
```

Preparation:

```txt
NaCl + H2O + CO2 + NH3 → NH4Cl + NaHCO3
```

Baking soda is a mild, non-corrosive basic salt.

### Heating of Baking Soda

On heating, baking soda decomposes to form sodium carbonate, water and carbon dioxide.

```txt
2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2
```

### Uses of Baking Soda

1. Making baking powder
2. As an antacid
3. In soda-acid fire extinguishers

### Baking Powder

Baking powder is a mixture of baking soda and a mild edible acid such as tartaric acid.

When baking powder is heated or mixed with water, carbon dioxide is produced.

```txt
NaHCO3 + H+ → CO2 + H2O + Sodium salt of acid
```

Carbon dioxide makes cakes and bread soft and spongy.

## 15. Washing Soda

Washing soda is sodium carbonate decahydrate.

Formula:

```txt
Na2CO3.10H2O
```

It can be obtained from sodium carbonate by recrystallisation.

```txt
Na2CO3 + 10H2O → Na2CO3.10H2O
```

Sodium carbonate can be obtained by heating sodium hydrogen carbonate.

```txt
2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2
```

### Uses of Washing Soda

- Used in glass, soap and paper industries
- Used in the manufacture of sodium compounds such as borax
- Used as a cleaning agent in homes
- Used to remove permanent hardness of water

---

## 16. Water of Crystallisation

Water of crystallisation is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.

### Copper Sulphate Crystals

Hydrated copper sulphate is blue.

Formula:

```txt
CuSO4.5H2O
```

When heated, copper sulphate loses water of crystallisation and becomes white.

When water is added again, it becomes blue again.

Important concept:

The blue colour of copper sulphate crystals is due to water of crystallisation.

### Gypsum

Gypsum contains two molecules of water of crystallisation.

Formula:

```txt
CaSO4.2H2O
```

---

## 17. Plaster of Paris

Plaster of Paris is calcium sulphate hemihydrate.

Formula:

```txt
CaSO4.1/2H2O
```

It is prepared by heating gypsum at `373 K`.

```txt
CaSO4.2H2O --373 K--> CaSO4.1/2H2O + 3/2H2O
```

When plaster of Paris is mixed with water, it changes back to gypsum and becomes hard.

```txt
CaSO4.1/2H2O + 3/2H2O → CaSO4.2H2O
```

### Uses of Plaster of Paris

- Making casts for fractured bones
- Making toys
- Making decorative materials
- Making surfaces smooth

Important storage point:

Plaster of Paris should be stored in moisture-proof containers because it reacts with water and becomes hard gypsum.

## 23. Long Answer Questions

### Q1. Explain the reaction of acids with metal carbonates and metal hydrogen carbonates.

Acids react with metal carbonates and metal hydrogen carbonates to form salt, carbon dioxide and water.

General reactions:

```txt
Metal carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
```

```txt
Metal hydrogen carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
```

Examples:

```txt
Na2CO3 + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2
```

```txt
NaHCO3 + HCl → NaCl + H2O + CO2
```

Carbon dioxide is tested by passing it through lime water. Lime water turns milky due to calcium carbonate formation.

```txt
Ca(OH)2 + CO2 → CaCO3 + H2O
```

### Q2. Why should acid be added to water and not water to acid?

Dilution of concentrated acid is highly exothermic. If water is added directly to concentrated acid, a large amount of heat is released suddenly. The mixture may splash and cause burns. Therefore, acid should be added slowly to water with continuous stirring.

### Q3. Explain the importance of pH in daily life.

pH is important in many daily-life situations:

- Human body works properly in pH range about 7.0 to 7.8.
- Acid rain with pH below 5.6 harms aquatic life.
- Plants need suitable soil pH for healthy growth.
- Excess stomach acid causes indigestion and is treated by antacids.
- Tooth decay starts when mouth pH falls below about 5.5.
- Bee sting and nettle sting acids can be neutralised by weak bases.

### Q4. Explain the chlor-alkali process.

When electricity is passed through aqueous sodium chloride solution, sodium chloride decomposes to form sodium hydroxide, chlorine gas and hydrogen gas.

Equation:

```txt
2NaCl(aq) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl2(g) + H2(g)
```

Chlorine is produced at the anode. Hydrogen is produced at the cathode. Sodium hydroxide solution is formed near the cathode.

This process is called the chlor-alkali process because chlorine and alkali are produced.

### Q5. Write the preparation and uses of bleaching powder.

Bleaching powder is prepared by passing chlorine gas over dry slaked lime.

```txt
Ca(OH)2 + Cl2 → CaOCl2 + H2O
```

Uses:

- Bleaching cotton and linen
- Bleaching wood pulp
- Bleaching clothes in laundry
- Disinfecting drinking water
- Oxidising agent in chemical industries

### Q6. Write the preparation and uses of baking soda.

Baking soda is sodium hydrogen carbonate, `NaHCO3`.

Preparation:

```txt
NaCl + H2O + CO2 + NH3 → NH4Cl + NaHCO3
```

Uses:

- Making baking powder
- As an antacid
- In soda-acid fire extinguishers

On heating:

```txt
2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2
```

### Q7. Write the preparation and uses of washing soda.

Washing soda is sodium carbonate decahydrate, `Na2CO3.10H2O`.

It is obtained by recrystallisation of sodium carbonate.

```txt
Na2CO3 + 10H2O → Na2CO3.10H2O
```

Uses:

- Glass, soap and paper industries
- Manufacture of borax
- Cleaning agent
- Removing permanent hardness of water

### Q8. What is water of crystallisation? Explain with example.

Water of crystallisation is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.

Example:

```txt
CuSO4.5H2O
```

Copper sulphate crystals are blue because of water of crystallisation. On heating, they lose water and become white. On adding water again, the blue colour returns.

### Q9. What is Plaster of Paris? Write its preparation and uses.

Plaster of Paris is calcium sulphate hemihydrate.

Formula:

```txt
CaSO4.1/2H2O
```

It is prepared by heating gypsum at 373 K.

```txt
CaSO4.2H2O --373 K--> CaSO4.1/2H2O + 3/2H2O
```

Uses:

- Making casts for fractured bones
- Making toys
- Making decorative items
- Making smooth surfaces

## 24. Exercise Questions with Answer Hints

### Q1. A solution turns red litmus blue. What is its likely pH?

It is basic, so its pH is greater than 7. Among common options, pH 10 is correct.

### Q2. A solution reacts with crushed egg shell and produces a gas that turns lime water milky. What is the solution?

Egg shell contains calcium carbonate. It reacts with acid to produce carbon dioxide, which turns lime water milky. The solution may be hydrochloric acid.

### Q3. 10 mL NaOH is neutralised by 8 mL HCl. How much HCl is needed for 20 mL NaOH?

For double the amount of NaOH, double the amount of HCl is needed.

Answer:

```txt
16 mL
```

### Q4. Which medicine is used to treat indigestion?

Antacid.

### Q5. Why are alcohol and glucose not classified as acids although they contain hydrogen?

They do not produce `H+` ions in aqueous solution. Therefore, they do not show acidic behaviour.

### Q6. Why is distilled water not a conductor of electricity, but rainwater is?

Distilled water has almost no ions, so it does not conduct electricity. Rainwater contains dissolved gases and salts, so it has ions and conducts electricity.

### Q7. Why does an acid not show acidic behaviour in the absence of water?

Acids produce `H+` ions only in the presence of water. Without water, ions are not produced, so acidic behaviour is not shown.

### Q8. Five solutions have pH values 4, 1, 11, 7 and 9. Identify their nature.

| pH | Nature |
|---|---|
| 1 | Strongly acidic |
| 4 | Weakly acidic |
| 7 | Neutral |
| 9 | Weakly basic |
| 11 | Strongly basic |

### Q9. Fresh milk has pH 6. What happens to pH when it becomes curd?

The pH decreases because lactic acid is formed during curd formation.

### Q10. Why does milk take longer to become curd when baking soda is added?

Baking soda is basic. It neutralises the acid produced during curd formation, so more acid must be produced before milk turns into curd. Therefore, curd formation takes longer.

### Q11. What is neutralisation reaction? Give two examples.

Neutralisation is the reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.

Examples:

```txt
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O
```

```txt
H2SO4 + 2KOH → K2SO4 + 2H2O
```

### Q12. Write two uses each of washing soda and baking soda.

Washing soda:

- Used as a cleaning agent
- Used to remove permanent hardness of water

Baking soda:

- Used in baking powder
- Used as an antacid
