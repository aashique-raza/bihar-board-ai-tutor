# Chapter 3: Metals and Non-metals

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains how elements are classified as metals and non-metals on the basis of their physical and chemical properties. It covers metallic lustre, hardness, malleability, ductility, conductivity, sonority, reactions of metals with oxygen, water, acids and salt solutions, the reactivity series, formation and properties of ionic compounds, extraction of metals from ores, refining of metals, corrosion, prevention of corrosion and alloys.

The chapter is important for board exams because many questions are asked from:
- physical differences between metals and non-metals,
- exceptions in metallic/non-metallic properties,
- balanced chemical equations,
- displacement reactions,
- activity series,
- ionic bond formation,
- extraction of metals,
- corrosion and rusting,
- galvanisation and alloys.

---

After studying this chapter, a student should be able to:

1. Identify metals and non-metals using physical properties.
2. Explain exceptions such as mercury, iodine, graphite, gallium and caesium.
3. Write chemical equations for reactions of metals with oxygen, water, acids and salt solutions.
4. Explain amphoteric oxides.
5. Arrange metals according to the activity series.
6. Explain how ionic compounds are formed.
7. List the properties of ionic compounds.
8. Define mineral, ore and gangue.
9. Explain extraction methods for metals of low, medium and high reactivity.
10. Explain corrosion, rusting and methods of prevention.
11. Explain alloys and their uses.

# 1. Physical Properties of Metals and Non-metals

## 1.1 Metals

Metals generally show the following physical properties:

| Property | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Metallic lustre | Metals have a shiny surface when freshly cut or polished. | Iron, copper, aluminium |
| Hardness | Most metals are hard. | Iron, copper |
| Malleability | Metals can be beaten into thin sheets. | Gold, silver, aluminium |
| Ductility | Metals can be drawn into thin wires. | Gold, copper |
| Conductivity | Metals conduct heat and electricity. | Silver, copper |
| Sonority | Metals produce sound when struck. | Iron bell |
| Solid state | Most metals are solid at room temperature. | Iron, aluminium |

### Important points

- Gold and silver are the most malleable metals.
- Gold is highly ductile. A small amount of gold can be drawn into a very long wire.
- Silver and copper are very good conductors of heat.
- Lead and mercury are poor conductors of heat compared to other metals.
- School bells are made of metals because metals are sonorous.

---

## 1.2 Exceptions in Metals

Some metals do not follow the usual properties of metals.

| Exception | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Mercury | It is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature. |
| Sodium, potassium, lithium | These are soft metals and can be cut with a knife. |
| Gallium and caesium | These metals have low melting points and can melt on the palm. |
| Lead and mercury | These are poor conductors of heat compared to silver and copper. |
| Pure gold | It is very soft; therefore it is mixed with other metals to make jewellery. |

---

## 1.3 Non-metals

Non-metals generally show properties opposite to metals.

| Property | Non-metal behaviour |
|---|---|
| Lustre | Usually dull |
| Hardness | Usually soft or brittle |
| Malleability | Not malleable |
| Ductility | Not ductile |
| Conductivity | Poor conductors of heat and electricity |
| Sonority | Not sonorous |
| State | May be solid, liquid or gas |

### Examples of non-metals

- Carbon
- Sulphur
- Iodine
- Oxygen
- Hydrogen
- Nitrogen
- Chlorine
- Bromine

### Important exceptions in non-metals

| Exception | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Iodine | A non-metal but shiny/lustrous. |
| Graphite | A non-metal but conducts electricity. |
| Diamond | A form of carbon and the hardest natural substance. |
| Bromine | A non-metal that is liquid at room temperature. |

---

## 1.4 Physical Difference Between Metals and Non-metals

| Property | Metals | Non-metals |
|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Generally shiny | Generally dull |
| Hardness | Usually hard | Usually soft or brittle |
| Malleability | Malleable | Non-malleable |
| Ductility | Ductile | Non-ductile |
| Heat conduction | Good conductors | Poor conductors |
| Electrical conduction | Good conductors | Poor conductors except graphite |
| Sonority | Sonorous | Non-sonorous |
| Room temperature state | Mostly solid except mercury | Solid, liquid or gas |

# 2. Chemical Properties of Metals

## 2.1 Reaction of Metals with Oxygen

Most metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides.

```text
Metal + Oxygen → Metal oxide
```

### Example: Copper

When copper is heated in air, it reacts with oxygen and forms black copper(II) oxide.

```text
2Cu + O2 → 2CuO
```

### Example: Aluminium

Aluminium reacts with oxygen to form aluminium oxide.

```text
4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3
```

### Nature of metal oxides

Most metal oxides are basic in nature. They react with acids to form salt and water.

```text
Metal oxide + Acid → Salt + Water
```

---

## 2.2 Amphoteric Oxides

Some metal oxides react with both acids and bases to form salt and water. Such oxides are called amphoteric oxides.

### Examples

- Aluminium oxide, Al2O3
- Zinc oxide, ZnO

### Aluminium oxide with acid

```text
Al2O3 + 6HCl → 2AlCl3 + 3H2O
```

### Aluminium oxide with base

```text
Al2O3 + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO2 + H2O
```

Here sodium aluminate, NaAlO2, is formed.

---

## 2.3 Metal Oxides Soluble in Water

Some metal oxides dissolve in water to form alkalis.

### Sodium oxide

```text
Na2O(s) + H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq)
```

### Potassium oxide

```text
K2O(s) + H2O(l) → 2KOH(aq)
```

---

## 2.4 Reactivity of Metals with Oxygen

Different metals react with oxygen at different rates.

| Metal | Behaviour with oxygen |
|---|---|
| Potassium and sodium | React so vigorously that they catch fire if kept open. Stored in kerosene. |
| Magnesium | Burns with a bright white flame. |
| Aluminium, zinc, lead | Form a thin oxide layer on the surface. |
| Iron | Does not burn easily, but iron filings burn when sprinkled in flame. |
| Copper | Does not burn, but forms black copper oxide on heating. |
| Silver and gold | Do not react with oxygen even at high temperature. |

---

# 3. Reaction of Metals with Water

Metals react with water to form metal oxide or metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

```text
Metal + Water → Metal oxide + Hydrogen
Metal oxide + Water → Metal hydroxide
```

Not all metals react with water in the same way.

---

## 3.1 Potassium and Sodium with Water

Potassium and sodium react violently with cold water. The reaction is highly exothermic and the hydrogen gas produced may catch fire.

```text
2K(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H2(g) + Heat
```

```text
2Na(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H2(g) + Heat
```

### Why sodium is stored in kerosene

Sodium reacts vigorously with oxygen and moisture present in air. To prevent accidental fire, sodium is stored under kerosene oil.

---

## 3.2 Calcium with Water

Calcium reacts with cold water less violently than sodium and potassium.

```text
Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)
```

Calcium starts floating because bubbles of hydrogen gas stick to its surface.

---

## 3.3 Magnesium with Water

Magnesium does not react with cold water. It reacts with hot water to form magnesium hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

```text
Mg(s) + 2H2O(hot) → Mg(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)
```

Magnesium may also start floating because hydrogen gas bubbles stick to its surface.

---

## 3.4 Aluminium, Zinc and Iron with Water

Aluminium, zinc and iron do not react with cold or hot water. They react with steam to form metal oxides and hydrogen gas.

### Aluminium with steam

```text
2Al(s) + 3H2O(g) → Al2O3(s) + 3H2(g)
```

### Iron with steam

```text
3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)
```

---

## 3.5 Metals that Do Not React with Water

Lead, copper, silver and gold do not react with water.

---

# 4. Reaction of Metals with Dilute Acids

Metals generally react with dilute acids to produce salt and hydrogen gas.

```text
Metal + Dilute acid → Salt + Hydrogen gas
```

### Example: Zinc with dilute sulphuric acid

```text
Zn + H2SO4 → ZnSO4 + H2
```

### Example: Magnesium with dilute hydrochloric acid

```text
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl2 + H2
```

### Example: Iron with dilute sulphuric acid

```text
Fe + H2SO4 → FeSO4 + H2
```

---

## 4.1 Test for Hydrogen Gas

Hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound when a burning candle or matchstick is brought near it.

---

## 4.2 Reactivity Order with Dilute HCl

The rate of bubbles and heat produced shows the reactivity of metals.

```text
Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Cu
```

Copper does not react with dilute hydrochloric acid under normal conditions because copper is less reactive than hydrogen.

---

## 4.3 Metals with Nitric Acid

Metals generally do not produce hydrogen gas with nitric acid.

Reason:

Nitric acid is a strong oxidising agent. It oxidises the hydrogen gas produced into water and itself gets reduced to oxides of nitrogen such as N2O, NO or NO2.

Exception:

Magnesium and manganese react with very dilute nitric acid to produce hydrogen gas.

---

# 5. Reaction of Metals with Salt Solutions

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

```text
More reactive metal + Salt solution of less reactive metal
→ Salt solution of more reactive metal + Less reactive metal
```

### Example: Iron and copper sulphate

Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

```text
Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
```

Observation:
- Blue colour of copper sulphate solution fades.
- Brown copper deposits on the iron nail.

### Example: Zinc and copper sulphate

```text
Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
```

### Example: Copper and iron sulphate

Copper cannot displace iron from iron sulphate solution because copper is less reactive than iron.

```text
Cu + FeSO4 → No reaction
```

# 6. Activity Series of Metals

The activity series is a list of metals arranged in decreasing order of reactivity.

## Activity Series

```text
K    Potassium       Most reactive
Na   Sodium
Ca   Calcium
Mg   Magnesium
Al   Aluminium
Zn   Zinc
Fe   Iron
Pb   Lead
H    Hydrogen
Cu   Copper
Hg   Mercury
Ag   Silver
Au   Gold            Least reactive
```

## Use of Activity Series

1. Metals above hydrogen can displace hydrogen from dilute acids.
2. Metals below hydrogen cannot usually displace hydrogen from dilute acids.
3. A metal can displace another metal placed below it from its salt solution.
4. The method of extraction depends on the position of the metal in the activity series.

# 7. How Metals and Non-metals React

Metals tend to lose electrons and form positive ions.

```text
Metal atom → Positive ion + Electron(s)
```

Non-metals tend to gain electrons and form negative ions.

```text
Non-metal atom + Electron(s) → Negative ion
```

The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions forms an ionic bond.

---

## 7.1 Formation of Sodium Chloride

Sodium has one electron in its outermost shell.

```text
Na → Na+ + e-
```

Chlorine has seven electrons in its outermost shell and needs one electron to complete its octet.

```text
Cl + e- → Cl-
```

Sodium transfers one electron to chlorine. Sodium becomes Na+ and chlorine becomes Cl-. These oppositely charged ions attract each other and form sodium chloride.

```text
Na+ + Cl- → NaCl
```

Sodium chloride is not made of individual NaCl molecules. It exists as an ionic lattice of Na+ and Cl- ions.

---

## 7.2 Formation of Magnesium Chloride

Magnesium has two electrons in its outermost shell.

```text
Mg → Mg2+ + 2e-
```

Each chlorine atom gains one electron.

```text
Cl + e- → Cl-
```

One magnesium atom transfers two electrons to two chlorine atoms.

```text
Mg2+ + 2Cl- → MgCl2
```

Ions present in magnesium chloride:
- Mg2+ = magnesium ion
- Cl- = chloride ion

---

# 8. Ionic Compounds

Compounds formed by transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal are called ionic compounds or electrovalent compounds.

## 8.1 Properties of Ionic Compounds

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Physical nature | Ionic compounds are usually solid and hard. |
| Brittleness | They break into pieces under pressure. |
| High melting and boiling points | Strong electrostatic forces between ions require large energy to break. |
| Solubility | Usually soluble in water, insoluble in petrol, kerosene and similar solvents. |
| Electrical conductivity in solid state | Do not conduct electricity because ions cannot move freely. |
| Electrical conductivity in molten state | Conduct electricity because ions can move freely. |
| Electrical conductivity in aqueous solution | Conduct electricity because ions are free to move in water. |

### Examples of ionic compounds

| Ionic compound | Ions present |
|---|---|
| NaCl | Na+, Cl- |
| MgCl2 | Mg2+, Cl- |
| CaCl2 | Ca2+, Cl- |
| MgO | Mg2+, O2- |
| Na2O | Na+, O2- |

---

# 9. Occurrence of Metals

The Earth's crust is the main source of metals. Some salts are also found in sea water.

## Important Terms

### Mineral

Naturally occurring elements or compounds found in the Earth's crust are called minerals.

### Ore

A mineral from which a metal can be extracted profitably is called an ore.

### Gangue

Impurities such as soil, sand and other unwanted materials present in an ore are called gangue.

---

# 10. Extraction of Metals

Metals are extracted from their ores according to their reactivity.

## 10.1 Metals of Low Reactivity

Metals placed low in the activity series are less reactive. They may occur in free state or can be obtained easily from their compounds.

Examples:
- Gold
- Silver
- Platinum
- Copper
- Mercury

### Extraction of mercury from cinnabar

Cinnabar is mercury sulphide, HgS.

First, mercury sulphide is heated in air to form mercury oxide.

```text
2HgS(s) + 3O2(g) → 2HgO(s) + 2SO2(g)
```

Then mercury oxide is heated to obtain mercury.

```text
2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O2(g)
```

### Extraction of copper from copper sulphide

Copper can be obtained from copper sulphide by heating in air.

```text
2Cu2S + 3O2 → 2Cu2O + 2SO2
```

```text
2Cu2O + Cu2S → 6Cu + SO2
```

---

## 10.2 Metals of Medium Reactivity

Metals in the middle of the activity series are found mainly as sulphides or carbonates.

Examples:
- Zinc
- Iron
- Lead
- Copper

It is easier to obtain a metal from its oxide than from its sulphide or carbonate. Therefore sulphide and carbonate ores are first converted into oxides.

---

## 10.3 Roasting

Roasting is the process of heating sulphide ore strongly in the presence of excess air to convert it into metal oxide.

### Example

```text
2ZnS(s) + 3O2(g) → 2ZnO(s) + 2SO2(g)
```

---

## 10.4 Calcination

Calcination is the process of heating carbonate ore strongly in limited air to convert it into metal oxide.

### Example

```text
ZnCO3(s) → ZnO(s) + CO2(g)
```

---

## 10.5 Reduction of Metal Oxides

After conversion into oxide, the metal oxide is reduced to metal using reducing agents such as carbon.

### Example: Zinc oxide reduced by carbon

```text
ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g)
```

This is a reduction process because zinc oxide loses oxygen.

---

## 10.6 Displacement Method for Extraction

Highly reactive metals such as aluminium can displace less reactive metals from their oxides.

### Manganese dioxide with aluminium

```text
3MnO2(s) + 4Al(s) → 3Mn(l) + 2Al2O3(s) + Heat
```

---

## 10.7 Thermite Reaction

The reaction between iron(III) oxide and aluminium is called the thermite reaction.

```text
Fe2O3(s) + 2Al(s) → 2Fe(l) + Al2O3(s) + Heat
```

This reaction produces a very high amount of heat. The molten iron formed is used to join railway tracks and repair broken machine parts.

---

## 10.8 Metals of High Reactivity

Highly reactive metals are placed at the top of the activity series.

Examples:
- Potassium
- Sodium
- Calcium
- Magnesium
- Aluminium

These metals cannot be obtained by reducing their oxides with carbon because they have more affinity for oxygen than carbon. They are extracted by electrolytic reduction of their molten chlorides or oxides.

### Example: Sodium from molten sodium chloride

At cathode:

```text
Na+ + e- → Na
```

At anode:

```text
2Cl- → Cl2 + 2e-
```

---

# 11. Refining of Metals

Metals obtained after extraction usually contain impurities. The process of removing impurities from metals is called refining.

## 11.1 Electrolytic Refining

Electrolytic refining is a common method used to purify metals such as copper, zinc, tin, nickel, silver and gold.

### Electrolytic refining of copper

| Part | Material |
|---|---|
| Anode | Impure copper |
| Cathode | Thin strip of pure copper |
| Electrolyte | Acidified copper sulphate solution |

When electric current is passed:
- impure copper from the anode dissolves into the electrolyte,
- pure copper deposits on the cathode,
- soluble impurities remain in solution,
- insoluble impurities settle below the anode as anode mud.

# 12. Corrosion

Corrosion is the slow destruction of metals due to reaction with substances present in the environment such as air, moisture, acids or gases.

## Examples

| Metal | Corrosion effect |
|---|---|
| Iron | Forms reddish-brown rust in moist air. |
| Silver | Turns black due to silver sulphide formation. |
| Copper | Forms green coating due to copper carbonate/basic copper carbonate. |

---

## 12.1 Rusting of Iron

Rusting of iron requires both air and water.

In the rusting experiment:
- Test tube A contains both air and water, so iron nails rust.
- Test tube B contains boiled water and oil layer, so dissolved air is absent; nails do not rust.
- Test tube C contains dry air with anhydrous calcium chloride, so moisture is absent; nails do not rust.

Conclusion:

```text
Iron rusts only when both oxygen/air and water/moisture are present.
```

---

# 13. Prevention of Corrosion

Iron can be protected from rusting by preventing contact with air and moisture.

## Common Methods

1. Painting
2. Oiling
3. Greasing
4. Galvanisation
5. Chrome plating
6. Anodising
7. Alloying

---

## 13.1 Galvanisation

Galvanisation is the process of coating iron or steel with a thin layer of zinc to protect it from rusting.

Even if the zinc coating is scratched, iron remains protected because zinc is more reactive than iron and gets oxidised first.

---

## 13.2 Anodising

Anodising is the process of forming a thick oxide layer on aluminium.

Aluminium naturally forms a thin oxide layer when exposed to air. This layer protects it from corrosion. In anodising, this layer is made thicker by electrolysis, making aluminium more resistant to corrosion. The oxide layer can also be dyed to make attractive aluminium articles.

---

# 14. Alloys

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.

Alloying is done to improve the properties of metals.

## Examples of Alloys

| Alloy | Composition | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Steel | Iron + carbon | Strong structures, tools |
| Stainless steel | Iron + nickel + chromium | Utensils, corrosion-resistant objects |
| Brass | Copper + zinc | Decorative articles, instruments |
| Bronze | Copper + tin | Statues, medals |
| Solder | Lead + tin | Joining electrical wires |
| Amalgam | Alloy containing mercury | Dental/metal applications |

### Important points

- Pure iron is soft and stretches easily when hot. Adding carbon makes it harder and stronger.
- Stainless steel does not rust easily.
- Pure gold is 24 carat and soft. It is mixed with copper or silver to make jewellery.
- 22 carat gold means 22 parts gold and 2 parts copper or silver.
- If mercury is one component of an alloy, the alloy is called an amalgam.
- Alloys usually have lower electrical conductivity and lower melting point than pure metals.

# 17. Important Definitions

## Metal

A metal is an element that usually loses electrons to form positive ions and generally shows properties such as lustre, malleability, ductility and conductivity.

## Non-metal

A non-metal is an element that usually gains electrons to form negative ions and generally lacks metallic properties.

## Malleability

The property by which metals can be beaten into thin sheets is called malleability.

## Ductility

The property by which metals can be drawn into thin wires is called ductility.

## Sonority

The property by which metals produce a ringing sound when struck is called sonority.

## Amphoteric oxide

An oxide that reacts with both acids and bases to form salt and water is called an amphoteric oxide.

## Activity series

The arrangement of metals in decreasing order of their reactivity is called the activity series.

## Ionic compound

A compound formed by transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal is called an ionic compound.

## Mineral

Naturally occurring elements or compounds found in the Earth's crust are called minerals.

## Ore

A mineral from which a metal can be extracted profitably is called an ore.

## Gangue

Impurities such as sand and soil present in an ore are called gangue.

## Roasting

Heating sulphide ore in excess air to convert it into oxide is called roasting.

## Calcination

Heating carbonate ore in limited air to convert it into oxide is called calcination.

## Reduction

Removal of oxygen from a compound or gain of electrons is called reduction.

## Corrosion

The slow destruction of a metal due to reaction with air, moisture or other substances in the environment is called corrosion.

## Galvanisation

The process of coating iron or steel with zinc to prevent rusting is called galvanisation.

## Alloy

A homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal, is called an alloy.

## Anode mud

The insoluble impurities that settle below the anode during electrolytic refining are called anode mud.

# 19. Short Answer Questions

## Q1. Why are metals used for making cooking utensils?

Metals are good conductors of heat. They transfer heat quickly and evenly, so they are used for making cooking utensils.

## Q2. Why are electric wires covered with plastic or rubber?

Metals conduct electricity, so touching bare metal wires can cause electric shock. Plastic and rubber are insulators and protect users from electric current.

## Q3. Why is sodium stored in kerosene?

Sodium reacts vigorously with oxygen and moisture in air and may catch fire. Kerosene prevents contact with air and water.

## Q4. Why does calcium float on water?

Calcium reacts with water to produce hydrogen gas. Bubbles of hydrogen gas stick to the surface of calcium and make it float.

## Q5. Why does copper not react with dilute hydrochloric acid?

Copper is less reactive than hydrogen, so it cannot displace hydrogen from dilute hydrochloric acid.

## Q6. What is the test for hydrogen gas?

Hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound when a burning matchstick is brought near it.

## Q7. What are amphoteric oxides? Give examples.

Oxides that react with both acids and bases to form salt and water are called amphoteric oxides. Examples: Al2O3 and ZnO.

## Q8. Why do ionic compounds have high melting points?

Ionic compounds contain strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions. A large amount of energy is needed to break these forces.

## Q9. Why do ionic compounds conduct electricity in molten state?

In molten state, ions are free to move. These moving ions conduct electricity.

## Q10. Why does iron rust only in moist air?

Rusting needs both oxygen and water. In moist air, both are available, so iron rusts.

## Q11. What is galvanisation?

Galvanisation is the process of coating iron or steel with zinc to protect it from rusting.

## Q12. Why is aluminium used for making cooking utensils although it is reactive?

Aluminium forms a protective oxide layer on its surface. This layer prevents further corrosion and makes aluminium useful for utensils.

# 20. Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Explain the reaction of metals with water with examples.

Metals react with water to produce metal oxides or metal hydroxides and hydrogen gas. Different metals react differently depending on their reactivity.

Potassium and sodium react violently with cold water:

```text
2Na + 2H2O → 2NaOH + H2 + Heat
```

Calcium reacts less violently:

```text
Ca + 2H2O → Ca(OH)2 + H2
```

Magnesium reacts with hot water:

```text
Mg + 2H2O → Mg(OH)2 + H2
```

Aluminium and iron react with steam:

```text
2Al + 3H2O → Al2O3 + 3H2
```

```text
3Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2
```

Lead, copper, silver and gold do not react with water.

---

## Q2. Explain displacement reactions using the activity series.

A more reactive metal can displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Example:

```text
Fe + CuSO4 → FeSO4 + Cu
```

Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate because iron is more reactive than copper.

Another example:

```text
Zn + CuSO4 → ZnSO4 + Cu
```

Zinc displaces copper because zinc is more reactive than copper.

Copper cannot displace iron from iron sulphate because copper is less reactive than iron.

```text
Cu + FeSO4 → No reaction
```

## Q3. Explain the formation of sodium chloride.

Sodium has one electron in its outermost shell. It loses this electron and forms Na+.

```text
Na → Na+ + e-
```

Chlorine has seven electrons in its outermost shell. It gains one electron and forms Cl-.

```text
Cl + e- → Cl-
```

Na+ and Cl- are oppositely charged ions. They attract each other strongly and form sodium chloride.

```text
Na+ + Cl- → NaCl
```

This is an ionic compound.

---

## Q4. Explain the properties of ionic compounds.

Ionic compounds have the following properties:

1. They are usually solid and hard.
2. They are brittle.
3. They have high melting and boiling points because strong electrostatic forces hold the ions together.
4. They are usually soluble in water.
5. They are usually insoluble in petrol and kerosene.
6. They do not conduct electricity in solid state because ions cannot move.
7. They conduct electricity in molten or aqueous state because ions are free to move.

---

## Q5. Explain extraction of metals in the middle of the activity series.

Metals in the middle of the activity series, such as zinc, iron and lead, are commonly found as sulphides or carbonates.

First, sulphide or carbonate ores are converted into oxides.

Sulphide ore is converted into oxide by roasting:

```text
2ZnS + 3O2 → 2ZnO + 2SO2
```

Carbonate ore is converted into oxide by calcination:

```text
ZnCO3 → ZnO + CO2
```

Then the metal oxide is reduced using carbon:

```text
ZnO + C → Zn + CO
```

## Q6. Explain electrolytic refining of copper.

In electrolytic refining of copper:
- impure copper is used as anode,
- pure copper strip is used as cathode,
- acidified copper sulphate solution is used as electrolyte.

When electric current is passed, copper from the anode dissolves into the solution and the same amount of pure copper deposits on the cathode. Insoluble impurities settle below the anode as anode mud.

## Q7. What is corrosion? How can it be prevented?

Corrosion is the slow destruction of metals due to reaction with air, moisture, acids or other substances in the environment.

Example: Iron rusts in moist air.

Methods to prevent corrosion:
1. Painting
2. Oiling
3. Greasing
4. Galvanisation
5. Chrome plating
6. Anodising
7. Alloying

Galvanisation protects iron by coating it with zinc. Zinc is more reactive and prevents iron from rusting.

---

## Q8. What are alloys? Why are they useful?

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.

Alloys are useful because they improve the properties of metals. They may become harder, stronger, more corrosion-resistant or more useful for specific purposes.

Examples:
- Stainless steel: iron, nickel and chromium; does not rust easily.
- Brass: copper and zinc.
- Bronze: copper and tin.
- Solder: lead and tin; used for joining electrical wires.
- 22 carat gold: gold mixed with copper or silver for jewellery.
