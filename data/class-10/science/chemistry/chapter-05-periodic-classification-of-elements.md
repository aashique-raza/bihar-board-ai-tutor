# Chapter 5: Periodic Classification of Elements

## Chapter Overview

Elements are the basic substances made up of only one kind of atom. As more elements were discovered, scientists needed a systematic way to arrange them so that their properties could be studied easily.

Early scientists tried to classify elements on the basis of similarities in physical and chemical properties. Later, the modern periodic table arranged elements according to increasing atomic number. This arrangement explains the periodic repetition of properties such as valency, atomic size, metallic character, and non-metallic character.

This chapter explains:

- Early attempts to classify elements
- Dobereiner's triads
- Newlands' law of octaves
- Mendeleev's periodic table
- Achievements and limitations of Mendeleev's periodic table
- Modern periodic law
- Modern periodic table
- Groups and periods
- Trends in valency, atomic size, metallic character, and non-metallic character

---

## 1. Need for Classification of Elements

By around 1800, only about 30 elements were known. Later, many more elements were discovered. Today, more than 100 elements are known.

As the number of known elements increased, it became difficult to study each element separately. Scientists tried to classify elements into groups so that elements with similar properties could be studied together.

### Purpose of classification

Classification helps to:

- Arrange elements systematically
- Study elements easily
- Identify similarities and differences among elements
- Predict properties of unknown elements
- Understand the relationship between atomic structure and chemical properties

---

## 2. Early Attempts at Classification

Initially, elements were broadly classified as:

- Metals
- Non-metals

But this classification was not enough because many elements showed intermediate or special properties. Scientists then started searching for better patterns in the properties of elements.

---

## 3. Dobereiner's Triads

In 1817, Johann Wolfgang Dobereiner tried to classify elements into groups of three elements. These groups were called triads.

### Definition

A Dobereiner triad is a group of three elements having similar chemical properties in which the atomic mass of the middle element is approximately the average of the atomic masses of the other two elements.

### Example: Lithium, Sodium, Potassium

| Element | Symbol | Atomic Mass |
|---|---:|---:|
| Lithium | Li | 6.9 |
| Sodium | Na | 23.0 |
| Potassium | K | 39.0 |

Average atomic mass of lithium and potassium:

```text
(6.9 + 39.0) / 2 = 22.95
```

This value is very close to the atomic mass of sodium, which is 23.0.

So, lithium, sodium, and potassium form a Dobereiner triad.

### Other examples of Dobereiner triads

| Triad | Elements |
|---|---|
| Alkali metals | Li, Na, K |
| Alkaline earth metals | Ca, Sr, Ba |
| Halogens | Cl, Br, I |

### Limitation of Dobereiner's triads

Dobereiner could identify only a few triads among the elements known at that time. Most elements could not be arranged in this way. Therefore, this classification was not successful as a general system.

---

## 4. Newlands' Law of Octaves

In 1866, John Newlands arranged the known elements in increasing order of atomic mass. He found that the properties of every eighth element were similar to those of the first element.

He compared this repetition with the octaves in music.

### Statement of Newlands' Law of Octaves

When elements are arranged in increasing order of atomic mass, the properties of every eighth element are similar to those of the first element.

### Example

Lithium and sodium show similar properties. Sodium is the eighth element after lithium in Newlands' arrangement.

Similarly, beryllium and magnesium show similarity.

### Limitations of Newlands' Law of Octaves

Newlands' law had several problems:

1. It was applicable only up to calcium.
2. After calcium, every eighth element did not show properties similar to the first element.
3. Newlands assumed that only 56 elements existed in nature and no more elements would be discovered.
4. Later, many new elements were discovered, and their properties did not fit the law of octaves.
5. To fit elements into his table, Newlands placed two elements in the same slot.
6. Some dissimilar elements were grouped together.
7. Some similar elements were placed far apart.

### Example of problem

Cobalt and nickel were placed with fluorine, chlorine, and bromine, even though cobalt and nickel do not have properties similar to halogens.

Iron was placed far away from cobalt and nickel, although iron shows similarity with them.

Therefore, Newlands' classification was not widely accepted.

## 5. Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Dmitri Ivanovich Mendeleev made a major contribution to the classification of elements. At his time, 63 elements were known.

Mendeleev studied the relationship between:

- Atomic mass
- Physical properties
- Chemical properties

He focused mainly on the compounds formed by elements with oxygen and hydrogen because oxygen and hydrogen are very reactive and form compounds with many elements.

### Mendeleev's method

Mendeleev wrote the properties of each element on separate cards. He arranged the cards in increasing order of atomic mass and grouped elements with similar properties together.

He found that similar properties repeated after regular intervals.

### Mendeleev's Periodic Law

The properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic masses.

### Groups and periods in Mendeleev's table

In Mendeleev's periodic table:

- Vertical columns were called groups.
- Horizontal rows were called periods.

### Meaning of `R` in Mendeleev's table

In formulas like `RH4` and `RO2`, the letter `R` represents any element of a group.

Example:

- Carbon hydride: `CH4`, represented as `RH4`
- Carbon oxide: `CO2`, represented as `RO2`

This helped Mendeleev compare chemical properties of elements.

---

## 6. Achievements of Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Mendeleev's periodic table was a very successful classification system for its time.

### 6.1 Correct placement of elements

Mendeleev sometimes placed an element with higher atomic mass before an element with lower atomic mass to keep elements with similar properties in the same group.

Example:

Cobalt has a slightly higher atomic mass than nickel, but cobalt was placed before nickel because this arrangement matched their chemical properties.

This showed that chemical properties were more important than strict atomic mass order.

### 6.2 Prediction of undiscovered elements

Mendeleev left blank spaces in his periodic table for elements that had not yet been discovered.

He predicted the properties of these unknown elements based on their positions.

He named them using the Sanskrit prefix `eka`.

Examples:

| Predicted Name | Later Discovered Element |
|---|---|
| Eka-boron | Scandium |
| Eka-aluminium | Gallium |
| Eka-silicon | Germanium |

### 6.3 Prediction of eka-aluminium

Mendeleev predicted the properties of eka-aluminium. Later, gallium was discovered and its properties closely matched Mendeleev's prediction.

| Property | Eka-aluminium | Gallium |
|---|---:|---:|
| Atomic mass | 68 | 69.7 |
| Formula of oxide | E2O3 | Ga2O3 |
| Formula of chloride | ECl3 | GaCl3 |

This confirmed the accuracy of Mendeleev's periodic table.

### 6.4 Accommodation of noble gases

Noble gases such as helium, neon, and argon were discovered later because they are very unreactive and present in very small amounts in the atmosphere.

When noble gases were discovered, they could be placed in a separate group without disturbing Mendeleev's existing arrangement.

This was another major achievement of Mendeleev's periodic table.

---

## 7. Limitations of Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Although Mendeleev's table was successful, it had some serious limitations.

### 7.1 Position of hydrogen

Hydrogen could not be assigned a fixed position.

Hydrogen resembles alkali metals because:

- It has one electron in its outermost shell.
- It can form compounds similar to alkali metals.

Examples:

| Hydrogen Compound | Sodium Compound |
|---|---|
| HCl | NaCl |
| H2O | Na2O |
| H2S | Na2S |

Hydrogen also resembles halogens because:

- It exists as a diatomic molecule, `H2`.
- It forms covalent compounds with metals and non-metals.
- It can gain one electron like halogens.

Therefore, hydrogen could be placed either with alkali metals or with halogens, but neither position was completely satisfactory.

### 7.2 Position of isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element having the same chemical properties but different atomic masses.

Mendeleev's table was based on atomic mass. Therefore, isotopes created a problem because different isotopes of the same element would need different positions.

Example:

Chlorine has isotopes:

- Cl-35
- Cl-37

Both have the same chemical properties but different atomic masses. They cannot be placed separately in the periodic table.

### 7.3 Irregular increase in atomic mass

Atomic mass does not increase regularly from one element to another. Because of this, it was difficult to predict how many elements could exist between two known elements, especially among heavier elements.

---

## 8. Modern Periodic Table

In 1913, Henry Moseley showed that atomic number is a more fundamental property of an element than atomic mass.

Atomic number represents the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. When elements are arranged in increasing order of atomic number, many problems of Mendeleev's table are solved.

### Modern Periodic Law

The properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers.

### Modern periodic table

The modern periodic table arranges elements in increasing order of atomic number.

This arrangement gives a more accurate prediction of element properties.

---

## 9. How Modern Periodic Table Solved Mendeleev's Problems

### 9.1 Cobalt and nickel problem

In Mendeleev's table, cobalt and nickel were arranged against the strict order of atomic mass.

In the modern periodic table, elements are arranged according to atomic number, so cobalt and nickel get correct positions.

### 9.2 Isotope problem

All isotopes of an element have the same atomic number because they have the same number of protons.

Therefore, all isotopes occupy the same position in the modern periodic table.

Example:

Cl-35 and Cl-37 both have atomic number 17, so both belong to the same position.

### 9.3 No element between hydrogen and helium

Atomic numbers increase by whole numbers. Hydrogen has atomic number 1 and helium has atomic number 2. Therefore, there can be no element with atomic number 1.5 between them.

---

## 10. Position of Elements in the Modern Periodic Table

The modern periodic table has:

- 18 vertical columns called groups
- 7 horizontal rows called periods

### Groups

Groups are vertical columns.

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons. Therefore, they show similar chemical properties.

Example:

Group 1 elements have one valence electron.

| Element | Electronic Configuration |
|---|---|
| Lithium | 2, 1 |
| Sodium | 2, 8, 1 |
| Potassium | 2, 8, 8, 1 |

All have one electron in the outermost shell, so they show similar chemical properties.

### Periods

Periods are horizontal rows.

Elements in the same period have the same number of occupied shells.

Example:

Second period elements have two shells.

| Element | Atomic Number | Electronic Configuration |
|---|---:|---|
| Lithium | 3 | 2, 1 |
| Beryllium | 4 | 2, 2 |
| Boron | 5 | 2, 3 |
| Carbon | 6 | 2, 4 |
| Nitrogen | 7 | 2, 5 |
| Oxygen | 8 | 2, 6 |
| Fluorine | 9 | 2, 7 |
| Neon | 10 | 2, 8 |

All these elements have two shells.

### Period number and shells

The period number tells the number of occupied shells.

| Period | Shells Filled | Number of Elements |
|---|---|---:|
| 1st period | K shell | 2 |
| 2nd period | L shell | 8 |
| 3rd period | M shell | 8 |
| 4th period | N shell starts filling | 18 |

### Maximum electrons in a shell

The maximum number of electrons in a shell is given by:

```text
2n²
```

where `n` is the shell number.

Examples:

| Shell | n | Maximum Electrons |
|---|---:|---:|
| K | 1 | 2 |
| L | 2 | 8 |
| M | 3 | 18 |

However, the outermost shell cannot have more than 8 electrons.

## 11. Valency Trends in the Modern Periodic Table

Valency is the combining capacity of an element. It depends on the number of valence electrons.

### Valency across a period

Across a period, valency first increases and then decreases.

Example for second period:

| Element | Electronic Configuration | Valency |
|---|---|---:|
| Li | 2, 1 | 1 |
| Be | 2, 2 | 2 |
| B | 2, 3 | 3 |
| C | 2, 4 | 4 |
| N | 2, 5 | 3 |
| O | 2, 6 | 2 |
| F | 2, 7 | 1 |
| Ne | 2, 8 | 0 |

### Valency down a group

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons, so their valency is generally the same.

Example:

Lithium, sodium, and potassium all have valency 1.

---

## 12. Atomic Size Trend

Atomic size means the radius of an atom.

It is the distance from the centre of the nucleus to the outermost shell of an isolated atom.

Atomic radius is commonly measured in picometre.

```text
1 pm = 10^-12 m
```

Hydrogen atom radius is about 37 pm.

### Atomic size across a period

Atomic size decreases from left to right across a period.

Reason:

- Nuclear charge increases.
- Electrons are added to the same shell.
- The increased nuclear charge pulls electrons closer to the nucleus.
- Therefore, atomic radius decreases.

Example for second period:

| Element | Atomic Radius (pm) |
|---|---:|
| Li | 152 |
| Be | 111 |
| B | 88 |
| C | 77 |
| N | 74 |
| O | 66 |

So, lithium is larger and oxygen is smaller among these.

### Atomic size down a group

Atomic size increases down a group.

Reason:

- A new shell is added at each step down the group.
- The distance between nucleus and outermost shell increases.
- Therefore, atomic size increases.

Example for group 1:

| Element | Atomic Radius (pm) |
|---|---:|
| Li | 152 |
| Na | 186 |
| K | 231 |
| Rb | 244 |
| Cs | 262 |

## 13. Metallic and Non-metallic Character

### Metallic character

Metallic character means the tendency of an atom to lose electrons and form positive ions.

Metals are electropositive because they lose electrons.

### Non-metallic character

Non-metallic character means the tendency of an atom to gain electrons and form negative ions.

Non-metals are electronegative because they gain electrons.

### Metallic character across a period

Metallic character decreases from left to right across a period.

Reason:

- Nuclear charge increases.
- The tendency to lose electrons decreases.
- Elements become less metallic and more non-metallic.

Example:

In the third period:

- Sodium and magnesium are metals.
- Silicon is a metalloid.
- Sulphur and chlorine are non-metals.

### Metallic character down a group

Metallic character increases down a group.

Reason:

- Atomic size increases.
- The outermost electron is farther from the nucleus.
- The tendency to lose electrons increases.

### Non-metallic character across a period

Non-metallic character increases from left to right across a period.

Reason:

- Nuclear charge increases.
- The tendency to gain electrons increases.

### Non-metallic character down a group

Non-metallic character decreases down a group.

Reason:

- Atomic size increases.
- The nucleus attracts incoming electrons less strongly.
- The tendency to gain electrons decreases.

---

## 14. Metalloids

Metalloids are elements that show properties of both metals and non-metals.

In the modern periodic table, a zig-zag line separates metals and non-metals.

Elements along this zig-zag line are metalloids.

Examples:

- Boron
- Silicon
- Germanium
- Arsenic
- Antimony
- Tellurium
- Polonium

---

## 15. Nature of Oxides

The position of an element in the periodic table also helps predict the nature of its oxide.

### Metallic oxides

Metals generally form basic oxides.

Example:

```text
Na2O
MgO
```

### Non-metallic oxides

Non-metals generally form acidic oxides.

Example:

```text
CO2
SO2
```

Across a period, the nature of oxides changes from basic to acidic.

## 18. Important Formula and Rules

### Average atomic mass in Dobereiner triad

```text
Atomic mass of middle element ≈ (Atomic mass of first element + Atomic mass of third element) / 2
```

### Maximum electrons in a shell

```text
Maximum electrons = 2n²
```

where `n` is the shell number.

Examples:

```text
K shell = 2 × 1² = 2
L shell = 2 × 2² = 8
M shell = 2 × 3² = 18
```

### Valency rule

For elements with 1 to 4 valence electrons:

```text
Valency = number of valence electrons
```

For elements with 5 to 8 valence electrons:

```text
Valency = 8 - number of valence electrons
```

## 21. Long Answer Questions

### Q1. Explain Dobereiner's triads with example and limitation.

Dobereiner arranged elements in groups of three called triads. The elements in a triad had similar chemical properties. The atomic mass of the middle element was approximately the average of the atomic masses of the other two elements.

Example:

Lithium, sodium, and potassium form a triad.

```text
Atomic mass of Li = 6.9
Atomic mass of K = 39.0

Average = (6.9 + 39.0) / 2 = 22.95
```

This is close to the atomic mass of sodium, which is 23.0.

Limitation:

Dobereiner could identify only a few triads, so this system could not classify all known elements.

### Q2. Explain Newlands' Law of Octaves and its limitations.

Newlands arranged elements in increasing order of atomic mass. He observed that the properties of every eighth element were similar to the first element. This was called Newlands' Law of Octaves.

Limitations:

- It worked only up to calcium.
- New elements discovered later did not fit the law.
- Newlands assumed only 56 elements existed.
- He placed some dissimilar elements together.
- He placed some similar elements far apart.
- He sometimes placed two elements in one position.

Therefore, Newlands' classification was not accepted.

### Q3. Explain the achievements of Mendeleev's periodic table.

Mendeleev's periodic table had several achievements:

1. It arranged elements with similar properties in the same group.
2. It gave importance to chemical properties.
3. It corrected the order of some elements based on properties rather than atomic mass.
4. It left gaps for undiscovered elements.
5. It predicted properties of unknown elements such as eka-aluminium, eka-boron, and eka-silicon.
6. Later discoveries of gallium, scandium, and germanium confirmed Mendeleev's predictions.
7. Noble gases were placed in a separate group without disturbing the table.

### Q4. Explain the limitations of Mendeleev's periodic table.

Mendeleev's table had three main limitations:

1. Hydrogen could not be given a fixed position because it resembles both alkali metals and halogens.
2. Isotopes could not be placed properly because isotopes have different atomic masses but same chemical properties.
3. Atomic mass did not increase regularly, so it was difficult to predict the number of elements between two known elements.

### Q5. Explain how the modern periodic table solved Mendeleev's problems.

The modern periodic table is based on atomic number instead of atomic mass.

It solved Mendeleev's problems in these ways:

- Isotopes occupy the same position because they have the same atomic number.
- Elements like cobalt and nickel get correct positions according to atomic number.
- Atomic number increases by one from one element to the next, so there is no uncertainty about missing elements.
- Properties repeat periodically with atomic number.

### Q6. Explain trends in atomic size in the modern periodic table.

Across a period:

Atomic size decreases from left to right. This happens because nuclear charge increases while electrons are added to the same shell. The nucleus pulls electrons more strongly, so the atom becomes smaller.

Down a group:

Atomic size increases from top to bottom. This happens because a new shell is added at each step. The outermost electron moves farther from the nucleus, so the atom becomes larger.

### Q7. Explain trends in metallic and non-metallic character.

Metallic character means the tendency to lose electrons.

Across a period, metallic character decreases because the tendency to lose electrons decreases.

Down a group, metallic character increases because atomic size increases and outermost electrons can be lost more easily.

Non-metallic character means the tendency to gain electrons.

Across a period, non-metallic character increases because the nuclear attraction for electrons increases.

Down a group, non-metallic character decreases because atomic size increases and the attraction for incoming electrons decreases.

## 22. Common Confusions

### Hydrogen's position

Hydrogen is placed at the top of group 1 in many periodic tables, but its position is still considered special because it resembles both alkali metals and halogens.

### Atomic mass vs atomic number

Mendeleev's table was based on atomic mass. The modern table is based on atomic number.

Atomic number is more reliable because it directly represents the number of protons in the atom.

### Group vs period

Group means vertical column.

Period means horizontal row.

### Same group vs same period

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons.

Elements in the same period have the same number of shells.
