# Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

## Chapter Overview

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances change into new substances with different properties. In daily life, many changes are chemical reactions, such as milk turning sour, iron rusting, grapes fermenting, food being cooked, food being digested, and respiration.

A chemical reaction may be identified by one or more observable changes:

- Change in state
- Change in colour
- Evolution of gas
- Change in temperature
- Formation of a precipitate
- Emission or absorption of heat, light, or electricity

In a chemical reaction:

- Substances that take part in the reaction are called **reactants**.
- Substances formed after the reaction are called **products**.

Example:

```txt
Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
```

Here, magnesium and oxygen are reactants. Magnesium oxide is the product.

---

## 1. Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae.

### 1.1 Word Equation

A word equation represents a chemical reaction using names of substances.

Example:

```txt
Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
```

Rules:

- Reactants are written on the left-hand side (LHS).
- Products are written on the right-hand side (RHS).
- An arrow `→` separates reactants and products.
- The arrow shows the direction of the reaction.
- The plus sign `+` separates multiple reactants or products.

### 1.2 Skeletal Chemical Equation

A skeletal chemical equation uses chemical formulae but may not be balanced.

Example:

```txt
Mg + O2 → MgO
```

This equation is not balanced because the number of oxygen atoms is not equal on both sides.

### 1.3 Balanced Chemical Equation

A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides.

Balanced equation for magnesium burning in oxygen:

```txt
2Mg + O2 → 2MgO
```

Balancing is necessary because of the **law of conservation of mass**.

### Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Therefore, the total mass of reactants must be equal to the total mass of products.

---

## 2. Balancing Chemical Equations

### Example: Iron reacts with steam

Unbalanced equation:

```txt
Fe + H2O → Fe3O4 + H2
```

Balanced equation:

```txt
3Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2
```

With physical states:

```txt
3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)
```

### Steps for Balancing by Hit-and-Trial Method

1. Write the skeletal equation.
2. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
3. Start with the compound containing the maximum number of atoms.
4. Balance one element at a time.
5. Do not change chemical formulae.
6. Only change coefficients.
7. Recount atoms on both sides.
8. Add physical state symbols if required.

### Physical State Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `(s)` | Solid |
| `(l)` | Liquid |
| `(g)` | Gas |
| `(aq)` | Aqueous solution |

Example:

```txt
3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)
```

### Reaction Conditions

Sometimes reaction conditions are written above or below the arrow.

Examples:

```txt
CO(g) + 2H2(g) --340 atm--> CH3OH(l)
```

Photosynthesis:

```txt
6CO2(aq) + 12H2O(l) --sunlight/chlorophyll--> C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) + 6H2O(l)
```

## 3. Important Activity-Based Concepts

### Activity 1.1: Burning of Magnesium Ribbon

Observation:

- Magnesium ribbon burns with a dazzling white flame.
- A white powder is formed.
- The white powder is magnesium oxide.

Equation:

```txt
2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s)
```

Concept:

- Magnesium combines with oxygen.
- Magnesium oxide is formed.
- This is a combination reaction.
- Magnesium is oxidised.

Important point:

Magnesium ribbon is cleaned with sandpaper before burning because it has a layer of magnesium oxide on its surface. Cleaning removes the oxide layer and allows magnesium to burn properly.

### Activity 1.2: Lead Nitrate and Potassium Iodide

When lead nitrate solution is mixed with potassium iodide solution, a yellow precipitate of lead iodide is formed.

Equation:

```txt
Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI2(s) + 2KNO3(aq)
```

Concept:

- This is a double displacement reaction.
- It is also a precipitation reaction.

### Activity 1.3: Zinc and Dilute Acid

Zinc reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid or dilute sulphuric acid and produces hydrogen gas.

With dilute sulphuric acid:

```txt
Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g)
```

With dilute hydrochloric acid:

```txt
Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)
```

Observations:

- Bubbles of hydrogen gas are produced.
- Temperature may rise.
- The reaction mixture becomes warm.

## 4. Types of Chemical Reactions

## 4.1 Combination Reaction

A combination reaction is a reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product.

General form:

```txt
A + B → AB
```

Examples:

### Calcium oxide reacts with water

```txt
CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq)
```

Calcium oxide is also called quicklime. Calcium hydroxide is also called slaked lime.

Observation:

- A large amount of heat is produced.
- The reaction mixture becomes hot.

### Burning of carbon

```txt
C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)
```

### Formation of water

```txt
2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)
```

### Whitewashing reaction

Calcium hydroxide reacts slowly with carbon dioxide in air to form calcium carbonate.

```txt
Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)
```

Calcium carbonate gives a shiny finish to walls after whitewashing. Marble also has the chemical formula `CaCO3`.

---

## 4.2 Exothermic Reaction

An exothermic reaction is a reaction in which heat is released along with the formation of products.

Examples:

### Burning of natural gas

```txt
CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g) + Energy
```

### Respiration

Respiration is an exothermic reaction because glucose is broken down in the presence of oxygen to release energy.

```txt
C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + Energy
```

### Decomposition of vegetable matter into compost

The decomposition of plant waste into compost is also an exothermic process.

---

## 4.3 Decomposition Reaction

A decomposition reaction is a reaction in which a single reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products.

General form:

```txt
AB → A + B
```

Decomposition reactions are opposite of combination reactions.

### Thermal Decomposition

Thermal decomposition occurs when a compound decomposes due to heat.

#### Heating ferrous sulphate crystals

Ferrous sulphate crystals lose water and then decompose into ferric oxide, sulphur dioxide, and sulphur trioxide.

```txt
2FeSO4(s) --heat--> Fe2O3(s) + SO2(g) + SO3(g)
```

Observations:

- Green ferrous sulphate crystals change colour.
- A characteristic smell of burning sulphur is observed.
- Ferric oxide is solid.
- Sulphur dioxide and sulphur trioxide are gases.

#### Heating calcium carbonate

```txt
CaCO3(s) --heat--> CaO(s) + CO2(g)
```

Calcium carbonate is limestone. Calcium oxide is quicklime. This reaction is used in industries, especially in cement manufacturing.

#### Heating lead nitrate

```txt
2Pb(NO3)2(s) --heat--> 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)
```

Observation:

- Brown fumes of nitrogen dioxide are evolved.

### Electrolytic Decomposition

Electrolytic decomposition occurs when a compound decomposes due to electricity.

Example: Electrolysis of water

```txt
2H2O(l) --electricity--> 2H2(g) + O2(g)
```

Observation:

- Hydrogen gas is collected at the cathode.
- Oxygen gas is collected at the anode.
- The volume of hydrogen gas is twice the volume of oxygen gas.

Reason:

Water contains hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio 2:1.

### Photolytic Decomposition

Photolytic decomposition occurs when a compound decomposes due to light.

Example: Silver chloride in sunlight

```txt
2AgCl(s) --sunlight--> 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)
```

Observation:

- White silver chloride turns grey in sunlight because silver metal is formed.

Example: Silver bromide in sunlight

```txt
2AgBr(s) --sunlight--> 2Ag(s) + Br2(g)
```

Use:

These reactions are used in black-and-white photography.

---

## 4.4 Endothermic Reaction

An endothermic reaction is a reaction in which energy is absorbed.

In decomposition reactions, energy is often required in the form of heat, light, or electricity.

Examples:

- Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate
- Electrolysis of water
- Photolytic decomposition of silver chloride

Another example:

When barium hydroxide reacts with ammonium chloride, heat is absorbed and the test tube becomes cold.

---

## 4.5 Displacement Reaction

A displacement reaction is a reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

General form:

```txt
A + BC → AC + B
```

Example: Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution

```txt
Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
```

Observations:

- Iron nail becomes brown due to copper deposition.
- Blue colour of copper sulphate solution fades.
- Ferrous sulphate is formed.

Other examples:

```txt
Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
```

```txt
Pb(s) + CuCl2(aq) → PbCl2(aq) + Cu(s)
```

Reason:

Zinc, iron, and lead are more reactive than copper. Therefore, they can displace copper from its compounds.

---

## 4.6 Double Displacement Reaction

A double displacement reaction is a reaction in which ions are exchanged between two reactants to form new compounds.

General form:

```txt
AB + CD → AD + CB
```

Example:

```txt
Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
```

Here:

- Sodium sulphate reacts with barium chloride.
- Barium sulphate is formed as a white precipitate.
- Sodium chloride remains in solution.

---

## 4.7 Precipitation Reaction

A precipitation reaction is a reaction in which an insoluble solid is formed. This insoluble solid is called a precipitate.

Example:

```txt
Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
```

In this reaction, `BaSO4` is the white precipitate.

Another example:

```txt
Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI2(s) + 2KNO3(aq)
```

In this reaction, `PbI2` is the yellow precipitate.

---

## 4.8 Oxidation and Reduction

### Oxidation

Oxidation is a process in which:

- Oxygen is added to a substance, or
- Hydrogen is removed from a substance.

Example:

```txt
2Cu + O2 --heat--> 2CuO
```

Copper gains oxygen and forms copper oxide. Therefore, copper is oxidised.

### Reduction

Reduction is a process in which:

- Oxygen is removed from a substance, or
- Hydrogen is added to a substance.

Example:

```txt
CuO + H2 --heat--> Cu + H2O
```

Copper oxide loses oxygen and becomes copper. Therefore, copper oxide is reduced.

Hydrogen gains oxygen and forms water. Therefore, hydrogen is oxidised.

### Redox Reaction

A reaction in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously is called a redox reaction.

Example:

```txt
CuO + H2 → Cu + H2O
```

In this reaction:

- `CuO` is reduced to `Cu`.
- `H2` is oxidised to `H2O`.

Other examples:

```txt
ZnO + C → Zn + CO
```

In this reaction:

- `ZnO` is reduced to `Zn`.
- `C` is oxidised to `CO`.

```txt
MnO2 + 4HCl → MnCl2 + 2H2O + Cl2
```

In this reaction:

- `MnO2` is reduced to `MnCl2`.
- `HCl` is oxidised to `Cl2`.

## 5. Effects of Oxidation Reactions in Daily Life

## 5.1 Corrosion

Corrosion is the process in which metals are slowly destroyed by the action of substances such as oxygen, moisture, acids, or other chemicals present in the environment.

Examples:

- Iron rusts and forms a reddish-brown layer.
- Silver gets a black coating.
- Copper gets a green coating.

Rusting of iron is a common example of corrosion.

Effects of corrosion:

- Damage to car bodies
- Damage to bridges
- Damage to iron railings
- Damage to ships
- Damage to metal structures

Corrosion of iron is a serious problem because it causes huge economic loss.

### Why do we paint iron objects?

Iron objects are painted to prevent contact between iron and oxygen/moisture. This prevents rusting.

---

## 5.2 Rancidity

Rancidity is the process in which oils and fats get oxidised and develop an unpleasant smell and taste.

Examples:

- Old oily food smells bad.
- Fat-rich food becomes stale.
- Chips and fried snacks can become rancid if exposed to air for a long time.

Methods to prevent rancidity:

- Add antioxidants.
- Store food in airtight containers.
- Keep food away from oxygen.
- Use nitrogen gas packaging for chips.
- Refrigerate oily and fatty foods.

### Why are chips packets filled with nitrogen?

Chips packets are filled with nitrogen because nitrogen is less reactive than oxygen. It prevents oxidation of oils and fats and delays rancidity.

## 9. Exam-Focused Points

- Magnesium ribbon should be cleaned before burning to remove the oxide layer.
- A chemical equation must be balanced because mass is conserved.
- Physical states make a chemical equation more informative.
- Combination reactions produce a single product.
- Decomposition reactions break one reactant into multiple products.
- Thermal decomposition uses heat.
- Electrolytic decomposition uses electricity.
- Photolytic decomposition uses light.
- In electrolysis of water, hydrogen and oxygen are produced in the ratio 2:1 by volume.
- A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.
- A double displacement reaction involves exchange of ions.
- A precipitation reaction produces an insoluble solid.
- Oxidation and reduction occur together in redox reactions.
- Rusting, corrosion, and rancidity are effects of oxidation.
- Rancidity is prevented by antioxidants, airtight packing, refrigeration, and nitrogen flushing.

## 13. Important Long Answer Topics

### 13.1 Why is a chemical equation balanced?

A chemical equation is balanced because atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of the equation. This follows the law of conservation of mass.

### 13.2 Difference between Combination and Decomposition Reactions

| Combination Reaction | Decomposition Reaction |
|---|---|
| Two or more reactants form one product. | One reactant breaks into two or more products. |
| General form: `A + B → AB` | General form: `AB → A + B` |
| Example: `CaO + H2O → Ca(OH)2` | Example: `CaCO3 → CaO + CO2` |

### 13.3 Difference between Displacement and Double Displacement Reactions

| Displacement Reaction | Double Displacement Reaction |
|---|---|
| One element displaces another element from its compound. | Ions are exchanged between two compounds. |
| General form: `A + BC → AC + B` | General form: `AB + CD → AD + CB` |
| Example: `Fe + CuSO4 → FeSO4 + Cu` | Example: `Na2SO4 + BaCl2 → BaSO4 + 2NaCl` |

### 13.4 Difference between Oxidation and Reduction

| Oxidation | Reduction |
|---|---|
| Addition of oxygen | Removal of oxygen |
| Removal of hydrogen | Addition of hydrogen |
| Example: `2Cu + O2 → 2CuO` | Example: `CuO + H2 → Cu + H2O` |

### 13.5 Why is respiration called an exothermic reaction?

Respiration is called an exothermic reaction because glucose is broken down in the presence of oxygen and energy is released.

```txt
C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy
```

### 13.6 Why are oils and fatty foods flushed with nitrogen?

Oils and fatty foods are flushed with nitrogen to prevent oxidation. Nitrogen is less reactive than oxygen, so it slows down rancidity and keeps food fresh for a longer time.
