# Chapter 4: Carbon and Its Compounds

## Chapter Overview

Carbon is one of the most important elements for living organisms and daily-use materials. Food, clothes, medicines, books, fuels, plastics, oils, soaps, detergents and all living structures contain carbon compounds in some form.

Carbon is present in small amounts in nature, but it forms a very large number of compounds because of two main properties:

1. **Tetravalency** — carbon has valency 4 and can form four covalent bonds.
2. **Catenation** — carbon can form long chains, branched chains and ring structures by bonding with other carbon atoms.

This chapter explains covalent bonding, carbon compounds, hydrocarbons, functional groups, homologous series, nomenclature, chemical reactions of carbon compounds, ethanol, ethanoic acid, soaps and detergents.

---

## 1. Bonding in Carbon: Covalent Bond

### Why carbon does not usually form ionic bonds

Carbon has atomic number 6.

Electronic configuration:

```txt
Carbon = 2, 4
```

Carbon has 4 electrons in its outermost shell. To achieve a stable noble gas configuration, carbon would need either:

1. To gain 4 electrons and form C⁴⁻, or
2. To lose 4 electrons and form C⁴⁺.

Both processes are difficult:

- Gaining 4 electrons is difficult because the nucleus with 6 protons cannot easily hold 10 electrons strongly.
- Losing 4 electrons requires a very large amount of energy.

So carbon completes its octet by **sharing electrons** with other atoms.

### Covalent Bond

A bond formed by sharing electron pairs between two atoms is called a **covalent bond**.

Covalent compounds usually have:

- Low melting points.
- Low boiling points.
- Poor electrical conductivity.
- Molecules formed by sharing electrons, not by ion formation.

## 2. Electron Sharing in Simple Molecules

### Hydrogen Molecule: H₂

Hydrogen has one electron and needs one more electron to complete its K shell.

Two hydrogen atoms share one pair of electrons.

```txt
H + H → H₂
H—H
```

This is a **single covalent bond**.

### Chlorine Molecule: Cl₂

Each chlorine atom has 7 valence electrons and needs one more electron.

```txt
Cl—Cl
```

The two chlorine atoms share one pair of electrons and form a single covalent bond.

### Oxygen Molecule: O₂

Oxygen has 6 valence electrons and needs 2 more electrons.

Two oxygen atoms share two pairs of electrons.

```txt
O=O
```

This is a **double covalent bond**.

### Nitrogen Molecule: N₂

Nitrogen has 5 valence electrons and needs 3 more electrons.

Two nitrogen atoms share three pairs of electrons.

```txt
N≡N
```

This is a **triple covalent bond**.

### Water Molecule: H₂O

Oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogen atoms.

```txt
H—O—H
```

Water contains two single covalent bonds.

### Methane Molecule: CH₄

Methane is one of the simplest carbon compounds.

Carbon shares its four valence electrons with four hydrogen atoms.

```txt
      H
      |
H — C — H
      |
      H
```

Methane contains four single covalent bonds.

## 3. Allotropes of Carbon

Carbon exists in different physical forms. These different forms of the same element are called **allotropes**.

Important allotropes of carbon:

1. Diamond
2. Graphite
3. Fullerene

### Diamond

In diamond:

- Each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms.
- A strong three-dimensional network is formed.
- Diamond is extremely hard.
- It has a very high melting point.
- It does not conduct electricity.

Uses:

- Cutting tools.
- Jewellery.
- Drilling instruments.

### Graphite

In graphite:

- Each carbon atom is bonded to three other carbon atoms.
- Hexagonal layers are formed.
- Layers can slide over each other.
- Graphite is soft and slippery.
- Graphite conducts electricity.

Uses:

- Pencil lead.
- Lubricant.
- Electrodes.

### Fullerene

Fullerene is another allotrope of carbon.

The most common fullerene is **C₆₀**, where carbon atoms are arranged like a football.

## 4. Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon forms a huge number of compounds because of:

1. Catenation
2. Tetravalency

### 4.1 Catenation

The ability of carbon to form bonds with other carbon atoms is called **catenation**.

Carbon can form:

- Long straight chains.
- Branched chains.
- Ring structures.
- Single, double and triple bonds.

Example straight chain:

```txt
C—C—C—C
```

Example branched chain:

```txt
    C
    |
C—C—C
```

Example ring:

```txt
Cyclic carbon structure
```

### 4.2 Tetravalency

Carbon has valency 4. So it can bond with:

- Carbon
- Hydrogen
- Oxygen
- Nitrogen
- Sulphur
- Chlorine
- Bromine

Because carbon is small in size, it forms strong covalent bonds with these elements.

## 5. Hydrocarbons

Compounds made only of carbon and hydrogen are called **hydrocarbons**.

Hydrocarbons are of three main types:

1. Alkanes
2. Alkenes
3. Alkynes

## 6. Saturated and Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

### Saturated Compounds

Carbon compounds containing only single bonds between carbon atoms are called **saturated compounds**.

Example:

```txt
Ethane: C₂H₆
CH₃—CH₃
```

Saturated hydrocarbons are called **alkanes**.

General formula of alkanes:

```txt
CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
```

Examples:

| Carbon atoms | Name | Formula |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Methane | CH₄ |
| 2 | Ethane | C₂H₆ |
| 3 | Propane | C₃H₈ |
| 4 | Butane | C₄H₁₀ |
| 5 | Pentane | C₅H₁₂ |
| 6 | Hexane | C₆H₁₄ |

### Unsaturated Compounds

Carbon compounds containing double or triple bonds between carbon atoms are called **unsaturated compounds**.

### Alkenes

Hydrocarbons with at least one double bond are called **alkenes**.

Example:

```txt
Ethene: C₂H₄
CH₂=CH₂
```

General formula:

```txt
CₙH₂ₙ
```

### Alkynes

Hydrocarbons with at least one triple bond are called **alkynes**.

Example:

```txt
Ethyne: C₂H₂
HC≡CH
```

General formula:

```txt
CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
```

## 7. Chains, Branches and Rings

Carbon compounds may have:

1. Straight chain structure
2. Branched chain structure
3. Ring structure

### Butane Example

Butane has molecular formula:

```txt
C₄H₁₀
```

It can have two different structures:

```txt
Straight chain:
CH₃—CH₂—CH₂—CH₃

Branched chain:
      CH₃
      |
CH₃—CH—CH₃
```

These compounds have the same molecular formula but different structures. This is called **structural isomerism**.

### Cyclohexane

Cyclohexane is a ring compound.

Formula:

```txt
C₆H₁₂
```

### Benzene

Benzene is also a cyclic hydrocarbon.

Formula:

```txt
C₆H₆
```

## 8. Functional Groups

An atom or group of atoms that gives characteristic properties to a carbon compound is called a **functional group**.

Functional groups replace one or more hydrogen atoms in a hydrocarbon chain.

### Important Functional Groups

| Functional Group | Formula | Compound Type | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Halo group | —Cl, —Br | Chloro/Bromo compounds | Chloropropane |
| Alcohol group | —OH | Alcohol | Ethanol |
| Aldehyde group | —CHO | Aldehyde | Ethanal |
| Ketone group | —CO— | Ketone | Propanone |
| Carboxylic acid group | —COOH | Carboxylic acid | Ethanoic acid |

### Heteroatoms

Atoms other than carbon and hydrogen present in carbon compounds are called **heteroatoms**.

Examples:

- Oxygen
- Nitrogen
- Sulphur
- Chlorine
- Bromine

---

## 9. Homologous Series

A series of carbon compounds in which the same functional group substitutes hydrogen in a carbon chain is called a **homologous series**.

### Features of a Homologous Series

1. Members have the same functional group.
2. Members have similar chemical properties.
3. Successive members differ by a —CH₂— unit.
4. Molecular mass differs by 14 u between successive members.
5. Physical properties such as melting point and boiling point change gradually.

### Example: Alcohol Homologous Series

| Name | Formula |
|---|---|
| Methanol | CH₃OH |
| Ethanol | C₂H₅OH |
| Propanol | C₃H₇OH |
| Butanol | C₄H₉OH |

Each successive compound differs by:

```txt
—CH₂—
```

## 12. Combustion

Carbon and most carbon compounds burn in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water, heat and light.

### General combustion examples

```txt
C + O₂ → CO₂ + heat + light
```

```txt
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + heat + light
```

```txt
CH₃CH₂OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O + heat + light
```

### Flame Type

| Compound / Condition | Flame |
|---|---|
| Saturated hydrocarbons with sufficient oxygen | Clean blue flame |
| Unsaturated hydrocarbons | Yellow sooty flame |
| Limited oxygen supply | Sooty flame |
| Proper oxygen supply in LPG stove | Blue flame |

If the bottom of a cooking vessel becomes black, it means the fuel is not burning completely and air holes may be blocked.

### Environmental Impact

Coal and petroleum contain small amounts of nitrogen and sulphur. Their combustion produces oxides of nitrogen and sulphur, which are pollutants.

---

## 13. Oxidation

Oxidation is the process in which oxygen is added to a substance or hydrogen is removed from it.

Some substances can add oxygen to other substances. Such substances are called **oxidising agents**.

Common oxidising agents:

- Alkaline potassium permanganate
- Acidified potassium dichromate

### Oxidation of Ethanol

Ethanol can be oxidised to ethanoic acid.

```txt
CH₃CH₂OH + [O] → CH₃COOH
```

In school-level reactions, this is shown using alkaline KMnO₄ or acidified K₂Cr₂O₇.

```txt
Ethanol → Ethanoic acid
```

This is an oxidation reaction because oxygen is added to ethanol.

---

## 14. Addition Reaction

Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts such as nickel or palladium to form saturated hydrocarbons.

### Hydrogenation

```txt
Unsaturated hydrocarbon + H₂ → Saturated hydrocarbon
```

Example:

```txt
Vegetable oil + H₂ --Ni--> Vanaspati ghee
```

This process is called **hydrogenation**.

### Industrial Use

Hydrogenation is used in converting vegetable oils into solid fats.

Vegetable oils generally contain unsaturated carbon chains. Animal fats mostly contain saturated carbon chains.

---

## 15. Substitution Reaction

A reaction in which one atom or group of atoms replaces another atom or group of atoms is called a **substitution reaction**.

Saturated hydrocarbons are generally less reactive, but in the presence of sunlight chlorine can substitute hydrogen atoms.

Example:

```txt
CH₄ + Cl₂ --sunlight--> CH₃Cl + HCl
```

This is a substitution reaction because chlorine replaces hydrogen in methane.

## 17. Ethanol

### Formula

```txt
C₂H₅OH
```

or

```txt
CH₃CH₂OH
```

### Common Name

Ethanol is commonly called **alcohol**.

### Physical Properties

- Liquid at room temperature.
- Mixes with water in all proportions.
- Good solvent.
- Used in medicines such as tincture iodine, cough syrups and tonics.

### Uses of Ethanol

1. Solvent in medicines.
2. Component of alcoholic beverages.
3. Fuel when blended with petrol.
4. Industrial solvent.

### Ethanol and Sodium

Ethanol reacts with sodium to form sodium ethoxide and hydrogen gas.

```txt
2Na + 2CH₃CH₂OH → 2CH₃CH₂ONa + H₂
```

Products:

- Sodium ethoxide
- Hydrogen gas

Hydrogen gas can be tested by bringing a burning splint near it. It burns with a pop sound.

### Dehydration of Ethanol

When ethanol is heated with excess concentrated sulphuric acid at 443 K, it loses water and forms ethene.

```txt
CH₃CH₂OH --conc. H₂SO₄, 443 K--> CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O
```

Concentrated sulphuric acid acts as a dehydrating agent.

### Harmful Effects of Alcohol

Excess ethanol consumption slows metabolic processes and affects the central nervous system. It may cause lack of coordination, mental confusion, drowsiness and poor judgement.

Methanol is highly poisonous. Even a small amount can be fatal or cause blindness because it gets converted into toxic methanal in the body.

Industrial alcohol is often made unfit for drinking by adding poisonous substances such as methanol. This is called **denatured alcohol**.

## 18. Ethanoic Acid

### Formula

```txt
CH₃COOH
```

### Common Name

Ethanoic acid is commonly called **acetic acid**.

### Vinegar

A 3–4% solution of acetic acid in water is called **vinegar**.

Uses of vinegar:

- Food preservative.
- Pickle preservation.
- Flavouring agent.

### Glacial Acetic Acid

Pure ethanoic acid freezes at about 290 K. Because it freezes in cold weather, pure ethanoic acid is called **glacial acetic acid**.

### Ethanoic Acid as a Weak Acid

Ethanoic acid belongs to the carboxylic acid group.

It is a weak acid compared to mineral acids like hydrochloric acid because it ionises only partially in water.

---

## 19. Reactions of Ethanoic Acid

### 19.1 Esterification Reaction

Ethanoic acid reacts with ethanol in the presence of an acid catalyst to form an ester.

```txt
CH₃COOH + CH₃CH₂OH --acid--> CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ + H₂O
```

Products:

- Ester
- Water

Esters usually have a sweet smell and are used in perfumes and flavouring agents.

### 19.2 Saponification

Esters react with acids or bases to give back alcohol and carboxylic acid.

When ester reacts with sodium hydroxide, alcohol and sodium salt of carboxylic acid are formed. This reaction is called **saponification** because it is used in soap making.

```txt
CH₃COOC₂H₅ + NaOH → C₂H₅OH + CH₃COONa
```

### 19.3 Reaction with Base

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium hydroxide to form sodium ethanoate and water.

```txt
NaOH + CH₃COOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
```

This is a neutralisation reaction.

### 19.4 Reaction with Carbonates

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium carbonate to form sodium ethanoate, carbon dioxide and water.

```txt
2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
```

### 19.5 Reaction with Hydrogen Carbonates

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium hydrogen carbonate to form sodium ethanoate, carbon dioxide and water.

```txt
CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
```

Carbon dioxide gas can be tested by passing it through lime water. Lime water turns milky.

## 20. Soaps and Detergents

### Soap

Soap molecules are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.

Soap molecule has two parts:

1. Hydrophilic head
2. Hydrophobic tail

### Hydrophilic Head

The ionic end of soap dissolves in water.

```txt
Water-loving part
```

### Hydrophobic Tail

The long hydrocarbon chain dissolves in oil or grease.

```txt
Water-hating / oil-loving part
```

### Cleaning Action of Soap

Most dirt is oily and does not dissolve in water.

Soap molecules arrange themselves around oily dirt:

- Hydrophobic tails go into oil/grease.
- Hydrophilic heads remain in water.

This forms a structure called a **micelle**.

Inside the micelle, oily dirt is trapped. The micelle remains suspended in water and can be washed away.

### Micelle

A cluster of soap molecules arranged around oily dirt is called a **micelle**.

Micelles help remove oily dirt from clothes and skin.

Soap solution looks cloudy because micelles scatter light.

---

## 21. Soap in Hard Water

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium salts.

Soap reacts with calcium and magnesium ions to form an insoluble substance called scum.

Problems with soap in hard water:

1. Less foam is produced.
2. More soap is needed.
3. Scum forms.
4. Cleaning becomes less effective.

### Detergents

Detergents are usually ammonium or sulphonate salts of long-chain compounds.

They do not form insoluble scum with calcium and magnesium ions.

So detergents are effective even in hard water.

Uses of detergents:

- Shampoos
- Laundry powders
- Cleaning liquids

## Important Definitions

### Carbon Compound

A compound containing carbon is called a carbon compound.

### Covalent Bond

A bond formed by sharing electron pairs between atoms is called a covalent bond.

### Allotropy

The existence of an element in different physical forms is called allotropy.

### Catenation

The ability of an element to form bonds with atoms of the same element is called catenation.

### Tetravalency

The valency of carbon is 4. This property is called tetravalency.

### Hydrocarbon

A compound made only of carbon and hydrogen is called a hydrocarbon.

### Saturated Compound

A carbon compound containing only single bonds between carbon atoms is called a saturated compound.

### Unsaturated Compound

A carbon compound containing double or triple bonds between carbon atoms is called an unsaturated compound.

### Alkane

A saturated hydrocarbon is called an alkane.

### Alkene

An unsaturated hydrocarbon containing at least one double bond is called an alkene.

### Alkyne

An unsaturated hydrocarbon containing at least one triple bond is called an alkyne.

### Structural Isomerism

The phenomenon in which compounds have the same molecular formula but different structures is called structural isomerism.

### Functional Group

An atom or group of atoms that gives characteristic properties to a carbon compound is called a functional group.

### Homologous Series

A series of carbon compounds with the same functional group and similar chemical properties is called a homologous series.

### Combustion

The burning of a substance in oxygen to produce heat and light is called combustion.

### Oxidising Agent

A substance that adds oxygen to another substance or removes hydrogen from it is called an oxidising agent.

### Addition Reaction

A reaction in which atoms are added to an unsaturated compound is called an addition reaction.

### Substitution Reaction

A reaction in which one atom or group replaces another atom or group is called a substitution reaction.

### Esterification

The reaction between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid to form an ester is called esterification.

### Saponification

The reaction of an ester with a base to form alcohol and the salt of carboxylic acid is called saponification.

### Micelle

A cluster of soap molecules formed around oily dirt in water is called a micelle.

### Detergent

A cleaning agent that works even in hard water and does not form scum is called a detergent.

## Common Exam Questions with Answer Hints

### 1. Why does carbon form covalent compounds?

Carbon has four valence electrons. Losing or gaining four electrons is difficult, so carbon completes its octet by sharing electrons and forming covalent bonds.

### 2. What are the two properties of carbon responsible for the large number of carbon compounds?

The two properties are:

1. Tetravalency
2. Catenation

### 3. Why is diamond hard while graphite is soft?

Diamond has a strong three-dimensional network of carbon atoms. Graphite has layered hexagonal sheets that can slide over one another, making it soft and slippery.

### 4. Why does graphite conduct electricity?

In graphite, each carbon atom bonds with three other carbon atoms and one electron remains relatively free to move. These mobile electrons make graphite a conductor of electricity.

### 5. What is a homologous series?

A homologous series is a series of carbon compounds having the same functional group and similar chemical properties, where successive members differ by a —CH₂— unit.

### 6. What is the difference between saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons?

Saturated hydrocarbons contain only single bonds between carbon atoms. Unsaturated hydrocarbons contain at least one double or triple bond.

### 7. What is hydrogenation?

Hydrogenation is the addition of hydrogen to unsaturated hydrocarbons in the presence of catalysts like nickel or palladium to form saturated hydrocarbons.

### 8. Why is ethanol converted into ethanoic acid called oxidation?

Because oxygen is added to ethanol during its conversion into ethanoic acid.

### 9. How does ethanol react with sodium?

Ethanol reacts with sodium to form sodium ethoxide and hydrogen gas.

```txt
2Na + 2CH₃CH₂OH → 2CH₃CH₂ONa + H₂
```

### 10. What is esterification?

Esterification is the reaction between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid in the presence of an acid catalyst to form an ester and water.

### 11. How can ethanoic acid be distinguished from ethanol?

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium carbonate or sodium hydrogen carbonate to produce carbon dioxide gas. Ethanol does not give this reaction.

### 12. Why does soap form micelles in water?

Soap molecules have a water-loving ionic head and an oil-loving hydrocarbon tail. In water, these molecules arrange around oily dirt to form micelles.

### 13. Why is soap ineffective in hard water?

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium ions. Soap reacts with these ions to form insoluble scum, reducing foam and cleaning ability.

### 14. Why are detergents effective in hard water?

Detergents do not form insoluble precipitates with calcium and magnesium ions, so they remain effective in hard water.

### 15. Why is the bottom of a cooking vessel blackened sometimes?

It means incomplete combustion is taking place due to insufficient oxygen supply. Fuel is not burning completely and soot is deposited.
