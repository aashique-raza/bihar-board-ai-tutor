# Chapter 5: Sources of Energy

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains why human society needs usable energy sources even though energy is conserved in physical and chemical processes. Energy is not destroyed, but after use it often gets converted into less useful forms and disperses into the surroundings. Therefore, practical energy sources are needed again and again for cooking, transport, lighting, industries, electricity generation, communication, and daily life.

The chapter compares conventional and non-conventional sources of energy, explains how electricity is generated from different sources, and discusses environmental concerns connected with energy use.

---

## Core Idea: Why Do We Need Energy Sources?

According to the law of conservation of energy, energy can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transformed from one form to another.

However, after energy is used, it usually becomes less available for useful work.

Examples:

- When a candle burns, the chemical energy of wax changes into heat and light.
- When hot water cools down, its heat spreads into the surroundings.
- When a plate falls, its potential energy changes into sound, heat, and other less useful forms.

So, even though total energy is conserved, usable energy becomes unavailable after use. This is why we need continuous energy sources.

# Conventional Sources of Energy

Conventional sources are energy sources that have been used traditionally for a long time.

Major conventional sources:

1. Fossil fuels
2. Thermal power plants
3. Hydroelectric power plants
4. Biomass
5. Wind energy

---

## Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels are formed from the remains of ancient plants and animals over millions of years.

Examples:

- Coal
- Petroleum
- Natural gas

### Uses of Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels are used for:

- Cooking
- Running vehicles
- Producing electricity
- Industrial heating
- Powering machines

### Disadvantages of Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels have major problems:

1. **Limited reserves**  
   They take millions of years to form, so they are non-renewable.

2. **Air pollution**  
   Burning fossil fuels releases harmful gases.

3. **Acid rain**  
   Oxides of sulphur and nitrogen can form acids in the atmosphere, causing acid rain.

4. **Greenhouse effect**  
   Carbon dioxide increases global warming.

5. **Environmental damage during extraction**  
   Mining and drilling disturb land, water, and ecosystems.

### Why Alternative Energy Sources Are Needed

We are searching for alternative energy sources because:

- Fossil fuels are limited.
- Energy demand is increasing.
- Fossil fuels cause pollution.
- Fossil fuels contribute to climate change.
- Cleaner and renewable sources are needed for long-term use.

---

## Thermal Power Plant

A thermal power plant uses heat energy from burning fossil fuels to produce electricity.

### Working

1. Fossil fuel such as coal or oil is burnt.
2. Heat is used to boil water.
3. Steam is produced at high pressure.
4. Steam rotates a turbine.
5. The turbine rotates a generator.
6. The generator produces electricity.

### Why Are Thermal Power Plants Often Near Coal or Oil Fields?

Transporting electricity is usually more efficient than transporting large quantities of coal or oil. Therefore, many thermal power plants are built near fuel sources.

### Energy Conversion

```txt
Chemical energy of fuel
↓
Heat energy
↓
Kinetic energy of steam
↓
Mechanical energy of turbine
↓
Electrical energy
```

### Limitations

- Uses fossil fuels.
- Produces smoke and harmful gases.
- Releases carbon dioxide.
- Produces ash.
- Causes thermal pollution and air pollution.

---

## Hydroelectric Power Plant

Hydroelectric power plants convert the potential energy of stored water into electrical energy.

### Working

1. A dam is built across a river.
2. Water is stored in a reservoir at a height.
3. Water is allowed to fall through pipes.
4. Falling water rotates turbine blades.
5. The turbine rotates a generator.
6. Electricity is produced.

### Energy Conversion

```txt
Potential energy of stored water
↓
Kinetic energy of falling water
↓
Mechanical energy of turbine
↓
Electrical energy
```

### Why Hydroelectric Energy Is Renewable

Water reservoirs are refilled by rainfall. Since the water cycle continues naturally, hydroelectric energy is considered renewable.

### Advantages

- Renewable source.
- No direct air pollution during operation.
- Useful for large-scale electricity generation.
- Dams can also help in irrigation and flood control.

### Disadvantages of Large Dams

Large dams create serious problems:

- Large areas of land are submerged.
- Forests and agricultural land may be destroyed.
- People living in the area may be displaced.
- Rehabilitation becomes difficult.
- Submerged vegetation decomposes anaerobically and produces methane, a greenhouse gas.
- Aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems may be damaged.

Examples of dam-related concerns mentioned in the chapter include projects such as Tehri Dam and Sardar Sarovar Dam.

## Biomass

Biomass means plant and animal waste used as a source of energy.

Examples:

- Wood
- Crop residue
- Cow dung
- Vegetable waste
- Agricultural waste
- Animal waste

### Why Biomass Is Renewable

Biomass comes from plants and animals. If plants are grown again and waste is continuously produced, biomass can be renewed.

## Wood and Charcoal

Wood has been used as fuel for a long time.

### Problems with Direct Burning of Wood

- Produces smoke.
- Produces less heat compared with improved fuels.
- Causes air pollution.
- Can lead to deforestation if not managed properly.

### Charcoal

When wood is burnt in limited oxygen, volatile materials and water are removed and charcoal remains.

Charcoal is better than wood because:

- It burns without flames.
- It produces less smoke.
- It has higher heat-producing efficiency.

---

## Biogas

Biogas is produced by the anaerobic decomposition of animal and plant waste.

It is commonly called **gobar gas** because cow dung is a major raw material.

### Raw Materials

- Cow dung
- Plant waste
- Crop residue
- Sewage
- Vegetable waste

### Main Components of Biogas

Biogas contains mainly:

- Methane
- Carbon dioxide
- Hydrogen
- Hydrogen sulphide

Methane is the major useful fuel component. Biogas may contain up to about 75% methane.

### Biogas Plant: Working

1. Cow dung and water are mixed to form slurry.
2. Slurry is fed into a digester.
3. The digester is an oxygen-free chamber.
4. Anaerobic microorganisms decompose the slurry.
5. Biogas is produced.
6. Gas collects in the gas tank above the digester.
7. Gas is taken out through pipes for use.
8. The leftover slurry is removed and used as manure.

### Advantages of Biogas

- Burns without smoke.
- Has high heating capacity.
- Leaves no ash after burning.
- Provides clean fuel.
- Produces useful manure as by-product.
- Helps in safe disposal of organic waste.
- Renewable source of energy.

### Biogas Plant Parts

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Mixing tank | Cow dung and water are mixed to form slurry. |
| Digester | Anaerobic decomposition happens here. |
| Gas tank | Stores biogas. |
| Outlet tank | Removes spent slurry. |
| Gas outlet pipe | Carries biogas for use. |

## Wind Energy

Wind energy is the kinetic energy of moving air.

Wind has been used traditionally for:

- Running windmills
- Pumping water
- Grinding grains
- Moving sailing boats

Today, wind energy is also used to generate electricity.

### Windmill

A windmill has large blades mounted on a strong tower. When wind blows, the blades rotate. This rotation can be used to run a generator.

### Wind Energy Farm

A single windmill produces only a small amount of electricity. Therefore, many windmills are installed over a large area and connected together. This is called a **wind energy farm**.

### Advantages

- Renewable.
- Environment-friendly during operation.
- No fuel cost after installation.
- Useful in windy regions.

### Limitations

- Wind farms can be built only where strong winds blow for most of the year.
- Wind speed should usually be more than about 15 km/h to maintain turbine motion.
- Backup systems such as storage cells are needed when wind is not blowing.
- Large land area is required.
- Initial cost is high.
- Maintenance is high because blades and towers face storms, rain, heat, and other natural forces.

### Important Data Points

- Denmark is known as the “country of winds”.
- India is among the major wind-energy-producing countries.
- A large wind energy farm is located near Kanyakumari in Tamil Nadu.

## Solar Energy

The Sun is the ultimate source of energy for most energy sources on Earth.

Solar energy reaches Earth as light and heat.

### Why India Has High Solar Energy Potential

India receives sunlight for a large part of the year. Therefore, solar energy can be used widely in India.

### Solar Constant

The solar constant is the amount of solar energy received per unit area per second on a surface placed perpendicular to the Sun’s rays at the average distance between the Sun and Earth.

Approximate value:

```txt
Solar constant ≈ 1.4 kW/m²
```

## Solar Cooker

A solar cooker uses sunlight to cook food.

### Construction

A typical solar cooker has:

- A black-painted box
- A glass sheet cover
- Reflector or mirror
- Insulated walls

### Working

- Black surface absorbs more heat.
- Glass sheet allows sunlight to enter but prevents heat from escaping easily.
- Mirror reflects more sunlight into the box.
- Insulation reduces heat loss.

### Best Mirror for Solar Cooker

A concave mirror is most suitable because it focuses sunlight at one region and raises the temperature.

### Advantages

- Uses free solar energy.
- Does not produce smoke.
- Saves fuel.
- Environment-friendly.
- Useful in sunny regions.

### Limitations

- Cannot be used at night.
- Cannot be used effectively on cloudy or rainy days.
- Cooking is slow.
- Direction of cooker may need adjustment.
- Not suitable for all types of cooking.
- Limited use in regions with low sunlight.

## Solar Water Heater

A solar water heater uses solar energy to heat water.

### Uses

- Homes

- Hostels

### Limitation

## Solar Cell

A solar cell converts solar energy directly into electrical energy.

### Output of a Typical Solar Cell

A typical solar cell can produce:

```txt
Voltage: about 0.5 V to 1.0 V
Power: about 0.7 W
```

### Solar Panel

A solar panel is made by connecting many solar cells together.

### Advantages of Solar Cells

- No moving parts.
- Low maintenance.
- Work without focusing devices.
- Useful in remote and inaccessible areas.
- No pollution during operation.
- Useful where power transmission lines are difficult or expensive.

### Disadvantages of Solar Cells

- Silicon used for making solar cells is abundant, but high-grade silicon is limited.
- Manufacturing process is expensive.
- Silver is used for interconnections, increasing cost.
- Efficiency is relatively low.
- Domestic use is limited due to high cost.

### Uses of Solar Cells

- Artificial satellites
- Space probes
- Mars orbiters
- Traffic signals
- Calculators
- Toys
- Remote TV relay stations
- Wireless communication systems
- Street lights in remote areas

# Energy from the Sea

The sea can provide energy in three major ways:

1. Tidal energy
2. Wave energy
3. Ocean thermal energy

---

## Tidal Energy

Tides are caused mainly by the gravitational pull of the Moon on Earth’s oceans.

The rise and fall of sea water can be used to generate electricity.

### Working

1. A dam is built across a narrow opening to the sea.
2. Water level rises and falls during tides.
3. Turbines installed at the dam rotate due to water movement.
4. Generators produce electricity.

### Limitation

- Suitable locations are limited.
- Construction cost can be high.
- Environmental impact on coastal ecosystems is possible.

---

## Wave Energy

Wave energy is the kinetic energy of ocean waves.

### Source of Waves

Strong winds blowing across the sea surface produce waves.

### Use

Wave energy can be trapped using special devices to rotate turbines and generate electricity.

### Limitation

- Useful only where waves are very strong.
- Technology is complex.
- Commercial exploitation is difficult.

---

## Ocean Thermal Energy

Ocean thermal energy is obtained from the temperature difference between warm surface water and cold deep water of the ocean.

### OTEC

OTEC stands for **Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion**.

### Condition for OTEC

OTEC plants can operate when the temperature difference between surface water and water at about 2 km depth is at least about 20°C.

### Working

1. Warm surface water is used to boil a volatile liquid such as ammonia.
2. Vapour of the liquid rotates a turbine.
3. The turbine runs a generator.
4. Cold deep-sea water condenses the vapour back into liquid.
5. The cycle repeats.

### Limitation

- Requires suitable oceanic conditions.
- Technology and infrastructure are expensive.
- Commercial use is difficult.

---

## Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy is heat energy from inside the Earth.

### How It Is Formed

Due to geological changes, molten rocks may come near the Earth’s crust. These hot regions are called **hot spots**.

When underground water comes in contact with hot spots, steam is produced. Sometimes this steam escapes naturally as hot springs. In some places, steam gets trapped under high pressure and can be taken out through pipes.

### Use

High-pressure steam from underground hot spots is used to rotate turbines and generate electricity.

### Advantages

- Does not require burning fuel.
- Operating cost can be low.
- Reliable where available.

### Limitations

- Suitable locations are limited.
- Commercial exploitation is practical only in specific regions.
- Initial setup can be difficult.

Examples mentioned include geothermal power plants in New Zealand and the United States.

---

## Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is released during nuclear reactions.

The chapter mainly discusses **nuclear fission**.

---

## Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission is the process in which the nucleus of a heavy atom, such as uranium, plutonium, or thorium, is split into lighter nuclei by bombarding it with low-energy neutrons.

### Result

- Large amount of energy is released.
- More neutrons may be released.
- A chain reaction can occur.

### Energy Comparison

The energy released by fission of one uranium atom is much greater than the energy released by burning one carbon atom from coal.

### Nuclear Reactor

In a nuclear power plant, nuclear fission is controlled. The heat released is used to produce steam, which rotates turbines and generates electricity.

### Energy Conversion

```txt
Nuclear energy
↓
Heat energy
↓
Steam energy
↓
Mechanical energy of turbine
↓
Electrical energy
```

### Mass-Energy Relation

Einstein’s mass-energy relation explains the energy released in nuclear reactions:

```txt
E = Δmc²
```

Where:

- `E` = energy released
- `Δm` = mass defect
- `c` = speed of light in vacuum

### Advantages of Nuclear Energy

- Produces a very large amount of energy from a small amount of fuel.
- Useful for large-scale electricity generation.
- Does not produce carbon dioxide during electricity generation in the same way fossil fuel plants do.

### Disadvantages of Nuclear Energy

- Nuclear waste is highly dangerous.
- Waste storage and disposal are difficult.
- Risk of accidental radiation leakage.
- High cost of construction.
- Uranium availability is limited.
- Nuclear radiation can harm living organisms and the environment.

---

## Nuclear Fusion

Nuclear fusion is the process in which two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus and release a large amount of energy.

Example:

```txt
²H + ²H → ³He + n + energy
```

Fusion is the source of energy in the Sun and other stars.

### Conditions Required

Fusion requires extremely high temperature and pressure.

### Hydrogen Bomb

Hydrogen bombs are based on thermonuclear fusion. A fission bomb is used to create the extremely high temperature needed to start fusion.

No energy source is completely free from environmental impact.

Even clean sources can create problems during:

- Construction
- Manufacturing
- Installation
- Maintenance
- Waste disposal
- Land use
- Extraction of raw materials

When choosing an energy source, consider:

1. Ease of extracting energy

3. Efficiency of technology
4. Environmental damage
5. Availability
6. Safety

It is more accurate to say one source is cleaner than another, instead of saying a source is completely pollution-free.

# Common Mistakes

1. **Mistake:** Energy is destroyed after use.  
   **Correction:** Energy is conserved, but it becomes less useful after conversion and dispersal.

2. **Mistake:** Solar energy is useful only for cooking.  
   **Correction:** Solar energy can be used for cooking, heating water, and generating electricity.

3. **Mistake:** Biogas and biomass are the same.  
   **Correction:** Biomass is the raw organic material; biogas is fuel gas produced from biomass.

4. **Mistake:** Hydroelectricity causes no environmental problem.  
   **Correction:** It produces no direct smoke, but dams can damage ecosystems and displace people.

5. **Mistake:** Nuclear energy is completely clean.  
   **Correction:** It avoids fossil-fuel smoke but creates radioactive waste and radiation risks.

6. **Mistake:** All renewable sources are always available everywhere.  
   **Correction:** Many depend on location, weather, season, or technology.

# Long Answer Questions

## 1. Explain the working of a hydroelectric power plant.

In a hydroelectric power plant, a dam is built across a river to store water at a height. The stored water has potential energy. When water is released through pipes, it falls with high speed and its potential energy changes into kinetic energy. This falling water rotates the blades of a turbine. The turbine is connected to a generator, which converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. Hydroelectric energy is renewable because reservoirs are refilled by rainfall. However, large dams can cause displacement of people, destruction of forests, loss of agricultural land, and ecological damage.

## 2. Explain the working of a biogas plant.

In a biogas plant, cow dung and water are mixed to form slurry. This slurry is sent into an oxygen-free digester. Anaerobic microorganisms decompose the slurry and produce biogas, which contains methane, carbon dioxide, hydrogen, and hydrogen sulphide. Biogas collects in a gas tank and is supplied through pipes for use as fuel. The spent slurry is removed and used as manure. Biogas is a good fuel because it burns without smoke, gives high heat, leaves no ash, and helps manage organic waste safely.

## 3. Explain the working of a solar cooker.

A solar cooker has a black box, insulated walls, a glass cover, and often a mirror. The black surface absorbs solar heat. The glass cover allows sunlight to enter but reduces heat loss due to the greenhouse effect. The mirror reflects extra sunlight into the box. Insulation prevents heat from escaping. The trapped heat cooks the food. Solar cookers save fuel and do not cause pollution, but they cannot be used at night or on cloudy days and cooking is slow.

## 4. Explain the working of an OTEC plant.

An OTEC plant uses the temperature difference between warm surface ocean water and cold deep ocean water. Warm surface water is used to boil a volatile liquid such as ammonia. The vapour produced rotates a turbine connected to a generator. Cold water pumped from deep ocean layers condenses the vapour back into liquid. The cycle continues. OTEC works only where the temperature difference between surface water and water at about 2 km depth is at least about 20°C.

## 5. Explain nuclear fission and its use in electricity generation.

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus such as uranium, plutonium, or thorium into lighter nuclei by bombarding it with low-energy neutrons. A large amount of energy is released due to mass defect, explained by Einstein’s equation `E = Δmc²`. In nuclear power plants, fission is controlled in a reactor. The heat released is used to produce steam. Steam rotates turbines connected to generators, producing electricity. Nuclear energy gives huge energy from small fuel, but nuclear waste and radiation leakage are serious concerns.

## 6. Discuss environmental concerns related to energy sources.

Every energy source affects the environment in some way. Fossil fuels release carbon dioxide, sulphur oxides, nitrogen oxides, smoke, and ash. Large dams can submerge forests and displace people. Wind farms need large land area and affect landscapes. Solar cells do not pollute during operation but their manufacturing has environmental costs. Nuclear power creates radioactive waste and risk of radiation leakage. Therefore, an energy source should be chosen by considering availability, cost, efficiency, safety, and environmental impact.
