# Chapter 1: Light - Reflection and Refraction

## Chapter Overview

Light makes objects visible. In a dark room objects cannot be seen, but when light falls on them and is reflected into our eyes, they become visible.

This chapter explains two major optical phenomena:

1. **Reflection of light** - bouncing back of light from a surface.
2. **Refraction of light** - bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another.

The chapter focuses on ray optics using the idea that light travels in straight lines. It covers plane mirrors, spherical mirrors, refraction through a glass slab, spherical lenses, mirror formula, lens formula, magnification, refractive index, and power of a lens.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Light Travels in a Straight Line

In ordinary situations, light appears to travel along straight paths. A narrow path of light is called a **ray of light**.

Evidence:
- Sharp shadows are formed by small light sources.
- Objects are visible only when reflected light reaches our eyes.
- Transparent media allow light to pass through them.

### 2. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it falls on a polished surface.

A highly polished surface, such as a mirror, reflects most of the light falling on it.

### 3. Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light when it travels obliquely from one transparent medium into another.

Refraction happens because the speed of light changes in different media.

Examples:
- A pencil partly dipped in water appears bent.
- A coin at the bottom of a water-filled bowl appears raised.
- Letters under a glass slab appear raised.
- A lemon in a glass of water appears larger.

### 4. Spherical Mirrors

A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface is part of a sphere.

There are two types:

1. **Concave mirror**  
   Reflecting surface curves inward.

2. **Convex mirror**  
   Reflecting surface bulges outward.

### 5. Spherical Lenses

A spherical lens is a transparent medium bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is spherical.

There are two major types:

1. **Convex lens**  
   Thicker at the centre and thinner at the edges. It converges light rays.

2. **Concave lens**  
   Thinner at the centre and thicker at the edges. It diverges light rays.

# Part 1: Reflection of Light

## Laws of Reflection

Reflection follows two laws:

1. **Angle of incidence is equal to angle of reflection.**

   ```txt
   ∠i = ∠r
   ```

2. **The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.**

These laws apply to plane mirrors as well as spherical mirrors.

---

## Image Formed by a Plane Mirror

A plane mirror always forms an image with these properties:

```txt
1. Virtual
2. Erect
3. Same size as the object
4. Laterally inverted
5. Formed behind the mirror
6. Image distance = object distance
```

### Lateral Inversion

In lateral inversion, the left side of the object appears as the right side in the image and the right side appears as the left side.

---

# Part 2: Spherical Mirrors

## Types of Spherical Mirrors

### Concave Mirror

A concave mirror has its reflecting surface curved inward.

It can form:
- Real and inverted images
- Virtual and erect images
- Magnified images
- Diminished images

The image depends on the position of the object.

### Convex Mirror

A convex mirror has its reflecting surface curved outward.

It always forms an image that is:
- Virtual
- Erect
- Diminished
- Behind the mirror

---

## Important Terms Related to Spherical Mirrors

### Pole

The centre of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror is called the **pole**. It is represented by **P**.

### Centre of Curvature

The centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part is called the **centre of curvature**. It is represented by **C**.

### Radius of Curvature

The distance between the pole and the centre of curvature is called the **radius of curvature**. It is represented by **R**.

```txt
PC = R
```

### Principal Axis

The straight line passing through the pole and centre of curvature is called the **principal axis**.

### Principal Focus

For a concave mirror, rays parallel to the principal axis meet at a point after reflection. This point is the **principal focus**.

For a convex mirror, rays parallel to the principal axis appear to diverge from a point behind the mirror. This point is the **principal focus**.

It is represented by **F**.

### Focal Length

The distance between the pole and the principal focus is called the **focal length**.

It is represented by **f**.

### Relation Between Radius of Curvature and Focal Length

For a spherical mirror with small aperture:

```txt
R = 2f
```

So,

```txt
f = R / 2
```

---

## Image Formation by a Concave Mirror

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Size | Nature of Image |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus F | Highly diminished, point-sized | Real and inverted |
| Beyond C | Between F and C | Diminished | Real and inverted |
| At C | At C | Same size | Real and inverted |
| Between C and F | Beyond C | Enlarged | Real and inverted |
| At F | At infinity | Highly enlarged | Real and inverted |
| Between P and F | Behind the mirror | Enlarged | Virtual and erect |

### Important Pattern

```txt
Concave mirror can form both real and virtual images.
When object is between pole and focus, image becomes virtual, erect, and enlarged.
```

---

## Image Formation by a Convex Mirror

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Size | Nature of Image |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus F behind the mirror | Highly diminished, point-sized | Virtual and erect |
| Between infinity and pole P | Between P and F behind the mirror | Diminished | Virtual and erect |

### Important Pattern

```txt
Convex mirror always forms virtual, erect, and diminished image.
```

---

## Ray Rules for Spherical Mirrors

### Rule 1

A ray parallel to the principal axis:

- After reflection from a concave mirror, passes through the principal focus.
- After reflection from a convex mirror, appears to come from the principal focus.

### Rule 2

A ray passing through the principal focus of a concave mirror:

- After reflection, becomes parallel to the principal axis.

A ray directed toward the principal focus of a convex mirror:

- After reflection, becomes parallel to the principal axis.

### Rule 3

A ray passing through the centre of curvature:

- Strikes the mirror normally.
- Returns along the same path after reflection.

For a convex mirror, a ray directed toward the centre of curvature appears to return along the same path.

### Rule 4

A ray incident at the pole:

- Reflects according to the law of reflection.
- Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection.

---

## Uses of Concave Mirrors

Concave mirrors are used in:

```txt
1. Torches
2. Searchlights
3. Vehicle headlights
4. Shaving mirrors
5. Dentists' mirrors
6. Solar furnaces
```

### Reason

Concave mirrors can:
- Produce a strong parallel beam of light.
- Form enlarged images when the object is close.
- Concentrate sunlight at the focus.

---

## Uses of Convex Mirrors

Convex mirrors are used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles.

### Reasons

```txt
1. They always form erect images.
2. They form diminished images.
3. They provide a wider field of view.
4. They allow the driver to see a large area behind the vehicle.
```

# Part 3: Sign Convention for Spherical Mirrors

The chapter uses the **New Cartesian Sign Convention**.

Rules:

```txt
1. The pole P of the mirror is taken as the origin.
2. The principal axis is taken as the x-axis.
3. The object is always placed on the left side of the mirror.
4. Distances measured in the direction of incident light are positive.
5. Distances measured opposite to the direction of incident light are negative.
6. Distances measured upward perpendicular to the principal axis are positive.
7. Distances measured downward perpendicular to the principal axis are negative.
```

### Practical Signs for Mirrors

| Quantity | Concave Mirror | Convex Mirror |
|---|---|---|
| Object distance `u` | Negative | Negative |
| Focal length `f` | Negative | Positive |
| Radius of curvature `R` | Negative | Positive |
| Real image distance `v` | Negative | Not usually formed |
| Virtual image distance `v` | Positive | Positive |

---

## Mirror Formula

For spherical mirrors:

```txt
1/v + 1/u = 1/f
```

Where:

```txt
u = object distance
v = image distance
f = focal length
```

This formula is valid for both concave and convex mirrors.

---

## Magnification by Spherical Mirrors

Magnification tells how many times the image is enlarged or diminished compared to the object.

```txt
m = h' / h
```

Where:

```txt
h  = height of object
h' = height of image
```

For mirrors:

```txt
m = h' / h = -v / u
```

### Meaning of Sign of Magnification

| Sign of `m` | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Positive | Image is virtual and erect |
| Negative | Image is real and inverted |

# Part 4: Refraction of Light

## What is Refraction?

Refraction is the change in direction of light when it passes obliquely from one transparent medium to another.

Reason:

```txt
Light travels with different speeds in different media.
```

---

## Everyday Examples of Refraction

```txt
1. A pencil partly dipped in water appears bent.
2. A coin inside water appears raised.
3. The bottom of a water tank appears raised.
4. Letters under a glass slab appear raised.
5. A lemon in water appears bigger.
```

---

## Refraction Through a Rectangular Glass Slab

When a light ray passes through a rectangular glass slab:

1. It bends toward the normal when it enters glass from air.
2. It bends away from the normal when it emerges from glass into air.
3. The emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray.
4. The emergent ray is laterally displaced.

### Why is the emergent ray parallel to the incident ray?

In a rectangular glass slab, the two opposite faces are parallel.

The bending at the first surface and the second surface is equal and opposite. So the emergent ray becomes parallel to the incident ray, but shifted sideways.

---

## Laws of Refraction

### First Law

The incident ray, refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

### Second Law: Snell's Law

For a given pair of media and a given colour of light:

```txt
sin i / sin r = constant
```

Where:

```txt
i = angle of incidence
r = angle of refraction
```

This constant is called the **refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first medium**.

---

## Bending of Light in Different Media

### From Rarer Medium to Denser Medium

When light travels from a rarer medium to a denser medium:

```txt
Speed decreases.
Ray bends towards the normal.
```

Example:

```txt
Air → Glass
Air → Water
```

### From Denser Medium to Rarer Medium

When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium:

```txt
Speed increases.
Ray bends away from the normal.
```

Example:

```txt
Glass → Air
Water → Air
```

---

## Refractive Index

Refractive index compares the speed of light in different media.

If light travels from medium 1 to medium 2:

```txt
n21 = speed of light in medium 1 / speed of light in medium 2
```

```txt
n21 = v1 / v2
```

Similarly:

```txt
n12 = v2 / v1
```

### Absolute Refractive Index

If medium 1 is vacuum or air, then the refractive index of medium 2 is called its absolute refractive index.

```txt
n = c / v
```

Where:

```txt
c = speed of light in vacuum
v = speed of light in the medium
```

Speed of light in vacuum:

```txt
c = 3 × 10^8 m/s
```

### Meaning of Higher Refractive Index

A medium with a higher refractive index is optically denser.

A medium with a lower refractive index is optically rarer.

Important:

```txt
Optical density is not the same as mass density.
```

Example:

```txt
Kerosene is optically denser than water because its refractive index is higher,
even though its mass density is lower than water.
```

---

## Refractive Indices of Some Media

| Medium | Refractive Index |
|---|---:|
| Air | 1.0003 |
| Ice | 1.31 |
| Water | 1.33 |
| Alcohol | 1.36 |
| Kerosene | 1.44 |
| Fused quartz | 1.46 |
| Turpentine oil | 1.47 |
| Benzene | 1.50 |
| Crown glass | 1.52 |
| Canada balsam | 1.53 |
| Rock salt | 1.54 |
| Carbon disulphide | 1.63 |
| Dense flint glass | 1.65 |
| Ruby | 1.71 |
| Sapphire | 1.77 |
| Diamond | 2.42 |

### Exam Point

Diamond has a high refractive index:

```txt
n = 2.42
```

This means:

```txt
Speed of light in diamond = speed of light in vacuum / 2.42
```

# Part 5: Spherical Lenses

## What is a Lens?

A lens is a transparent medium bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is spherical.

---

## Types of Spherical Lenses

### Convex Lens

A convex lens is thicker at the centre and thinner at the edges.

It converges parallel rays of light.

So, it is also called a:

```txt
Converging lens
```

### Concave Lens

A concave lens is thinner at the centre and thicker at the edges.

It diverges parallel rays of light.

So, it is also called a:

```txt
Diverging lens
```

---

## Important Terms Related to Lenses

### Centre of Curvature

A lens has two spherical surfaces. Each surface has a centre of curvature.

They are represented by:

```txt
C1 and C2
```

### Principal Axis

The imaginary straight line passing through both centres of curvature is the principal axis.

### Optical Centre

The central point of a lens is called its optical centre.

It is represented by:

```txt
O
```

A ray passing through the optical centre usually emerges without deviation.

### Principal Focus

A lens has two principal foci:

```txt
F1 and F2
```

For a convex lens:
- Parallel rays converge at the principal focus after refraction.

For a concave lens:
- Parallel rays appear to diverge from the principal focus.

### Focal Length

Distance between optical centre and principal focus is called focal length.

```txt
OF = f
```

### Aperture

The effective diameter of the circular outline of a lens is called aperture.

---

## Image Formation by a Convex Lens

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Size | Nature of Image |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus F2 | Highly diminished, point-sized | Real and inverted |
| Beyond 2F1 | Between F2 and 2F2 | Diminished | Real and inverted |
| At 2F1 | At 2F2 | Same size | Real and inverted |
| Between F1 and 2F1 | Beyond 2F2 | Enlarged | Real and inverted |
| At F1 | At infinity | Highly enlarged | Real and inverted |
| Between F1 and optical centre O | Same side as object | Enlarged | Virtual and erect |

### Important Pattern

```txt
Convex lens usually forms real and inverted images.
When object is between F1 and O, image becomes virtual, erect, and enlarged.
```

---

## Image Formation by a Concave Lens

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Size | Nature of Image |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus F1 | Highly diminished, point-sized | Virtual and erect |
| Between infinity and optical centre O | Between F1 and O | Diminished | Virtual and erect |

### Important Pattern

```txt
Concave lens always forms virtual, erect, and diminished image.
```

---

## Ray Rules for Lenses

### Rule 1

A ray parallel to the principal axis:

- After refraction through a convex lens, passes through the principal focus on the other side.
- After refraction through a concave lens, appears to diverge from the principal focus on the same side.

### Rule 2

A ray passing through the principal focus of a convex lens:

- After refraction, emerges parallel to the principal axis.

A ray directed toward the principal focus of a concave lens:

- After refraction, emerges parallel to the principal axis.

### Rule 3

A ray passing through the optical centre:

- Emerges without deviation.

---

# Part 6: Sign Convention for Lenses

The sign convention for lenses is similar to mirrors, but distances are measured from the optical centre.

Rules:

```txt
1. Optical centre O is taken as the origin.
2. Principal axis is taken as x-axis.
3. Object is usually placed on the left side of the lens.
4. Distances measured in the direction of incident light are positive.
5. Distances measured opposite to incident light are negative.
6. Heights above the principal axis are positive.
7. Heights below the principal axis are negative.
```

### Practical Signs for Lenses

| Quantity | Convex Lens | Concave Lens |
|---|---|---|
| Object distance `u` | Negative | Negative |
| Focal length `f` | Positive | Negative |
| Real image distance `v` | Positive | Usually not formed |
| Virtual image distance `v` | Negative | Negative |
| Power `P` | Positive | Negative |

---

## Lens Formula

For spherical lenses:

```txt
1/v - 1/u = 1/f
```

Where:

```txt
u = object distance
v = image distance
f = focal length
```

This formula is valid for both convex and concave lenses.

---

## Magnification by Lenses

Magnification by a lens is:

```txt
m = h' / h
```

Also:

```txt
m = v / u
```

Where:

```txt
h  = height of object
h' = height of image
u  = object distance
v  = image distance
```

### Meaning of Sign of Magnification

| Sign of `m` | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Positive | Image is virtual and erect |
| Negative | Image is real and inverted |

---

## Power of a Lens

Power of a lens is the degree of convergence or divergence of light rays.

It is the reciprocal of focal length in metres.

```txt
P = 1/f
```

Where:

```txt
P = power of lens
f = focal length in metre
```

### SI Unit of Power

The SI unit of power of a lens is:

```txt
Dioptre (D)
```

```txt
1 D = 1 m^-1
```

### Sign of Power

| Lens Type | Sign of Power |
|---|---|
| Convex lens | Positive |
| Concave lens | Negative |

### Examples

If a lens has focal length:

```txt
f = +0.50 m
```

Then:

```txt
P = 1 / 0.50 = +2.0 D
```

So, it is a convex lens.

If a lens has power:

```txt
P = -2.5 D
```

Then:

```txt
f = 1 / P = 1 / -2.5 = -0.40 m
```

So, it is a concave lens.

# Numerical Problem Patterns

## Pattern 1: Focal Length from Radius of Curvature

Question:

```txt
Radius of curvature of a spherical mirror = 20 cm.
Find focal length.
```

Solution:

```txt
R = 2f
f = R / 2
f = 20 / 2 = 10 cm
```

---

## Pattern 2: Speed of Light in a Medium

Question:

```txt
Light enters glass of refractive index 1.50.
Find speed of light in glass.
Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10^8 m/s.
```

Solution:

```txt
n = c / v
v = c / n
v = (3 × 10^8) / 1.50
v = 2 × 10^8 m/s
```

---

## Pattern 3: Power from Focal Length

Question:

```txt
Find power of a concave lens of focal length 2 m.
```

Solution:

For concave lens:

```txt
f = -2 m
P = 1/f
P = 1 / -2
P = -0.5 D
```

---

## Pattern 4: Focal Length from Power

Question:

```txt
Power of a corrective lens is +1.5 D.
Find focal length and type of lens.
```

Solution:

```txt
P = +1.5 D
f = 1/P
f = 1 / 1.5
f = 0.67 m
```

Since power is positive, the lens is convex.

---

## Pattern 5: Mirror Formula

Question:

```txt
A concave mirror of focal length 15 cm forms an image of an object placed 25 cm away.
Find image distance.
```

Use:

```txt
1/v + 1/u = 1/f
```

For concave mirror:

```txt
u = -25 cm
f = -15 cm
```

Then substitute carefully using signs.

---

## Pattern 6: Lens Formula

Question:

```txt
A convex lens of focal length 10 cm has an object placed 15 cm from it.
Find the image distance.
```

Use:

```txt
1/v - 1/u = 1/f
```

For convex lens:

```txt
u = -15 cm
f = +10 cm
```

Then substitute carefully using signs.

# Common Confusions

## Concave Mirror vs Convex Lens

Both can converge light, but:

| Concave Mirror | Convex Lens |
|---|---|
| Works by reflection | Works by refraction |
| Has pole P | Has optical centre O |
| Focal length is negative by sign convention | Focal length is positive by sign convention |
| Used in headlights, shaving mirrors | Used in magnifying glass, cameras, spectacles |

## Convex Mirror vs Concave Lens

Both diverge light, but:

| Convex Mirror | Concave Lens |
|---|---|
| Works by reflection | Works by refraction |
| Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image | Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image |
| Focal length positive | Focal length negative |
| Used as rear-view mirror | Used in spectacles for myopia |

## Real Image vs Virtual Image

| Real Image | Virtual Image |
|---|---|
| Can be obtained on screen | Cannot be obtained on screen |
| Usually inverted | Usually erect |
| Formed by actual meeting of rays | Formed by apparent meeting of rays |

# Exam-Focused Points

1. Light travels in straight lines in a homogeneous transparent medium.
2. Reflection follows two laws: `i = r`, and incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane.
3. Plane mirror forms virtual, erect, same-size, laterally inverted image.
4. Concave mirror can form real as well as virtual images.
5. Convex mirror always forms virtual, erect, and diminished image.
6. Concave mirrors are used in torches, headlights, shaving mirrors, dentists' mirrors, and solar furnaces.
7. Convex mirrors are used as rear-view mirrors because they give a wider field of view.
8. For spherical mirrors, `R = 2f`.
9. Mirror formula is `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`.
10. Mirror magnification is `m = h'/h = -v/u`.
11. Refraction occurs because light changes speed in different media.
12. Light bends toward the normal when it enters a denser medium from a rarer medium.
13. Light bends away from the normal when it enters a rarer medium from a denser medium.
14. Snell's law is `sin i / sin r = constant`.
15. Absolute refractive index is `n = c/v`.
16. Convex lens is a converging lens.
17. Concave lens is a diverging lens.
18. Convex lens can form real and virtual images.

# Short Answer Questions

## 1. What is reflection of light?

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it falls on a surface.

## 2. State the laws of reflection.

1. Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection.
2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane.

## 3. What is the principal focus of a concave mirror?

It is the point on the principal axis where rays parallel to the principal axis meet after reflection.

## 4. What is the principal focus of a convex mirror?

It is the point on the principal axis from which rays parallel to the principal axis appear to diverge after reflection.

## 5. What is the relation between radius of curvature and focal length?

```txt
R = 2f
```

## 6. Why are convex mirrors used as rear-view mirrors?

Because they form erect, diminished images and provide a wide field of view.

## 7. What is refraction?

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes obliquely from one transparent medium to another.

## 8. Why does a pencil appear bent in water?

Because light from the submerged part of the pencil bends when it passes from water to air.

## 9. State Snell's law.

For a given pair of media and a given colour of light:

```txt
sin i / sin r = constant
```

## 10. What is absolute refractive index?

Absolute refractive index is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in the medium.

```txt
n = c/v
```

## 11. Which lens is called a converging lens?

A convex lens is called a converging lens.

## 12. Which lens is called a diverging lens?

A concave lens is called a diverging lens.

## 13. Define one dioptre.

One dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre.

```txt
1 D = 1 m^-1
```

## 14. What is the power of a convex lens?

The power of a convex lens is positive.

## 15. What is the power of a concave lens?

The power of a concave lens is negative.

# Long Answer Questions

## 1. Explain image formation by a concave mirror for different object positions.

A concave mirror forms different types of images depending on object position. When the object is at infinity, the image forms at focus and is highly diminished, real, and inverted. When the object is beyond C, the image forms between F and C and is diminished, real, and inverted. At C, the image is formed at C and has the same size. Between C and F, the image is formed beyond C and is enlarged, real, and inverted. At F, the image is formed at infinity. Between P and F, the image is formed behind the mirror and is virtual, erect, and enlarged.

## 2. Explain why refraction occurs.

Refraction occurs because light travels at different speeds in different transparent media. When light enters another medium obliquely, its speed changes and its direction changes. When it travels from a rarer medium to a denser medium, it bends toward the normal. When it travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium, it bends away from the normal.

## 3. Explain refraction through a rectangular glass slab.

When a ray of light enters a rectangular glass slab from air, it bends toward the normal because glass is optically denser than air. When the ray emerges from glass into air, it bends away from the normal. Since the opposite faces of the slab are parallel, the emergent ray becomes parallel to the incident ray, but it is laterally displaced.

## 4. Compare convex and concave lenses.

A convex lens is thicker at the centre and thinner at the edges. It converges light rays and has positive focal length. It can form real and virtual images depending on the position of the object.

A concave lens is thinner at the centre and thicker at the edges. It diverges light rays and has negative focal length. It always forms virtual, erect, and diminished images.

## 5. Explain power of a lens.

Power of a lens is its ability to converge or diverge light rays. It is the reciprocal of focal length in metres.

```txt
P = 1/f
```

The SI unit of power is dioptre. Convex lenses have positive power and concave lenses have negative power.
