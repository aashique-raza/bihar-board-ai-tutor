# Chapter 4: Magnetic Effects of Electric Current

## Chapter Overview

Electric current produces a magnetic effect. A current-carrying conductor behaves like a magnet and creates a magnetic field around it. This chapter connects electricity with magnetism and explains magnetic field lines, magnetic field around current-carrying conductors, solenoids, electromagnets, force on a current-carrying conductor, electric motors, electromagnetic induction, electric generators, and domestic electric circuits.

The main idea is:

```text
Electric current can produce magnetism.
Changing magnetism can produce electric current.
```

This relationship is the basis of electric motors, generators, loudspeakers, microphones, electric measuring instruments, and many electrical safety systems.

- An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.
- Domestic circuits use live wire, neutral wire, earth wire, fuse, and parallel connections for safety and proper working.

## Important Definitions

### Magnetic Field

The region around a magnet where its force can be detected is called the magnetic field.

### Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines used to represent the direction and strength of a magnetic field.

A magnetic field line shows the path along which a free north pole would move.

### Compass Needle

A compass needle is a small magnet. Its north end points approximately toward the geographic north direction.

### Solenoid

A solenoid is a coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wound closely in the shape of a cylinder.

### Electromagnet

An electromagnet is a temporary magnet produced by passing electric current through a coil wound around a soft iron core.

### Electric Motor

An electric motor is a rotating device that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.

### Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic induction is the process by which an electric current is induced in a conductor due to a changing magnetic field.

### Electric Generator

An electric generator is a device that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.

### Alternating Current

Alternating current is current that changes its direction periodically.

### Direct Current

Direct current is current that flows in one direction only.

### Domestic Electric Circuit

A domestic electric circuit is the wiring system used to supply electricity safely to different appliances in a house.

### Short Circuit

A short circuit occurs when the live wire and neutral wire come into direct contact, causing a sudden large current.

### Overloading

Overloading occurs when too many appliances draw current from the same circuit, causing the current to exceed the safe limit.

### Fuse

A fuse is a safety device made of a low-melting-point wire. It melts and breaks the circuit when excessive current flows.

### Earthing

Earthing is the process of connecting the metallic body of an appliance to the earth through a low-resistance wire to prevent electric shock.

## 1. Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

When electric current flows through a metallic conductor, a magnetic field is produced around the conductor.

This can be shown by placing a compass near a current-carrying wire. When current flows through the wire, the compass needle deflects.

This proves that electricity and magnetism are related.

### Oersted’s Observation

Hans Christian Oersted discovered in 1820 that a compass needle gets deflected when placed near a current-carrying wire.

His discovery proved that electric current produces a magnetic field.

---

## 2. Magnetic Field and Field Lines

A magnet influences the space around it. This region is called the magnetic field.

Magnetic field lines are used to represent this field.

### Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

1. Magnetic field lines emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole outside the magnet.
2. Inside the magnet, field lines go from south pole to north pole.
3. Magnetic field lines form closed curves.
4. The direction of the magnetic field at any point is given by the direction in which the north pole of a compass needle points.
5. The closeness of field lines shows the strength of the magnetic field.
6. Magnetic field is stronger where field lines are closer.
7. Magnetic field lines never intersect each other.

### Why Magnetic Field Lines Never Intersect

If two magnetic field lines intersected at one point, a compass needle placed at that point would have to point in two directions at the same time. This is impossible. Therefore, magnetic field lines never intersect.

---

## 3. Magnetic Field Due to a Current-Carrying Conductor

A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it.

The direction and pattern of the magnetic field depend on:

- Shape of the conductor
- Direction of current
- Magnitude of current
- Distance from the conductor

---

## 4. Magnetic Field Around a Straight Current-Carrying Wire

When current flows through a straight conductor, magnetic field lines around it are concentric circles.

The centre of these circles lies on the wire.

### Important Points

- If current increases, magnetic field strength increases.
- If distance from the wire increases, magnetic field strength decreases.
- The direction of magnetic field changes when the direction of current is reversed.

### Direction Rule

The direction of magnetic field around a straight current-carrying wire is given by the Right-Hand Thumb Rule.

---

## 5. Right-Hand Thumb Rule

Hold the current-carrying conductor in your right hand such that your thumb points in the direction of current.

Then the curled fingers show the direction of magnetic field lines around the conductor.

```text
Thumb = Direction of current
Curled fingers = Direction of magnetic field
```

This rule is also called Maxwell’s corkscrew rule.

---

## 6. Magnetic Field Due to a Current-Carrying Circular Loop

When a current flows through a circular loop, magnetic field lines are formed around every part of the loop.

At the centre of the loop, the field lines become nearly straight and perpendicular to the plane of the loop.

### Important Points

- Magnetic field at the centre of a circular loop increases with current.
- Magnetic field increases when the number of turns in the loop increases.
- If a coil has `n` turns, the magnetic field becomes `n` times stronger than that due to one turn, because the magnetic field of each turn adds up.

---

## 7. Magnetic Field Due to a Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of many turns of insulated copper wire.

When current passes through a solenoid, it produces a magnetic field similar to that of a bar magnet.

One end of the solenoid behaves like a north pole and the other end behaves like a south pole.

### Magnetic Field Inside a Solenoid

Inside a solenoid, magnetic field lines are nearly straight and parallel.

This means the magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform.

### Uses of a Solenoid

A solenoid can be used to make an electromagnet by placing a soft iron core inside it.

---

## 8. Electromagnet

An electromagnet is made by winding insulated copper wire around a soft iron core and passing electric current through the coil.

### Why Soft Iron Is Used

Soft iron becomes strongly magnetised when current flows and loses magnetism when current is switched off.

This makes it suitable for temporary magnets.

### Uses of Electromagnets

- Electric bells
- Cranes for lifting iron scrap
- Relays
- Loudspeakers
- Electric motors
- Magnetic separation

---

## 9. Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force.

This force is maximum when the direction of current is perpendicular to the direction of magnetic field.

The direction of the force depends on:

- Direction of current
- Direction of magnetic field

If the direction of current is reversed, the direction of force also reverses.

If the direction of magnetic field is reversed, the direction of force also reverses.

---

## 10. Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule

Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule gives the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field.

Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the left hand so that they are mutually perpendicular.

```text
Forefinger = Direction of magnetic field
Middle finger = Direction of current
Thumb = Direction of force or motion
```

This rule is used in electric motors.

---

## 11. Electric Motor

An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.

### Principle

A current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a force. Due to this force, the coil rotates.

### Main Parts of a Simple Electric Motor

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Rectangular coil ABCD | Carries current and rotates in magnetic field |
| Permanent magnet | Provides magnetic field |
| Split-ring commutator | Reverses current after every half rotation |
| Brushes | Maintain contact between external circuit and rotating coil |
| Battery | Supplies current |
| Axle | Allows rotation of coil |

### Working

A rectangular coil ABCD is placed between the poles of a magnet.

When current flows through the coil, forces act on arms AB and CD in opposite directions.

By Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule:

- Arm AB is pushed downward.
- Arm CD is pushed upward.

This produces rotation of the coil.

After half rotation, the split-ring commutator reverses the current in the coil. Therefore, the forces on the two arms also reverse. This keeps the coil rotating continuously in the same direction.

### Role of Split-Ring Commutator

The split-ring commutator reverses the direction of current in the coil after every half rotation so that the motor continues rotating in the same direction.

### Commercial Motors

Commercial motors use:

- Electromagnets instead of permanent magnets
- Large number of turns in the coil
- Soft iron core on which the coil is wound

The coil and soft iron core together form the armature.

### Uses of Electric Motors

- Electric fans
- Refrigerators
- Mixers
- Washing machines
- Computers
- MP3 players
- Electric vehicles
- Water pumps

## 12. Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon in which current is induced in a coil due to a changing magnetic field.

### Faraday’s Experiment

When a magnet is moved toward a coil connected to a galvanometer, the galvanometer needle deflects.

When the magnet is moved away from the coil, the needle deflects in the opposite direction.

When the magnet is kept stationary near the coil, there is no deflection.

This shows that current is induced only when there is relative motion between the magnet and the coil, or when the magnetic field linked with the coil changes.

### Important Conclusion

A changing magnetic field produces an induced current in a conductor.

---

## 13. Galvanometer

A galvanometer is a device used to detect the presence and direction of small electric current in a circuit.

If no current flows, the pointer remains at zero.

If current flows in one direction, the pointer deflects to one side.

If current flows in the opposite direction, the pointer deflects to the other side.

---

## 14. Induced Current Using Two Coils

If two coils are placed near each other and current in one coil changes, current is induced in the second coil.

The first coil is called the primary coil.

The second coil is called the secondary coil.

When current in the primary coil changes, the magnetic field around it also changes. This changing magnetic field induces current in the secondary coil.

---

## 15. Fleming’s Right-Hand Rule

Fleming’s Right-Hand Rule gives the direction of induced current.

Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the right hand so that they are mutually perpendicular.

```text
Forefinger = Direction of magnetic field
Thumb = Direction of motion of conductor
Middle finger = Direction of induced current
```

This rule is used in electric generators.

---

## 16. Electric Generator

An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.

### Principle

An electric generator works on the principle of electromagnetic induction.

When a coil is rotated in a magnetic field, current is induced in the coil.

### Main Parts of an AC Generator

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Rectangular coil ABCD | Rotates in magnetic field |
| Permanent magnet | Provides magnetic field |
| Slip rings R1 and R2 | Connect rotating coil with external circuit |
| Brushes B1 and B2 | Maintain contact with slip rings |
| Galvanometer / external circuit | Shows or receives induced current |
| Axle | Rotates the coil |

### Working

When the coil rotates between the poles of a magnet, its arms cut magnetic field lines.

By Fleming’s Right-Hand Rule, induced current is produced in the coil.

After every half rotation, the direction of induced current reverses.

Therefore, the current produced by this type of generator is alternating current.

---

## 17. AC Generator and DC Generator

### AC Generator

An AC generator produces alternating current.

It uses slip rings.

In alternating current, direction changes periodically.

### DC Generator

A DC generator produces direct current.

It uses a split-ring commutator instead of slip rings.

Direct current flows in one direction only.

### Difference Between AC and DC

| Point | AC | DC |
|---|---|---|
| Full form | Alternating Current | Direct Current |
| Direction | Changes periodically | Remains same |
| Source examples | AC generator, power station supply | Cell, battery |
| Transmission | Can be transmitted over long distances efficiently | Less suitable for long-distance transmission |
| In India | Frequency is 50 Hz | No frequency because direction does not reverse |

### AC in India

In India, alternating current changes direction every `1/100 s`.

This means its frequency is `50 Hz`.

Domestic supply is usually `220 V, 50 Hz`.

## 18. Domestic Electric Circuits

Electric power reaches homes through mains supply wires.

Usually, three wires are used:

| Wire | Usual Colour | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Live wire | Red | Carries current at high potential |
| Neutral wire | Black | Completes circuit and is at low/zero potential |
| Earth wire | Green | Safety wire connected to metal body of appliances |

In India, the potential difference between live wire and neutral wire is about `220 V`.

### Main Components of Domestic Wiring

- Live wire
- Neutral wire
- Earth wire
- Electric meter
- Main fuse
- Main switch
- Distribution box
- Separate fuses for separate circuits
- Switches
- Sockets
- Appliances

---

## 19. Why Appliances Are Connected in Parallel

Domestic appliances are connected in parallel because:

1. Each appliance gets the same voltage.
2. Each appliance can be switched on or off independently.
3. If one appliance fails, others continue working.
4. Different appliances can draw different currents according to their power rating.

Series connection is not used in domestic wiring because if one appliance fails, the whole circuit breaks.

---

## 20. Earthing

Earthing is an important safety measure.

The earth wire is connected to the metal body of appliances such as electric iron, toaster, refrigerator, and table fan.

If current leaks to the metallic body of the appliance, the earth wire provides a low-resistance path to the ground.

This keeps the appliance body at earth potential and prevents electric shock.

---

## 21. Fuse

A fuse protects circuits and appliances from excessive current.

It is connected in series with the live wire.

When current exceeds the safe limit, the fuse wire heats up, melts, and breaks the circuit.

This prevents damage due to overloading or short circuit.

---

## 22. Short Circuit and Overloading

### Short Circuit

A short circuit occurs when live wire and neutral wire come into direct contact.

This causes a sudden large current.

Reasons:

- Damaged insulation
- Faulty appliances
- Loose wires

### Overloading

Overloading occurs when the current drawn from a circuit exceeds its safe limit.

Reasons:

- Too many appliances connected to one socket
- High-power appliances used on a low-current circuit
- Sudden rise in supply voltage

### Protection

- Use proper fuse or MCB.
- Do not connect many appliances to one socket.
- Use proper earthing.
- Keep wiring insulation safe.
- Use separate circuits for high-power appliances.

## Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain the magnetic field lines around a bar magnet.

Magnetic field lines around a bar magnet emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole outside the magnet. Inside the magnet, they move from south pole to north pole. They form closed curves. The field is stronger near the poles because the lines are closer there. These lines never intersect.

### 2. Explain magnetic field due to a straight current-carrying conductor.

A straight current-carrying conductor produces magnetic field lines in the form of concentric circles around it. The centre of these circles lies on the conductor. The direction of the magnetic field is given by the Right-Hand Thumb Rule. The field increases with current and decreases with distance from the conductor.

### 3. Explain the magnetic field due to a current-carrying solenoid.

A solenoid behaves like a bar magnet when current flows through it. One end acts as north pole and the other as south pole. Inside the solenoid, magnetic field lines are straight, parallel, and equally spaced. Therefore, the magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform. If a soft iron core is placed inside the solenoid, it becomes an electromagnet.

### 4. Explain Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule with use.

Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule gives the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field. The forefinger represents magnetic field, the middle finger represents current, and the thumb represents force or motion. This rule is used to find the direction of rotation in an electric motor.

### 5. Explain the working of an electric motor.

An electric motor contains a rectangular current-carrying coil placed between the poles of a magnet. When current flows through the coil, the two arms of the coil experience forces in opposite directions. These forces rotate the coil. After every half rotation, the split-ring commutator reverses current, so the forces reverse and the coil continues rotating in the same direction. Thus electrical energy is converted into mechanical energy.

### 6. Explain electromagnetic induction.

When a magnet is moved toward or away from a coil connected to a galvanometer, the galvanometer shows deflection. This indicates that current is induced in the coil. If the magnet and coil are stationary relative to each other, no current is induced. Therefore, induced current is produced only when the magnetic field linked with the coil changes.

### 7. Explain the working of an electric generator.

An electric generator consists of a rectangular coil rotating between the poles of a magnet. When the coil rotates, its arms cut magnetic field lines. Due to electromagnetic induction, current is induced in the coil. The direction of induced current changes after every half rotation, so an AC generator produces alternating current. A DC generator uses a split-ring commutator to produce direct current.

### 8. Explain domestic electric wiring and safety devices.

Domestic supply has live, neutral, and earth wires. The live wire carries high potential, neutral wire completes the circuit, and earth wire provides safety. Appliances are connected in parallel so each gets 220 V and works independently. A fuse or MCB protects the circuit by breaking it during excessive current due to short circuit or overloading. Earthing protects users from electric shock.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule with Fleming’s Right-Hand Rule.
- Writing that motor converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. Correct: motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.
- Writing that generator converts electrical energy into mechanical energy. Correct: generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.
- Forgetting that AC changes direction but DC does not.
- Forgetting that domestic appliances are connected in parallel, not series.
- Calling green wire live wire. Correct: green wire is earth wire.
- Forgetting that magnetic field lines never intersect.
- Forgetting that split rings are used in DC motor and DC generator, while slip rings are used in AC generator.
