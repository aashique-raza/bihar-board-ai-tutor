# Chapter 3: Electricity

## Chapter Overview

Electricity has an important place in modern society. It is a convenient and controllable form of energy used in homes, schools, hospitals, industries, and many other places.

This chapter explains:

- electric current and electric circuit
- electric potential and potential difference
- circuit diagrams and symbols
- Ohm's law
- resistance and resistivity
- factors affecting resistance
- series and parallel combination of resistors
- heating effect of electric current
- electric fuse
- electric power and electrical energy

---

## 1. Electric Current and Electric Circuit

### Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

If a net charge `Q` flows through any cross-section of a conductor in time `t`, then electric current `I` is:

```txt
I = Q / t
```

Where:

```txt
I = electric current
Q = electric charge
t = time
```

### SI Unit of Electric Current

The SI unit of electric current is **ampere (A)**.

```txt
1 ampere = 1 coulomb / 1 second
1 A = 1 C/s
```

A current of 1 A means that 1 C charge flows through a cross-section of a conductor in 1 second.

### Smaller Units of Current

```txt
1 mA = 10^-3 A
1 μA = 10^-6 A
```

### Electric Charge

The SI unit of electric charge is **coulomb (C)**.

Charge on one electron:

```txt
1 electron charge = 1.6 × 10^-19 C
```

Therefore:

```txt
1 C charge ≈ 6 × 10^18 electrons
```

### Direction of Electric Current

In a metallic conductor, electrons flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal.

But by convention, the direction of electric current is taken opposite to the direction of electron flow.

```txt
Electron flow: negative terminal → positive terminal
Conventional current: positive terminal → negative terminal
```

### Electric Circuit

An electric circuit is a continuous closed path through which electric current flows.

A simple electric circuit may contain:

- cell or battery
- conducting wires
- switch or plug key
- bulb or resistor
- ammeter

If the circuit is broken or the switch is off, current stops flowing and the bulb does not glow.

### Ammeter

An ammeter is used to measure electric current.

Important points:

- It is always connected in **series** with the circuit.
- It has very low resistance.
- Its symbol is usually written as `A` inside a circle.

## 2. Electric Potential and Potential Difference

### Why Charges Flow

Charges do not flow through a conductor on their own. They flow only when there is a difference in electric potential between two points.

A cell or battery creates this potential difference.

### Electric Potential Difference

The electric potential difference between two points in a current-carrying circuit is defined as the work done to move a unit charge from one point to another.

Formula:

```txt
V = W / Q
```

Where:

```txt
V = potential difference
W = work done
Q = charge
```

### SI Unit of Potential Difference

The SI unit of potential difference is **volt (V)**.

```txt
1 V = 1 J / 1 C
1 V = 1 J C^-1
```

If 1 J work is done to move 1 C charge between two points, then the potential difference between those two points is 1 V.

### Voltmeter

A voltmeter is used to measure potential difference.

Important points:

- It is always connected in **parallel** across the two points.
- It has very high resistance.
- Its symbol is usually written as `V` inside a circle.

## 4. Ohm's Law

### Statement of Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, the potential difference across the ends of a metallic conductor is directly proportional to the electric current flowing through it.

```txt
V ∝ I
```

Therefore:

```txt
V / I = constant
```

This constant is called resistance `R`.

So:

```txt
V = IR
```

This is Ohm's law.

### Resistance

Resistance is the property of a conductor by which it opposes the flow of electric charge through it.

Formula:

```txt
R = V / I
```

Where:

```txt
R = resistance
V = potential difference
I = current
```

### SI Unit of Resistance

The SI unit of resistance is **ohm (Ω)**.

```txt
1 Ω = 1 V / 1 A
```

If a potential difference of 1 V across a conductor produces a current of 1 A, then the resistance of the conductor is 1 Ω.

### Current Through a Resistor

From Ohm's law:

```txt
I = V / R
```

So, for a fixed potential difference:

```txt
Current is inversely proportional to resistance.
```

If resistance is doubled, current becomes half.

### V-I Graph

For an ohmic conductor, the graph between potential difference `V` and current `I` is a straight line passing through the origin.

This shows that:

```txt
V / I = constant
```

The constant ratio is resistance.

## 5. Variable Resistance and Rheostat

A variable resistor is a component used to change resistance in an electric circuit without changing the voltage source.

A rheostat is a device used to vary current in a circuit by changing resistance.

Use:

```txt
Increase resistance → current decreases
Decrease resistance → current increases
```

---

## 6. Factors Affecting Resistance of a Conductor

The resistance of a conductor depends on:

```txt
1. Length of conductor
2. Area of cross-section
3. Nature of material
4. Temperature
```

### Dependence on Length

Resistance is directly proportional to the length of the conductor.

```txt
R ∝ l
```

Longer wire has greater resistance.

### Dependence on Area of Cross-Section

Resistance is inversely proportional to the area of cross-section.

```txt
R ∝ 1 / A
```

Thicker wire has less resistance.

### Dependence on Material

Different materials offer different resistance. Good conductors like copper and aluminium have low resistance. Poor conductors and insulators have high resistance.

### Formula for Resistance

Combining length and area dependence:

```txt
R = ρl / A
```

Where:

```txt
R = resistance
ρ = resistivity
l = length of conductor
A = area of cross-section
```

---

## 7. Resistivity

Resistivity is a characteristic property of the material of a conductor.

Formula:

```txt
ρ = RA / l
```

### SI Unit of Resistivity

The SI unit of resistivity is:

```txt
Ω m
```

### Important Points About Resistivity

- Metals and alloys have very low resistivity.
- Insulators like rubber and glass have very high resistivity.
- Resistivity changes with temperature.
- Alloys usually have higher resistivity than their constituent metals.
- Alloys do not oxidise easily at high temperature, so they are used in heating devices.

### Examples of Materials and Their Use

| Material | Property / Use |
|---|---|
| Copper | Used in electrical wiring because of low resistivity |
| Aluminium | Used in transmission wires because it conducts well and is lighter |
| Tungsten | Used in bulb filaments because it has very high melting point |
| Nichrome | Used in heating elements because it has high resistivity and withstands high temperature |
| Glass, rubber, ebonite | Used as insulators because of high resistivity |

Resistors can be connected in two main ways:

```txt
1. Series combination
2. Parallel combination
```

## 9. Resistors in Series

### Meaning

When resistors are connected end-to-end in a single path, they are said to be connected in series.

Example:

```txt
R1 — R2 — R3
```

### Current in Series Combination

In a series combination, the same current flows through each resistor.

```txt
I = I1 = I2 = I3
```

### Potential Difference in Series Combination

Total potential difference is equal to the sum of potential differences across individual resistors.

```txt
V = V1 + V2 + V3
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
V1 = IR1
V2 = IR2
V3 = IR3
```

### Equivalent Resistance in Series

```txt
Rs = R1 + R2 + R3
```

For many resistors:

```txt
Rs = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
```

### Important Points

- Equivalent resistance in series is greater than any individual resistance.
- Current is same through all resistors.
- Voltage is divided among resistors.
- If one component fails, the entire circuit breaks.

### Disadvantage of Series Circuit

Series circuits are not suitable for domestic wiring because:

- all appliances get the same current, even though they need different currents
- if one appliance stops working, the entire circuit breaks
- appliances cannot be operated independently

---

## 10. Resistors in Parallel

### Meaning

When resistors are connected between the same two points, they are said to be connected in parallel.

Example:

```txt
R1, R2, R3 connected between points X and Y
```

### Potential Difference in Parallel Combination

In a parallel combination, the potential difference across each resistor is the same.

```txt
V = V1 = V2 = V3
```

### Current in Parallel Combination

Total current is equal to the sum of currents through individual branches.

```txt
I = I1 + I2 + I3
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
I1 = V / R1
I2 = V / R2
I3 = V / R3
```

### Equivalent Resistance in Parallel

```txt
1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
```

For many resistors:

```txt
1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...
```

### Important Points

- Equivalent resistance in parallel is less than the smallest individual resistance.
- Voltage is same across all resistors.
- Current divides in different branches.
- Appliances can work independently.
- Parallel combination is used in domestic wiring.

### Advantages of Parallel Combination in Domestic Circuits

Domestic appliances are connected in parallel because:

- each appliance gets the same voltage
- each appliance can draw current according to its resistance
- appliances can be switched on and off independently
- if one appliance fails, others continue to work
- total resistance decreases, allowing required current to flow

## 11. Heating Effect of Electric Current

When electric current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat energy.

This is called the heating effect of electric current.

Examples:

- electric heater
- electric iron
- toaster
- electric kettle
- electric bulb filament
- electric fuse

### Electrical Energy Supplied

If current `I` flows through a resistor for time `t`, and potential difference across it is `V`, then electrical energy supplied is:

```txt
W = VIt
```

### Heat Produced

If electrical energy is completely converted into heat:

```txt
H = VIt
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
V = IR
```

Therefore:

```txt
H = I²Rt
```

This is known as **Joule's law of heating**.

### Joule's Law of Heating

The heat produced in a resistor is:

```txt
1. directly proportional to the square of current
2. directly proportional to resistance
3. directly proportional to time
```

Formula:

```txt
H = I²Rt
```

Where:

```txt
H = heat produced
I = current
R = resistance
t = time
```

### Why Heater Element Gets Hot But Connecting Wire Does Not

The heating element of a heater has high resistance, so more heat is produced in it.

Connecting wires have low resistance, so very little heat is produced in them.

Since:

```txt
H ∝ R
```

for the same current and time, the high-resistance element becomes hot, but low-resistance connecting wire remains almost cool.

---

## 12. Practical Applications of Heating Effect

### Electric Heating Devices

Devices based on Joule's heating include:

- electric iron
- electric heater
- toaster
- electric kettle
- electric oven

These devices use high-resistance heating elements, often made of alloys such as nichrome.

### Electric Bulb

In an electric bulb, the filament becomes very hot and emits light.

Tungsten is used for bulb filaments because:

- it has a very high melting point
- it can become white hot without melting
- it is strong at high temperature

Bulbs are usually filled with inactive gases like nitrogen and argon to increase filament life.

### Electric Fuse

An electric fuse is a safety device used to protect circuits and appliances from excessive current.

A fuse is connected in series with the device.

It is made of a metal or alloy wire having:

- suitable low melting point
- suitable resistance

When current exceeds the safe limit:

```txt
high current → fuse wire heats up → fuse wire melts → circuit breaks
```

This prevents damage to appliances and reduces fire risk.

Common fuse ratings:

```txt
1 A, 2 A, 3 A, 5 A, 10 A
```

Example:

If an electric iron uses 1 kW power at 220 V:

```txt
I = P / V
I = 1000 / 220
I ≈ 4.54 A
```

So, a 5 A fuse should be used.

## 13. Electric Power

### Definition

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or dissipated in an electric circuit.

Formula:

```txt
P = VI
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
P = I²R
P = V² / R
```

Where:

```txt
P = electric power
V = potential difference
I = current
R = resistance
```

### SI Unit of Power

The SI unit of electric power is **watt (W)**.

```txt
1 W = 1 V × 1 A
1 W = 1 VA
```

One watt is the power consumed by a device when 1 A current flows through it at a potential difference of 1 V.

### Kilowatt

```txt
1 kW = 1000 W
```

### Electrical Energy

Electrical energy is:

```txt
Energy = Power × Time
```

If power is in watt and time is in second:

```txt
Energy = watt second = joule
```

If power is in kilowatt and time is in hour:

```txt
Energy = kilowatt hour = kWh
```

### Commercial Unit of Electrical Energy

The commercial unit of electrical energy is **kilowatt hour (kWh)**.

It is commonly called one **unit**.

```txt
1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10^6 J
```

## 16. Important Definitions

### Electric Current

The rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor is called electric current.

### Electric Circuit

A continuous closed path through which electric current flows is called an electric circuit.

### Potential Difference

The work done per unit charge to move a charge between two points is called potential difference.

### One Volt

The potential difference between two points is one volt if one joule of work is done to move one coulomb charge from one point to another.

### Resistance

Resistance is the property of a conductor by which it opposes the flow of electric current.

### One Ohm

A conductor has resistance of one ohm if a potential difference of one volt across its ends produces a current of one ampere through it.

### Resistivity

Resistivity is the resistance of a conductor of unit length and unit area of cross-section made of a given material.

### Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends.

### Joule's Law of Heating

The heat produced in a resistor is directly proportional to the square of current, resistance, and time.

### Electric Power

Electric power is the rate of consumption or dissipation of electrical energy.

### Kilowatt Hour

One kilowatt hour is the electrical energy consumed by a device of power 1 kW when it is used for 1 hour.

## 17. Key Concept Comparisons

### Ammeter vs Voltmeter

| Ammeter | Voltmeter |
|---|---|
| Measures current | Measures potential difference |
| Connected in series | Connected in parallel |
| Low resistance | High resistance |
| Unit reading in ampere | Unit reading in volt |

### Series vs Parallel Combination

| Series Combination | Parallel Combination |
|---|---|
| Same current flows through all resistors | Same voltage across all resistors |
| Total resistance increases | Total resistance decreases |
| `Rs = R1 + R2 + R3` | `1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3` |
| If one component fails, circuit breaks | Other components work even if one fails |
| Not suitable for domestic wiring | Used in domestic wiring |

### Conductor vs Resistor vs Insulator

| Type | Property |
|---|---|
| Good conductor | Low resistance, allows current easily |
| Resistor | Offers controlled resistance |
| Insulator | Very high resistance, does not allow current easily |

## 18. Solved Numerical Patterns

### Pattern 1: Charge from Current and Time

Formula:

```txt
Q = It
```

Example:

If current is `0.5 A` for `10 min`:

```txt
t = 10 min = 600 s
Q = 0.5 × 600
Q = 300 C
```

### Pattern 2: Work from Potential Difference and Charge

Formula:

```txt
W = VQ
```

Example:

If `V = 12 V` and `Q = 2 C`:

```txt
W = 12 × 2
W = 24 J
```

### Pattern 3: Current from Voltage and Resistance

Formula:

```txt
I = V / R
```

Example:

If `V = 220 V` and `R = 1200 Ω`:

```txt
I = 220 / 1200
I ≈ 0.18 A
```

### Pattern 4: Resistance from Ohm's Law

Formula:

```txt
R = V / I
```

Example:

If `V = 60 V` and `I = 4 A`:

```txt
R = 60 / 4
R = 15 Ω
```

### Pattern 5: Resistivity

Formula:

```txt
ρ = RA / l
```

For a cylindrical wire:

```txt
A = πd² / 4
```

So:

```txt
ρ = Rπd² / 4l
```

### Pattern 6: Series Resistance

Formula:

```txt
Rs = R1 + R2 + R3
```

Example:

```txt
20 Ω + 4 Ω = 24 Ω
```

### Pattern 7: Parallel Resistance

Formula:

```txt
1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
```

Example:

For `5 Ω`, `10 Ω`, and `30 Ω`:

```txt
1/Rp = 1/5 + 1/10 + 1/30
1/Rp = 6/30 + 3/30 + 1/30
1/Rp = 10/30 = 1/3
Rp = 3 Ω
```

### Pattern 8: Heat Produced

Formula:

```txt
H = I²Rt
```

Example:

If `I = 5 A`, `R = 20 Ω`, and `t = 30 s`:

```txt
H = 5² × 20 × 30
H = 25 × 20 × 30
H = 15000 J
```

### Pattern 9: Electric Power

Formula:

```txt
P = VI
```

Example:

If `V = 220 V` and `I = 0.50 A`:

```txt
P = 220 × 0.50
P = 110 W
```

### Pattern 10: Energy Bill

Formula:

```txt
Energy in kWh = Power in kW × Time in hours
Cost = Energy × Rate
```

Example:

A `400 W` refrigerator runs `8 h/day` for `30 days`.

```txt
Power = 400 W = 0.4 kW
Time = 8 × 30 = 240 h
Energy = 0.4 × 240 = 96 kWh
If rate = ₹3/kWh
Cost = 96 × 3 = ₹288
```

## 23. Long Answer Questions

### Q1. Explain Ohm's law with formula and graph.

Ohm's law states that at constant temperature, the potential difference across the ends of a metallic conductor is directly proportional to the current flowing through it.

```txt
V ∝ I
V = IR
```

Here `R` is resistance.

If a graph is plotted between `V` and `I`, it is a straight line passing through the origin. This shows that `V/I` is constant.

### Q2. Explain the factors on which resistance of a conductor depends.

Resistance depends on:

1. Length: resistance increases with length.
2. Area of cross-section: resistance decreases when area increases.
3. Nature of material: different materials have different resistivity.
4. Temperature: resistance changes with temperature.

Formula:

```txt
R = ρl / A
```

### Q3. Derive the formula for equivalent resistance in series.

In series, current is same through all resistors.

```txt
V = V1 + V2 + V3
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
V = IRs
V1 = IR1
V2 = IR2
V3 = IR3
```

Therefore:

```txt
IRs = IR1 + IR2 + IR3
Rs = R1 + R2 + R3
```

### Q4. Derive the formula for equivalent resistance in parallel.

In parallel, potential difference is same across all resistors.

```txt
I = I1 + I2 + I3
```

Using Ohm's law:

```txt
I = V/Rp
I1 = V/R1
I2 = V/R2
I3 = V/R3
```

Therefore:

```txt
V/Rp = V/R1 + V/R2 + V/R3
1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
```

### Q5. Explain Joule's law of heating.

When current flows through a resistor, heat is produced.

The heat produced is:

```txt
H = I²Rt
```

It shows that heat produced is directly proportional to:

```txt
1. square of current
2. resistance
3. time
```

This is Joule's law of heating.

### Q6. Explain the working of an electric fuse.

An electric fuse is connected in series with an appliance. It is made of a wire having a suitable low melting point.

When current exceeds the safe limit, heat produced in the fuse wire increases. The fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit. This stops current and protects the appliance.

### Q7. Why is parallel combination preferred over series combination in household wiring?

Parallel combination is preferred because:

- each appliance gets full supply voltage
- appliances can work independently
- failure of one appliance does not affect others
- different appliances can draw current according to their resistance
- total resistance decreases and required current can flow
