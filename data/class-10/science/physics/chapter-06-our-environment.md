# Chapter 5: Our Environment

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains how living organisms interact with each other and with their physical surroundings. It also explains how human activities affect the environment through waste generation, pollution, ozone layer depletion, and improper disposal of non-biodegradable substances.

The main focus of this chapter is:

- biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste
- ecosystem and its components
- producers, consumers, and decomposers
- food chains and food webs
- trophic levels
- flow of energy in an ecosystem
- biological magnification
- ozone layer and its depletion
- waste management and environmental impact of human activities

---

## 1. Environment

The environment includes everything around us that affects living organisms. It includes living components such as plants, animals, microorganisms, and humans, and non-living components such as air, water, soil, temperature, minerals, sunlight, and rainfall.

Human activities affect the environment directly or indirectly. Waste disposal, use of chemicals, pollution, and overuse of resources can disturb the balance of natural systems.

---

## 2. Waste in the Environment

In daily life, humans produce many kinds of waste materials such as:

- leftover food
- vegetable peels
- used tea leaves
- paper
- plastic packets
- empty medicine bottles
- broken shoes
- old clothes
- metal and glass objects

When these waste materials are thrown into the environment, some decompose naturally, while others remain unchanged for a long time.

---

## 3. Biodegradable Substances

Biodegradable substances are materials that can be broken down by biological processes.

They are decomposed by microorganisms such as bacteria and fungi.

Examples:

- vegetable peels
- leftover food
- paper
- cotton cloth
- dead plants and animals
- cow dung
- fruit peels

These substances return nutrients to the soil after decomposition.

### Why Some Substances Are Biodegradable

Microorganisms produce specific enzymes that break down specific types of substances. If microorganisms can digest or decompose a substance using enzymes, that substance is biodegradable.

---

## 4. Non-Biodegradable Substances

Non-biodegradable substances are materials that cannot be broken down by biological processes.

They either remain in the environment for a very long time or harm living organisms and ecosystems.

Examples:

- plastic
- glass
- metal cans
- polythene bags
- synthetic chemicals
- pesticides
- electronic waste

These substances may undergo physical changes due to heat and pressure, but they do not decompose easily under normal environmental conditions.

### Effects of Non-Biodegradable Substances

Non-biodegradable substances can:

- accumulate in the environment
- pollute soil and water
- harm animals if swallowed
- enter food chains
- cause biological magnification
- create long-term waste disposal problems

---

## 5. Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a system formed by the interaction of living organisms with each other and with the physical environment.

An ecosystem includes:

- all living organisms in an area
- all non-living physical factors in that area
- interactions between living and non-living components

Examples of ecosystems:

- forest
- pond
- lake
- garden
- crop field
- aquarium

A forest, pond, and lake are natural ecosystems. A garden, crop field, and aquarium are human-made ecosystems.

---

## 6. Components of an Ecosystem

An ecosystem has two major components:

1. Biotic components
2. Abiotic components

---

## 7. Biotic Components

Biotic components are the living components of an ecosystem.

They include:

- plants
- animals
- humans
- bacteria
- fungi
- other microorganisms

Biotic components are divided into three main groups:

1. Producers
2. Consumers
3. Decomposers

---

## 8. Abiotic Components

Abiotic components are the non-living physical and chemical components of an ecosystem.

Examples:

- sunlight
- air
- water
- soil
- temperature
- rainfall
- minerals

Abiotic factors affect the growth, reproduction, and survival of living organisms.

---

## 9. Producers

Producers are organisms that make their own food.

Green plants and blue-green algae are producers because they prepare food by photosynthesis using:

- carbon dioxide
- water
- sunlight
- chlorophyll

They convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in food.

Examples:

- grass
- trees
- algae
- green plants

Producers form the first trophic level in a food chain.

---

## 10. Consumers

Consumers are organisms that depend directly or indirectly on producers for food.

Consumers cannot prepare their own food.

Types of consumers:

1. Herbivores
2. Carnivores
3. Omnivores
4. Parasites

### Herbivores

Herbivores eat plants.

Examples:

- deer
- cow
- goat
- rabbit
- grasshopper

They are also called primary consumers.

### Carnivores

Carnivores eat other animals.

Examples:

- lion
- tiger
- snake
- frog
- eagle

### Omnivores

Omnivores eat both plants and animals.

Examples:

- human beings
- crow
- bear

### Parasites

Parasites live on or inside another organism and obtain food from it.

Examples:

- lice
- tapeworm
- Cuscuta

---

## 11. Decomposers

Decomposers are organisms that break down dead plants, dead animals, and organic waste into simpler inorganic substances.

Examples:

- bacteria
- fungi

Decomposers are important because they:

- clean the environment
- recycle nutrients
- convert complex organic matter into simple substances
- return minerals to the soil
- maintain the natural balance of ecosystems

Without decomposers, dead plants and animals would keep accumulating, and nutrients would not return to the soil.

---

## 12. Food Chain

A food chain is a sequence of organisms in which one organism eats another organism.

It shows the transfer of food and energy from one organism to another.

Example:

```txt
Grass → Deer → Tiger
```

Another example:

```txt
Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
```

Aquatic example:

```txt
Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small Fish → Large Fish
```

---

## 13. Trophic Levels

Each step of a food chain is called a trophic level.

### Example

```txt
Grass → Deer → Tiger
```

| Organism | Trophic Level | Role |
|---|---:|---|
| Grass | First trophic level | Producer |
| Deer | Second trophic level | Primary consumer |
| Tiger | Third trophic level | Secondary consumer / top carnivore |

### General Trophic Level Order

```txt
Producers
↓
Primary consumers
↓
Secondary consumers
↓
Tertiary consumers
```

---

## 14. Food Web

In nature, most organisms are part of more than one food chain.

A network of interconnected food chains is called a food web.

Example:

```txt
Grass → Goat → Human
Grass → Deer → Tiger
Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
```

Food webs are more realistic than simple food chains because organisms usually have multiple food sources and multiple predators.

---

## 15. Flow of Energy in an Ecosystem

Energy flows from one trophic level to the next.

The main source of energy in most ecosystems is the Sun.

Green plants capture solar energy and convert it into chemical energy through photosynthesis.

Energy flow in an ecosystem is unidirectional. This means energy flows in only one direction:

```txt
Sun → Producers → Consumers → Decomposers
```

Energy does not return from consumers back to the Sun or from herbivores back to producers.

---

## 16. Ten Percent Law

Only about 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next trophic level.

The remaining energy is lost mainly as heat or used in life processes such as respiration, digestion, growth, movement, and reproduction.

Example:

```txt
Producers: 10,000 J
Primary consumers: 1,000 J
Secondary consumers: 100 J
Tertiary consumers: 10 J
```

Because energy decreases at each trophic level, food chains usually have only three or four steps.

---

## 17. Why Food Chains Are Usually Short

Food chains are usually limited to three or four trophic levels because a large amount of energy is lost at every step.

After the fourth trophic level, very little usable energy remains for the next level.

This is why long food chains are rare in nature.

---

## 18. Biological Magnification

Biological magnification is the increase in concentration of harmful non-biodegradable chemicals at successive trophic levels of a food chain.

Non-biodegradable chemicals such as pesticides enter the food chain through soil or water.

They are absorbed by plants and then passed to animals and humans.

Since these chemicals do not break down easily, they accumulate at each trophic level.

The highest concentration is found in top consumers.

### Example

```txt
Pesticides in water
↓
Aquatic plants
↓
Small fish
↓
Large fish
↓
Birds / Humans
```

Humans are often at the top of food chains, so harmful chemicals may accumulate in the human body in high concentration.

### Why Biological Magnification Is Dangerous

It can cause:

- poisoning
- reproductive problems
- nervous system damage
- long-term health effects
- harm to wildlife and humans

---

## 19. Human Activities and Environment

Humans affect the environment through:

- excessive use of plastic
- improper waste disposal
- use of pesticides and chemicals
- industrial waste
- sewage discharge
- air and water pollution
- use of CFCs
- overconsumption of resources

Human activities can disturb ecosystem balance and damage natural cycles.

## 20. Ozone Layer

Ozone is a molecule made of three oxygen atoms.

Formula:

```txt
O3
```

Oxygen gas has two oxygen atoms:

```txt
O2
```

Ozone is poisonous near the surface of the Earth, but in the upper atmosphere it performs an important protective function.

The ozone layer protects the Earth from harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun.

Ultraviolet radiation can cause:

- skin cancer
- eye damage
- damage to plants
- harm to animals
- disturbance in ecosystems

---

## 21. Formation of Ozone

Ozone is formed in the upper atmosphere by the action of ultraviolet radiation on oxygen molecules.

```txt
O2 --UV radiation--> O + O
O + O2 → O3
```

High-energy ultraviolet radiation splits oxygen molecules into oxygen atoms. These oxygen atoms combine with oxygen molecules to form ozone.

---

## 22. Ozone Layer Depletion

Ozone layer depletion means reduction in the amount of ozone in the upper atmosphere.

From around 1980, the amount of ozone in the atmosphere started decreasing rapidly.

The main cause of ozone depletion is chlorofluorocarbons, also called CFCs.

CFCs are human-made chemicals used in:

- refrigerators
- air conditioners
- fire extinguishers
- aerosol sprays

CFCs damage the ozone layer and allow more harmful ultraviolet radiation to reach the Earth.

In 1987, an agreement under the United Nations Environment Programme was made to limit CFC production.

---

## 23. Waste Management

Waste management means proper collection, separation, treatment, recycling, and disposal of waste.

Improper waste disposal causes:

- soil pollution
- water pollution
- air pollution
- spread of diseases
- harm to animals
- blockage of drains
- damage to ecosystems

Waste should be separated into:

1. Biodegradable waste
2. Non-biodegradable waste

### Biodegradable Waste Management

Biodegradable waste can be managed by:

- composting
- biogas production
- controlled decomposition
- using organic waste as manure

### Non-Biodegradable Waste Management

Non-biodegradable waste can be managed by:

- reducing use
- reusing materials
- recycling
- safe disposal
- avoiding single-use plastics

---

## 24. Disposable Items and Environmental Impact

Disposable items such as plastic cups, paper cups, plastic bags, and packaging materials increase waste.

Plastic disposable items are harmful because they are non-biodegradable.

Paper cups are better than plastic cups in some cases, but making large amounts of paper cups also uses trees, water, and energy.

Therefore, reusable materials are usually better than disposable materials.

Examples of environment-friendly choices:

- use cloth bags
- avoid single-use plastic
- switch off lights and fans when not needed
- walk or cycle short distances
- reuse containers
- recycle paper, plastic, glass, and metal
- segregate waste at source

---

## 25. Electronic Waste

Electronic waste includes discarded electronic items such as:

- mobile phones
- computers
- chargers
- batteries
- wires
- televisions
- electronic appliances

Electronic waste may contain harmful substances that can pollute soil and water if not disposed of safely.

E-waste should be handled through proper recycling and authorized collection systems.

## Long Answer Questions

### 1. Explain the role of decomposers in an ecosystem.

Decomposers break down dead plants, dead animals, and organic waste into simpler inorganic substances. These substances return to the soil and are reused by plants. Decomposers clean the environment and maintain nutrient cycling. Without decomposers, dead organisms and waste would accumulate, and nutrients would not return to the soil.

---

### 2. Explain energy flow in an ecosystem.

The Sun is the main source of energy in most ecosystems. Producers capture solar energy and convert it into chemical energy through photosynthesis. This energy passes from producers to herbivores, then to carnivores, and finally to decomposers. Energy flow is unidirectional. At each trophic level, a large amount of energy is lost as heat. Only about 10% of energy is transferred to the next trophic level.

---

### 3. Why are food chains usually limited to three or four trophic levels?

At each trophic level, only about 10% of energy is passed to the next level. The remaining energy is lost as heat or used in life processes. After three or four levels, very little energy remains. Therefore, food chains are usually short.

---

### 4. Explain biological magnification with an example.

Biological magnification is the increase in concentration of harmful non-biodegradable chemicals at successive trophic levels. For example, pesticides used in fields may enter soil and water. Plants absorb them. Herbivores eat plants, carnivores eat herbivores, and humans may eat plants or animals. Since pesticides do not decompose easily, their concentration increases at each level. The highest concentration is found in top consumers.

---

### 5. Explain ozone layer depletion.

The ozone layer is present in the upper atmosphere and protects Earth from harmful ultraviolet radiation. Ozone is formed when ultraviolet radiation breaks oxygen molecules into oxygen atoms, which then combine with oxygen molecules to form ozone. Chemicals such as CFCs damage the ozone layer. Due to ozone depletion, more harmful UV radiation reaches Earth, which can cause skin cancer and harm living organisms.

---

### 6. How do human activities affect the environment?

Human activities affect the environment through pollution, waste generation, excessive use of plastics, pesticides, industrial waste, sewage discharge, CFC emission, and overuse of natural resources. These activities disturb ecosystems, pollute air and water, damage the ozone layer, and create waste disposal problems.

---

### 7. What can we do to reduce waste disposal problems?

We can reduce waste disposal problems by:

- separating biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste
- composting organic waste
- recycling paper, plastic, glass, and metals
- reducing use of single-use plastic
- reusing containers and bags
- using cloth bags
- disposing of e-waste safely
- spreading awareness about waste management
