# Chapter 2: Human Eye and Colourful World

## Chapter Overview

This chapter explains how the human eye works, how common vision defects occur, how lenses correct those defects, and how light produces natural optical phenomena such as dispersion, rainbow, twinkling of stars, blue sky, and red sunrise or sunset.

The chapter connects earlier learning about refraction, lenses, and scattering with real-life observations.

## Core Ideas

- The human eye acts like a camera.
- The eye lens forms a real and inverted image on the retina.
- The retina converts light into electrical signals and sends them to the brain through the optic nerve.
- The eye can change the focal length of its lens to see nearby and distant objects clearly.
- This ability is called accommodation.
- Common vision defects include myopia, hypermetropia, and presbyopia.
- A prism refracts and deviates light.
- White light is made of seven colours.
- Splitting of white light into colours is called dispersion.
- The rainbow is a natural spectrum caused by refraction, dispersion, and internal reflection in water droplets.
- Atmospheric refraction causes twinkling of stars and apparent early sunrise and delayed sunset.
- Scattering of light explains the blue colour of the sky and the reddish colour of the sun during sunrise and sunset.

# 1. Human Eye

## 1.1 Importance of the Human Eye

The human eye is one of the most important sense organs. It allows us to see objects, colours, shape, distance, and movement.

The eye uses light reflected from objects. This light enters the eye and forms an image on the retina.

## 1.2 Structure of the Human Eye

The human eye is almost spherical in shape. Its diameter is about **2.3 cm**.

### Main Parts of the Eye

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Cornea | Transparent front part of the eye. Most refraction of light occurs here. |
| Iris | Dark muscular diaphragm behind the cornea. Controls pupil size. |
| Pupil | Opening through which light enters the eye. |
| Eye lens / Crystalline lens | Focuses light on the retina. |
| Ciliary muscles | Change the curvature and focal length of the eye lens. |
| Retina | Light-sensitive screen where image is formed. |
| Optic nerve | Carries electrical signals from retina to brain. |
| Vitreous humour | Transparent jelly-like fluid inside the eye. |

## 1.3 Working of the Human Eye

1. Light enters the eye through the cornea.
2. The pupil controls the amount of light entering the eye.
3. The eye lens focuses the light on the retina.
4. A real and inverted image is formed on the retina.
5. Light-sensitive cells on the retina get activated.
6. These cells produce electrical signals.
7. The optic nerve carries these signals to the brain.
8. The brain interprets the signals and we see the object upright.

## 1.4 Role of Pupil and Iris

The pupil works like an adjustable aperture.

- In bright light, the iris contracts and makes the pupil smaller.
- In dim light, the iris expands and makes the pupil larger.

This controls how much light enters the eye.

## 1.5 Why Do We Have Two Eyes?

Having two eyes gives many advantages:

- Wider field of view.
- Better ability to detect dim objects.
- Depth perception.
- Three-dimensional vision.
- Better judgement of distance.

A single eye gives a flatter view, while two eyes allow us to judge how near or far an object is.

# 2. Power of Accommodation

## 2.1 Meaning

The ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length to see nearby and distant objects clearly is called **power of accommodation**.

## 2.2 How Accommodation Happens

The eye lens is flexible. Its curvature changes with the help of ciliary muscles.

### For Distant Objects

- Ciliary muscles relax.
- Eye lens becomes thin.
- Focal length increases.
- Distant object is focused clearly on the retina.

### For Nearby Objects

- Ciliary muscles contract.
- Eye lens becomes thicker.
- Focal length decreases.
- Nearby object is focused clearly on the retina.

## 2.3 Near Point

The minimum distance at which an object can be seen clearly without strain is called the **near point** or **least distance of distinct vision**.

For a normal adult human eye:

```txt
Near point = 25 cm
```

## 2.4 Far Point

The farthest point up to which the eye can see objects clearly is called the **far point**.

For a normal human eye:

```txt
Far point = Infinity
```

So a normal eye can see clearly from:

```txt
25 cm to infinity
```

## 2.5 Cataract

Sometimes, especially in old age, the crystalline lens becomes cloudy or milky. This condition is called **cataract**.

Effects:

- Vision becomes blurred.
- Partial or complete loss of vision may occur.

Treatment:

- Cataract can usually be corrected by surgery.

# 3. Defects of Vision and Their Correction

The eye may lose its ability to focus light properly on the retina. Such problems are called **defects of vision** or **refractive defects**.

The three common defects are:

1. Myopia
2. Hypermetropia
3. Presbyopia

---

## 3.1 Myopia: Near-Sightedness

### Meaning

A person with myopia can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects clearly.

### Image Formation in Myopia

In a myopic eye, the image of a distant object is formed **in front of the retina** instead of on the retina.

### Causes of Myopia

Myopia may occur due to:

1. Excessive curvature of the eye lens.
2. Elongation of the eyeball.

### Correction of Myopia

Myopia is corrected by using a **concave lens** of suitable power.

A concave lens diverges light rays before they enter the eye. This shifts the image back onto the retina.

```txt
Myopia correction = Concave lens
```

---

## 3.2 Hypermetropia: Far-Sightedness

### Meaning

A person with hypermetropia can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects clearly.

### Image Formation in Hypermetropia

In a hypermetropic eye, the image of a nearby object is formed **behind the retina** instead of on the retina.

### Causes of Hypermetropia

Hypermetropia may occur due to:

1. Focal length of the eye lens becoming too large.
2. Eyeball becoming too short.

### Correction of Hypermetropia

Hypermetropia is corrected by using a **convex lens** of suitable power.

A convex lens converges light rays before they enter the eye. This helps the eye form the image on the retina.

```txt
Hypermetropia correction = Convex lens
```

---

## 3.3 Presbyopia

### Meaning

Presbyopia is an age-related defect in which the eye gradually loses its power of accommodation.

### Cause

It occurs due to:

- Weakening of ciliary muscles.
- Decrease in flexibility of the eye lens.

### Effect

A person has difficulty seeing nearby objects clearly.

### Correction

Presbyopia is corrected using suitable lenses.

Some people may suffer from both myopia and hypermetropia. Such people may require **bifocal lenses**.

### Bifocal Lens

A bifocal lens has two parts:

| Part | Lens Type | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Upper part | Concave lens | For distant vision |
| Lower part | Convex lens | For near vision |

Modern correction may also use contact lenses or surgery.

# 4. Refraction of Light Through a Prism

## 4.1 Prism

A prism is a transparent optical object with two triangular bases and three rectangular side faces.

A triangular glass prism has:

- Two triangular bases.
- Three rectangular lateral surfaces.
- Refracting faces inclined to each other.
- Angle between two refracting faces called the **angle of prism**.

## 4.2 Refraction Through Prism

When light passes through a glass prism:

1. At the first surface, light goes from air to glass and bends towards the normal.
2. At the second surface, light goes from glass to air and bends away from the normal.
3. The emergent ray is deviated from the original direction.

The angle between the incident ray's original direction and the emergent ray is called the **angle of deviation**.

## 4.3 Terms Related to Prism

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Incident ray | Ray entering the prism |
| Refracted ray | Ray inside the prism |
| Emergent ray | Ray coming out of the prism |
| Angle of incidence | Angle between incident ray and normal |
| Angle of refraction | Angle between refracted ray and normal |
| Angle of emergence | Angle between emergent ray and normal |
| Angle of prism | Angle between two refracting faces |
| Angle of deviation | Angle between original incident direction and emergent ray |

# 5. Dispersion of White Light

## 5.1 Meaning

The splitting of white light into its component colours is called **dispersion of light**.

When white light passes through a glass prism, it splits into seven colours.

## 5.2 Spectrum

The band of coloured components of light is called a **spectrum**.

The colours are:

```txt
Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red
```

Shortcut:

```txt
VIBGYOR
```

## 5.3 Order of Colours

| Order | Colour |
|---|---|
| 1 | Violet |
| 2 | Indigo |
| 3 | Blue |
| 4 | Green |
| 5 | Yellow |
| 6 | Orange |
| 7 | Red |

## 5.4 Why Does Dispersion Occur?

White light contains different colours. Each colour bends by a different amount when passing through a prism.

- Red light bends the least.
- Violet light bends the most.

This difference in bending separates white light into its component colours.

## 5.5 Newton's Experiment

Newton was the first to obtain the spectrum of sunlight using a glass prism.

He also used a second inverted prism to recombine the spectrum. The seven colours again combined to form white light.

This proved that sunlight is made of seven colours.

# 6. Rainbow

## 6.1 Meaning

A rainbow is a natural spectrum seen in the sky after rain.

It is formed due to sunlight interacting with tiny water droplets in the atmosphere.

## 6.2 Formation of Rainbow

A rainbow is formed due to:

1. Refraction of sunlight when it enters a water droplet.
2. Dispersion of white light into different colours.
3. Internal reflection inside the droplet.
4. Refraction again when light exits the droplet.

Water droplets act like tiny prisms.

## 6.3 Direction of Rainbow

A rainbow is always formed in the direction opposite to the Sun.

To see a rainbow:

- The Sun should be behind the observer.
- Water droplets should be in front of the observer.

# 7. Atmospheric Refraction

## 7.1 Meaning

The refraction of light by the Earth's atmosphere is called **atmospheric refraction**.

The atmosphere consists of layers of air with different densities and refractive indices. Because of this, light bends gradually while passing through the atmosphere.

## 7.2 Twinkling of Stars

Stars twinkle due to atmospheric refraction.

### Explanation

- Stars are very far away and behave like point sources of light.
- Their light passes through different layers of the atmosphere.
- These layers have changing refractive indices.
- So the path of starlight keeps changing.
- The apparent position and brightness of the star fluctuate.
- This makes the star appear to twinkle.

## 7.3 Why Planets Do Not Twinkle

Planets are much closer to Earth than stars. They appear as extended sources of light, not point sources.

Light from different points of a planet undergoes atmospheric refraction, but the variations average out.

Therefore, planets generally do not twinkle.

## 7.4 Advance Sunrise and Delayed Sunset

Due to atmospheric refraction:

- The Sun is visible about **2 minutes before actual sunrise**.
- The Sun remains visible about **2 minutes after actual sunset**.

So the day appears about **4 minutes longer**.

## 7.5 Flattening of the Sun Near Horizon

At sunrise and sunset, atmospheric refraction makes the Sun's disc appear slightly flattened.

# 8. Scattering of Light

## 8.1 Meaning

Scattering of light occurs when light interacts with small particles and gets redirected in different directions.

Scattering explains many natural phenomena, such as:

- Blue colour of the sky.
- Red colour of the Sun at sunrise and sunset.
- Visibility of light beams in fog, smoke, or dust.

## 8.2 Tyndall Effect

The scattering of light by colloidal particles is called the **Tyndall effect**.

Examples:

- A beam of sunlight visible in a dusty room.
- Sunlight passing through forest mist.
- Headlight beams visible in fog.
- Light path visible in a colloidal solution.

## 8.3 Colour of Scattered Light

The colour of scattered light depends on the size of scattering particles.

| Particle Size | Scattered Light |
|---|---|
| Very small particles | Mainly blue light |
| Larger particles | Longer wavelength light |
| Very large particles | Almost white light |

---

# 9. Why Is the Sky Blue?

The sky appears blue due to scattering of sunlight by tiny air molecules and fine particles in the atmosphere.

Blue light has a shorter wavelength than red light. Shorter wavelengths scatter more strongly.

So, when sunlight passes through the atmosphere, blue light scatters more than red light. This scattered blue light enters our eyes and makes the sky appear blue.

If Earth had no atmosphere, there would be no scattering and the sky would appear black.

This is why astronauts see the sky as black from space.

---

# 10. Why Does the Sun Appear Red During Sunrise and Sunset?

During sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels a longer distance through the atmosphere before reaching our eyes.

Most of the blue and shorter wavelength light is scattered away.

Only longer wavelength light, mainly red and orange, reaches our eyes.

Therefore, the Sun appears reddish during sunrise and sunset.

At noon, sunlight travels a shorter path through the atmosphere. Less blue light is scattered away, so the Sun appears nearly white.

---

# 11. Why Are Danger Signals Red?

Danger signals are red because red light has a longer wavelength and is scattered the least by fog, smoke, or dust.

So red light can travel longer distances and remains visible from far away.

# Long Answer Questions

## 1. Explain the structure and working of the human eye.

The human eye is nearly spherical and works like a camera. Light enters through the cornea, and most refraction happens at the cornea. The iris controls the size of the pupil, and the pupil controls the amount of light entering the eye. The eye lens focuses light on the retina. The retina contains light-sensitive cells that convert light into electrical signals. These signals are carried to the brain by the optic nerve. The brain interprets these signals and helps us see the object.

## 2. Explain myopia with causes and correction.

Myopia is a defect in which a person can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects clearly. In a myopic eye, the image of a distant object forms in front of the retina. It may occur because the eyeball becomes elongated or the eye lens becomes too curved. It is corrected using a concave lens of suitable power. The concave lens diverges light rays and helps the eye form the image on the retina.

## 3. Explain hypermetropia with causes and correction.

Hypermetropia is a defect in which a person can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects clearly. In a hypermetropic eye, the image of a nearby object forms behind the retina. It may occur due to a short eyeball or the focal length of the eye lens becoming too large. It is corrected using a convex lens of suitable power. The convex lens converges light rays and helps focus the image on the retina.

## 4. Explain dispersion of white light through a prism.

White light is made of seven colours. When white light passes through a glass prism, different colours bend by different amounts. Red bends the least and violet bends the most. As a result, white light splits into a band of seven colours called a spectrum. This splitting of white light is called dispersion.

## 5. Explain the formation of a rainbow.

A rainbow is formed when sunlight passes through tiny water droplets in the atmosphere. When sunlight enters a droplet, it is refracted and dispersed into different colours. The light then undergoes internal reflection inside the droplet and refracts again while coming out. The separated colours reach the observer's eye and form a rainbow. A rainbow is always seen in the direction opposite to the Sun.

## 6. Explain why stars twinkle but planets do not.

Stars are very far from Earth and act like point sources of light. Their light undergoes repeated atmospheric refraction as it passes through layers of air with changing refractive indices. This changes the apparent brightness and position of the star, causing twinkling.

Planets are closer and appear as extended sources. Light from different parts of a planet undergoes different changes, but the overall effect averages out. So planets generally do not twinkle.

## 7. Explain why the sky is blue and why the Sun appears red at sunrise and sunset.

The sky appears blue because tiny particles and air molecules in the atmosphere scatter shorter wavelength blue light more strongly than longer wavelength red light. This scattered blue light enters our eyes.

At sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels through a longer path in the atmosphere. Most of the blue light is scattered away before reaching us. The longer wavelength red and orange light reaches our eyes, so the Sun appears red.
