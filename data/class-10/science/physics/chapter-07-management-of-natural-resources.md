# Chapter 6: Management of Natural Resources

## Chapter Overview

Natural resources are materials and energy sources provided by nature that humans use for survival and development. Examples include air, water, soil, forests, wildlife, coal, petroleum, minerals, and sunlight.

This chapter explains why natural resources must be managed carefully. Human population growth, industrial development, pollution, overuse of resources, and unequal distribution create pressure on the environment. Sustainable management means using resources in a way that meets present needs without destroying the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

The chapter focuses mainly on:

- Conservation of natural resources
- The 3Rs: Reduce, Recycle, Reuse
- Sustainable development
- Forest and wildlife management
- Role of stakeholders in forest conservation
- Water resource management
- Dams and their problems
- Water harvesting
- Coal and petroleum conservation

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Natural Resources

Natural resources are materials available in nature that support life and human activities.

Examples:

- Forests
- Wildlife
- Water
- Soil
- Minerals
- Coal
- Petroleum
- Air
- Sunlight

Natural resources are not unlimited. Some can be renewed if managed properly, while others take millions of years to form and can be exhausted.

---

### 2. Need for Management of Natural Resources

Natural resources must be managed because:

- They are limited.
- Human population is increasing rapidly.
- Demand for food, water, energy, buildings, transport, and industries is increasing.
- Resources should be available for future generations.
- Overuse causes pollution and environmental damage.
- Resources should be distributed fairly among all people, not only among rich or powerful groups.
- Extraction and use of resources produce waste, which must be disposed of safely.

Good resource management requires a long-term view.

---

### 3. Sustainable Development

Sustainable development means development that meets the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

It includes:

- Careful use of resources
- Conservation of biodiversity
- Pollution control
- Equal distribution of resources
- Waste reduction
- Protection of local communities
- Protection of future generations

## Management of Natural Resources

### 1. Ganga Action Plan and Water Pollution

The Ganga Action Plan was started around 1985 because the water quality of the river Ganga had become very poor.

The Ganga flows about 2500 km from Gangotri in the Himalayas to Ganga Sagar in the Bay of Bengal. More than 100 towns in Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Bengal are located on its banks.

Major causes of Ganga pollution:

- Sewage from towns
- Industrial waste
- Human activities like bathing and washing clothes
- Ashes and dead bodies being released into the river
- Chemical discharge from industries

A high number of coliform bacteria in water indicates contamination by disease-causing microorganisms. Coliform bacteria are normally found in the human intestine. Their presence in water shows that the water is polluted by sewage.

---

### 2. Measuring Water Quality

Water quality can be measured using different factors.

Important indicators include:

- pH of water
- Dissolved oxygen
- Biological contamination
- Chemical pollutants
- Coliform bacteria count

Some pollutants are harmful even in very small quantities and require advanced instruments for detection.

## The 3Rs: Reduce, Recycle, Reuse

The 3Rs help reduce pressure on natural resources and decrease environmental pollution.

### Reduce

Reduce means using fewer resources and avoiding wastage.

Examples:

- Switch off fans and lights when not needed.
- Repair leaking taps.
- Avoid wasting food.
- Use less plastic.
- Use public transport or walk when possible.
- Save paper.

Reduce is the best option because it prevents unnecessary resource use from the beginning.

---

### Recycle

Recycle means converting used materials into new useful products.

Examples of recyclable materials:

- Paper
- Plastic
- Glass
- Metals

For recycling to work properly, waste must be separated before disposal. Recyclable waste should not be mixed with wet waste or non-recyclable garbage.

---

### Reuse

Reuse means using the same object again and again instead of throwing it away.

Examples:

- Reusing envelopes
- Using plastic containers for storage
- Reusing glass bottles
- Using cloth bags instead of disposable bags
- Donating old clothes or books

Reuse is often better than recycling because recycling requires energy.

## Forests and Wildlife

### 1. Forests as Biodiversity Hotspots

Forests are biodiversity hotspots because they contain many different species of plants, animals, fungi, microorganisms, insects, birds, reptiles, and other organisms.

Biodiversity includes:

- Variety of species
- Variety of life forms
- Genetic diversity
- Ecosystem diversity

Loss of biodiversity can disturb ecological balance.

---

### 2. Importance of Forests

Forests are important because they:

- Provide oxygen
- Absorb carbon dioxide
- Maintain the water cycle
- Prevent soil erosion
- Provide habitat for wildlife
- Support biodiversity
- Provide fuelwood, fruits, nuts, medicines, bamboo, timber, and fodder
- Support local communities
- Help maintain rainfall patterns
- Protect soil and water resources

---

## Stakeholders in Forest Conservation

A stakeholder is a person or group that has an interest in a resource.

In forest conservation, four main stakeholders are discussed.

| Stakeholder | Role / Interest |
|---|---|
| Local people living in or near forests | Depend on forests for fuelwood, fodder, bamboo, fruits, nuts, medicines, grazing, tools, and livelihood |
| Forest Department | Owns and controls forest resources on behalf of the government |
| Industrialists | Use forest products such as timber, tendu leaves, paper pulp, lac, bamboo, and raw materials |
| Nature and wildlife enthusiasts | Want to conserve forests and wildlife in their original natural form |

---

### Local People and Forests

People living near forests depend on forests for many daily needs:

- Fuelwood
- Small timber
- Bamboo for huts and storage
- Fodder for animals
- Fruits and nuts
- Medicinal plants
- Wood for agricultural tools
- Wood for fishing and hunting tools
- Grazing land for animals

Traditional local communities often used forests in a sustainable way before forest control shifted to colonial and later centralized systems.

---

### Industrial Use of Forests

Industries use forest products as raw materials.

Examples:

- Timber industry
- Paper industry
- Lac industry
- Sports goods industry
- Beedi industry using tendu leaves

Industrialists often focus on raw material supply and profit. If their use is not controlled, forests can be overexploited.

---

### Problems of Monoculture Plantations

Sometimes forests are replaced by plantations of only one type of tree, such as pine, teak, or eucalyptus.

Problems:

- Biodiversity decreases.
- Local people lose access to useful forest products.
- Fodder, fruits, nuts, and medicinal plants become unavailable.
- Ecological stability is disturbed.
- Forests become more useful to industries than local communities.

## Conservation Examples

### 1. Bishnoi Community and Amrita Devi Bishnoi

The Bishnoi community of Rajasthan has a tradition of protecting forests and wildlife as part of its religious practice.

Amrita Devi Bishnoi sacrificed her life in 1731 in Khejrali village near Jodhpur, Rajasthan, to protect khejri trees. Along with her, 363 people also sacrificed their lives.

In her memory, the Government of India instituted the Amrita Devi Bishnoi National Award for wildlife conservation.

---

### 2. Chipko Movement

The Chipko Movement started in the Garhwal Himalayas in the early 1970s.

Cause:

- A contractor was given permission to cut trees near a village.
- Local people opposed this because they depended on the forest.
- When contractor workers came to cut the trees, village women hugged the trees and prevented them from being cut.

Meaning of Chipko:

- “Chipko” means “to hug” or “to stick to”.

Importance:

- It showed that local people must be involved in forest management.
- It questioned commercial exploitation of forests.
- It spread awareness about sustainable use of forests.
- It forced the government to rethink forest policies.

---

### 3. People's Participation in Forest Management: Arabari Example

In 1972, the West Bengal Forest Department changed its forest management strategy in the Arabari forest area of Midnapore.

Important points:

- Local villagers were involved in protecting badly degraded sal forests.
- About 1272 hectares of sal forest were protected.
- Villagers received employment for forest protection.
- They were allowed to collect fuelwood and graze cattle at low cost.
- They were given rights over 25% of the forest produce.
- By 1983, the Arabari sal forest became rich and valuable.

This example shows that forest conservation becomes more effective when local communities are involved.

## Water for All

Water is a basic need for all living organisms. Human activities have affected both water quality and water availability.

Main problems related to water:

- Pollution of rivers, lakes, ponds, and groundwater
- Unequal distribution of water
- Overuse of groundwater
- Deforestation reducing groundwater recharge
- Industrial waste
- Sewage discharge
- Agricultural demand for water
- Seasonal rainfall due to monsoon dependence

Water scarcity is often closely related to poverty.

---

## Traditional Water Management

Before large government-controlled projects became common, many local communities managed their own water systems.

Examples:

- Local tanks
- Ponds
- Small canals
- Check dams
- Traditional irrigation channels
- Rainwater harvesting systems

These systems were managed according to local needs and local experience.

---

### Kulhs in Himachal Pradesh

Kulhs were traditional canal irrigation systems developed in parts of Himachal Pradesh about 400 years ago.

Features:

- Water from streams was carried through small human-made channels.
- Water was distributed among villages by mutual agreement.
- During the cropping season, water was first given to distant villages and then to villages located higher up.
- Two or three people were appointed to manage and maintain the kulhs.
- Villagers paid them for their work.
- Kulhs also helped groundwater recharge.

After government takeover, many kulhs became inactive and the earlier community-based distribution system declined.

## Dams

### Why Dams Are Built

Large dams are built to:

- Store large amounts of water
- Provide irrigation
- Generate electricity
- Supply water to distant places through canals
- Control floods in some cases

Example:

- The Indira Gandhi Canal brought greenery to large parts of Rajasthan.

---

### Problems Caused by Large Dams

Large dams are opposed mainly because of three types of problems.

| Type of Problem | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Social problems | Large numbers of farmers and tribal people are displaced. Many do not receive proper compensation or rehabilitation. |
| Economic problems | Large dams require huge public money, but the benefits may not match the cost. |
| Environmental problems | Forests are destroyed, biodiversity is lost, and ecosystems are disturbed. |

Examples mentioned:

- Tehri Dam on the Ganga
- Narmada Bachao Andolan against increasing the height of dams on the Narmada river
- Tawa Dam, where displaced people did not receive promised benefits for a long time

---

## Water Harvesting

Water harvesting means collecting and storing rainwater or runoff water for future use and groundwater recharge.

Main aims:

- Conserve water
- Recharge groundwater
- Reduce drought impact
- Reduce flood impact
- Increase biomass production
- Improve local water availability
- Support agriculture
- Reduce dependence on large dams

---

### Methods of Water Harvesting

Different regions of India use different traditional systems.

| Region | Traditional Water Harvesting System |
|---|---|
| Rajasthan | Khadins, tanks, nadis |
| Maharashtra | Bandharas, tals |
| Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh | Bundhis |
| Bihar | Ahars and pynes |
| Himachal Pradesh | Kulhs |
| Jammu region | Ponds |
| Tamil Nadu | Eris / tanks |
| Kerala | Surangams |
| Karnataka | Kattas |

---

### Check Dams

Check dams are small barriers built across seasonal streams or water channels.

They may be made of:

- Concrete
- Small stones
- Pebbles
- Local materials

Benefits:

- Stop rainwater runoff
- Allow water to collect behind the dam
- Recharge groundwater
- Improve soil moisture
- Support vegetation
- Reduce soil erosion

---

### Advantages of Groundwater Storage

Storing water underground has several benefits:

- It does not evaporate easily.
- It spreads out underground and recharges wells.
- It provides moisture to vegetation over a large area.
- It does not provide breeding ground for mosquitoes like stagnant surface water.
- It is less likely to be contaminated by human and animal waste than open ponds and lakes.

---

## Coal and Petroleum

Coal and petroleum are fossil fuels. They formed from the remains of living organisms over millions of years.

They are important sources of energy, but they are non-renewable and limited.

Estimated availability mentioned in the chapter:

- Petroleum may last for about 40 years if used at the current rate.
- Coal may last for about 200 years if used at the current rate.

---

### Why Coal and Petroleum Must Be Conserved

Coal and petroleum must be conserved because:

- They are limited.
- They take millions of years to form.
- They will eventually be exhausted.
- Their burning causes pollution.
- They release greenhouse gases.
- They are important raw materials for many industries.
- Future generations also need energy resources.

---

### Pollution from Burning Fossil Fuels

Coal and petroleum contain carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and sulphur.

When burned, they produce:

- Carbon dioxide
- Water vapour
- Oxides of nitrogen
- Oxides of sulphur
- Carbon monoxide if combustion is incomplete

Harmful effects:

- Carbon dioxide increases the greenhouse effect and global warming.
- Oxides of nitrogen and sulphur cause acid rain.
- Carbon monoxide is poisonous.
- Fossil fuel combustion causes air pollution.

---

### Reducing Coal and Petroleum Use

Ways to reduce consumption:

- Use public transport.
- Walk or cycle for short distances.
- Use fuel-efficient vehicles.
- Use energy-efficient appliances.
- Switch off lights, fans, and devices when not needed.
- Use stairs instead of lifts when possible.
- Wear warm clothes instead of using heaters excessively.
- Improve engine efficiency.
- Use renewable energy sources.

## Long Answer Questions

### Q1. Explain why natural resource management is necessary.

Natural resource management is necessary because resources are limited and human demand is increasing rapidly. Population growth, industrialization, agriculture, transport, and urbanization put pressure on forests, water, fossil fuels, minerals, and soil. If resources are used carelessly, they may be exhausted or polluted. Proper management ensures equal distribution, protects the environment, reduces waste, and keeps resources available for future generations. It also supports sustainable development.

---

### Q2. Explain the importance of the 3Rs in environmental conservation.

The 3Rs are Reduce, Recycle, and Reuse. Reduce means using fewer resources and avoiding wastage, such as saving electricity and water. Recycle means converting waste materials like paper, glass, plastic, and metals into useful products. Reuse means using the same object again, such as using old bottles or envelopes. These methods reduce waste, lower pressure on natural resources, save energy, and decrease pollution. Reuse is often better than recycling because recycling also consumes energy.

---

### Q3. Explain the role of local people in forest conservation.

Local people depend on forests for fuelwood, fodder, bamboo, fruits, nuts, medicines, grazing, and livelihood. Because they directly depend on forests, their participation is essential in conservation. Traditional communities often know sustainable methods of using forest resources. The Chipko Movement and Arabari forest example show that forest management becomes more effective when local communities are involved. Ignoring local people may cause conflict and poor forest management.

---

### Q4. What were the causes and importance of the Chipko Movement?

The Chipko Movement began when a contractor was allowed to cut trees near a village in the Garhwal Himalayas. Local people opposed this because they depended on the forest. When contractor workers came to cut trees, village women hugged the trees and stopped them. The movement became important because it showed that local communities must have a role in forest management. It also highlighted the need for sustainable forest use and questioned commercial exploitation of forests.

---

### Q5. Explain the problems associated with large dams.

Large dams store water and generate electricity, but they also create major problems. Socially, they displace farmers and tribal people, and rehabilitation is often poor. Economically, they require huge investment and may not provide benefits equal to the cost. Environmentally, they destroy forests, reduce biodiversity, disturb ecosystems, and submerge large areas of land. Therefore, large dams must be planned carefully and alternatives like local water harvesting should be considered.

---

### Q6. Explain water harvesting and its advantages.

Water harvesting is the collection and storage of rainwater or runoff water. It may be done through khadins, tanks, nadis, bandharas, tals, bundhis, ahars, pynes, kulhs, ponds, surangams, kattas, check dams, and rooftop collection. Its advantages include groundwater recharge, improved water availability, reduced drought and flood impact, better soil moisture, support for vegetation, and local control over water resources. It is often cheaper and more sustainable than large water projects.

---

### Q7. Why should coal and petroleum be conserved?

Coal and petroleum are fossil fuels formed over millions of years. They are non-renewable and limited. If used at the current rate, petroleum may last about 40 years and coal about 200 years. Burning them releases carbon dioxide, oxides of nitrogen and sulphur, and sometimes carbon monoxide. These cause air pollution, acid rain, greenhouse effect, and global warming. Therefore, they must be used judiciously and alternative energy sources should be developed.
