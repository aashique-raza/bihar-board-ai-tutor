# Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

## 1. Chapter Overview

This chapter explains how substances change during chemical reactions, how reactions are represented using chemical equations, why equations must be balanced, and how different types of reactions can be identified.

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances change into new substances with different properties. The starting substances are called reactants, and the new substances formed are called products.

In daily life, chemical reactions occur when milk turns sour, iron rusts, grapes ferment, food is cooked, food is digested, and respiration takes place in our body.

---

## 2. Learning Goals

After studying this chapter, a student should be able to:

- Explain what a chemical reaction is.
- Identify signs of a chemical reaction.
- Write word equations and chemical equations.
- Explain why chemical equations are balanced.
- Balance simple chemical equations.
- Write physical state symbols in chemical equations.
- Identify combination, decomposition, displacement, double displacement, precipitation, oxidation, reduction, and redox reactions.
- Explain corrosion and rancidity with examples.
- Solve common board exam questions from this chapter.

## 3. Chemical Reaction

A chemical reaction is a process in which reactants are converted into products.

### Example

When magnesium ribbon burns in air, it reacts with oxygen and forms magnesium oxide.

Equation:

```text
Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
```

Chemical equation:

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

Here:

- Magnesium and oxygen are reactants.
- Magnesium oxide is the product.

---

## 4. Signs of a Chemical Reaction

A chemical reaction may be identified by one or more of the following observations:

1. Change in state
2. Change in colour
3. Evolution of gas
4. Change in temperature
5. Formation of a precipitate
6. Production of light or heat

### Examples

| Observation | Example |
|---|---|
| Colour change | Copper sulphate solution changes colour when iron nails are dipped in it. |
| Gas evolution | Zinc reacts with dilute acid and produces hydrogen gas. |
| Temperature change | Calcium oxide reacts with water and releases heat. |
| Precipitate formation | Barium chloride reacts with sodium sulphate and forms white barium sulphate precipitate. |
| Light and heat | Magnesium burns with a bright white flame. |

---

## 5. Chemical Equation

A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae.

### Word Equation

```text
Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
```

### Chemical Equation

```text
Mg + O₂ → MgO
```

This equation is not balanced because the number of oxygen atoms is not equal on both sides.

Balanced equation:

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

---

## 6. Reactants and Products

Reactants are substances that take part in a chemical reaction.

Products are substances formed as a result of the chemical reaction.

Example:

```text
Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
```

Reactants:

- Zinc
- Sulphuric acid

Products:

- Zinc sulphate
- Hydrogen gas

---

## 7. Balanced Chemical Equation

A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the equation.

### Why is balancing necessary?

Balancing is necessary because of the law of conservation of mass.

According to the law of conservation of mass, mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. Therefore, the number of atoms of each element must be the same before and after the reaction.

### Example

Unbalanced equation:

```text
Mg + O₂ → MgO
```

Balanced equation:

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

---

## 8. How to Balance a Chemical Equation

### Example: Reaction of iron with steam

Unbalanced equation:

```text
Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
```

Balanced equation:

```text
3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂
```

### Steps

1. Count the number of atoms of each element on both sides.
2. Choose the compound with the highest number of atoms.
3. Balance one element at a time.
4. Do not change chemical formulae.
5. Only change coefficients.
6. Recheck all atoms after balancing.

### Important Rule

Wrong:

```text
H₂O₄
```

Correct:

```text
4H₂O
```

You cannot change the formula of a compound. You can only place coefficients before formulae.

## 9. Physical States in Chemical Equations

Physical states make chemical equations more informative.

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| (s) | Solid |
| (l) | Liquid |
| (g) | Gas |
| (aq) | Aqueous solution |

Example:

```text
3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) → Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)
```

Here, H₂O is shown as steam because its state is gas.

# 11. Types of Chemical Reactions

## 11.1 Combination Reaction

A combination reaction is a reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product.

General form:

```text
A + B → AB
```

### Example 1: Calcium oxide reacts with water

```text
CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + Heat
```

Calcium oxide reacts with water to form calcium hydroxide. A large amount of heat is released.

This reaction is also an exothermic reaction.

### Example 2: Burning of carbon

```text
C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
```

### Example 3: Formation of water

```text
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)
```

---

## 11.2 Exothermic Reaction

An exothermic reaction is a chemical reaction in which heat is released along with the formation of products.

### Examples

1. Burning of natural gas:

```text
CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) + Energy
```

2. Respiration:

```text
C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) + 6O₂(aq) → 6CO₂(aq) + 6H₂O(l) + Energy
```

3. Decomposition of vegetable matter into compost.

### Why is respiration exothermic?

Respiration is exothermic because glucose reacts with oxygen inside cells and releases energy needed by the body.

---

## 11.3 Decomposition Reaction

A decomposition reaction is a reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

General form:

```text
AB → A + B
```

Decomposition reactions usually require energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity.

---

## 11.4 Thermal Decomposition

Thermal decomposition is decomposition caused by heat.

### Example 1: Heating ferrous sulphate

```text
2FeSO₄(s) --heat--> Fe₂O₃(s) + SO₂(g) + SO₃(g)
```

Ferrous sulphate crystals lose water and decompose to form ferric oxide, sulphur dioxide, and sulphur trioxide.

Observation:

- Green crystals change colour.
- A characteristic smell of sulphur dioxide is produced.

### Example 2: Heating calcium carbonate

```text
CaCO₃(s) --heat--> CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
```

Calcium carbonate decomposes into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.

Calcium oxide is also called quicklime.

### Example 3: Heating lead nitrate

```text
2Pb(NO₃)₂(s) --heat--> 2PbO(s) + 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g)
```

Observation:

- Brown fumes of nitrogen dioxide are evolved.

---

## 11.5 Electrolytic Decomposition

Electrolytic decomposition is decomposition caused by electricity.

### Example: Electrolysis of water

```text
2H₂O(l) --electricity--> 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
```

During electrolysis of water:

- Hydrogen gas is collected at the cathode.
- Oxygen gas is collected at the anode.
- The volume of hydrogen gas is double the volume of oxygen gas.

Reason:

Water contains hydrogen and oxygen in a 2:1 ratio.

---

## 11.6 Photochemical Decomposition

Photochemical decomposition is decomposition caused by light.

### Example 1: Silver chloride in sunlight

```text
2AgCl(s) --sunlight--> 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g)
```

Silver chloride is white, but it turns grey in sunlight because silver metal is formed.

### Example 2: Silver bromide in sunlight

```text
2AgBr(s) --sunlight--> 2Ag(s) + Br₂(g)
```

This reaction is used in black-and-white photography.

---

## 11.7 Endothermic Reaction

An endothermic reaction is a reaction in which energy is absorbed.

Examples:

- Decomposition of calcium carbonate by heat
- Electrolysis of water
- Decomposition of silver chloride by sunlight
- Reaction of barium hydroxide with ammonium chloride

---

## 11.8 Displacement Reaction

A displacement reaction is a reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

General form:

```text
A + BC → AC + B
```

### Example 1: Iron displaces copper

```text
Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
```

Observation:

- Blue colour of copper sulphate solution fades.
- Iron nail gets a brown coating of copper.

Reason:

Iron is more reactive than copper, so it displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

### Example 2: Zinc displaces copper

```text
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
```

### Example 3: Lead displaces copper

```text
Pb(s) + CuCl₂(aq) → PbCl₂(aq) + Cu(s)
```

---

## 11.9 Double Displacement Reaction

A double displacement reaction is a reaction in which ions are exchanged between two reactants to form new products.

General form:

```text
AB + CD → AD + CB
```

### Example

```text
Na₂SO₄(aq) + BaCl₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
```

Here:

- Sodium sulphate reacts with barium chloride.
- White precipitate of barium sulphate is formed.
- Sodium chloride remains in solution.

---

## 11.10 Precipitation Reaction

A precipitation reaction is a reaction in which an insoluble solid is formed.

The insoluble solid formed is called a precipitate.

Example:

```text
Na₂SO₄(aq) + BaCl₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
```

Barium sulphate is an insoluble white precipitate.

Another example:

```text
Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI₂(s) + 2KNO₃(aq)
```

Lead iodide is a yellow precipitate.

---

# 12. Oxidation and Reduction

## 12.1 Oxidation

Oxidation is a reaction in which:

- Oxygen is added to a substance, or
- Hydrogen is removed from a substance.

Example:

```text
2Cu + O₂ --heat--> 2CuO
```

Copper reacts with oxygen to form copper oxide.

Copper is oxidised.

---

## 12.2 Reduction

Reduction is a reaction in which:

- Oxygen is removed from a substance, or
- Hydrogen is added to a substance.

Example:

```text
CuO + H₂ --heat--> Cu + H₂O
```

Copper oxide loses oxygen and forms copper.

Copper oxide is reduced.

Hydrogen gains oxygen and forms water.

Hydrogen is oxidised.

---

## 12.3 Redox Reaction

A redox reaction is a reaction in which oxidation and reduction occur at the same time.

Example:

```text
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
```

In this reaction:

- CuO is reduced to Cu.
- H₂ is oxidised to H₂O.

Other examples:

```text
ZnO + C → Zn + CO
```

In this reaction:

- ZnO is reduced to Zn.
- Carbon is oxidised to CO.

```text
MnO₂ + 4HCl → MnCl₂ + 2H₂O + Cl₂
```

In this reaction:

- HCl is oxidised to Cl₂.
- MnO₂ is reduced to MnCl₂.

---

# 13. Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

## 13.1 Corrosion

Corrosion is the process in which a metal is slowly destroyed by the action of air, moisture, acids, or other substances around it.

### Example: Rusting of iron

Iron objects become covered with a reddish-brown layer. This is called rusting.

Rusting occurs when iron reacts with oxygen and moisture.

### Other examples of corrosion

- Silver becomes black.
- Copper develops a green coating.
- Iron railings, bridges, ships, and car bodies get damaged.

### Why is corrosion harmful?

Corrosion weakens metal objects and causes financial loss because damaged metal parts need repair or replacement.

### How can corrosion be prevented?

- Painting
- Oiling
- Greasing
- Galvanisation
- Keeping metals dry
- Alloy formation

---

## 13.2 Rancidity

Rancidity is the process in which fats and oils get oxidised and develop an unpleasant smell and taste.

### Example

Old oily food or fried food may smell bad because fats and oils have become rancid.

### How can rancidity be prevented?

- Adding antioxidants
- Keeping food in airtight containers
- Refrigeration
- Flushing packets with nitrogen gas
- Keeping food away from light and heat

### Why are chips packets filled with nitrogen gas?

Nitrogen is less reactive than oxygen. It prevents oxidation of oils and fats in chips and keeps them fresh for a longer time.

# 16. Difference-Based Answers

## 16.1 Combination Reaction vs Decomposition Reaction

| Combination Reaction | Decomposition Reaction |
|---|---|
| Two or more reactants combine to form one product. | One reactant breaks into two or more products. |
| General form: A + B → AB | General form: AB → A + B |
| Example: CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ | Example: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ |

---

## 16.2 Displacement Reaction vs Double Displacement Reaction

| Displacement Reaction | Double Displacement Reaction |
|---|---|
| One element displaces another element from a compound. | Two compounds exchange ions. |
| Usually involves an element and a compound. | Usually involves two compounds. |
| General form: A + BC → AC + B | General form: AB + CD → AD + CB |
| Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu | Example: Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl |

---

## 16.3 Oxidation vs Reduction

| Oxidation | Reduction |
|---|---|
| Addition of oxygen | Removal of oxygen |
| Removal of hydrogen | Addition of hydrogen |
| Example: 2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO | Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O |

---

## 16.4 Exothermic Reaction vs Endothermic Reaction

| Exothermic Reaction | Endothermic Reaction |
|---|---|
| Releases heat | Absorbs heat |
| Products form with energy release | Energy is needed for reaction |
| Example: Respiration | Example: Decomposition of CaCO₃ |

# 17. Board Exam Style Questions and Answers

## Question 1: What is a chemical reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances are converted into new substances with different properties. The starting substances are called reactants and the new substances formed are called products.

Example:

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

---

## Question 2: Why should a magnesium ribbon be cleaned before burning?

Magnesium ribbon should be cleaned before burning because it has a thin layer of magnesium oxide on its surface. This oxide layer prevents proper burning. Cleaning with sandpaper removes the oxide layer and allows magnesium to react easily with oxygen.

---

## Question 3: What is a balanced chemical equation?

A balanced chemical equation is an equation in which the number of atoms of each element is equal on the reactant side and the product side.

Example:

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

---

## Question 4: Why is it necessary to balance a chemical equation?

It is necessary to balance a chemical equation because mass is conserved in a chemical reaction. Atoms are neither created nor destroyed. Therefore, the number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation.

---

Reaction type:

Combination reaction.

Displacement reaction.

Example:

A decomposition reaction is opposite to a combination reaction because in a combination reaction two or more substances combine to form one product, while in a decomposition reaction one substance breaks down into two or more products.

```text
CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂
```

Decomposition:

```text
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
```

---

A double displacement reaction is a reaction in which two compounds exchange ions to form two new compounds.


---


---

When iron nails are dipped in copper sulphate solution, iron displaces copper from the solution. The blue colour of copper sulphate fades and a brown coating of copper forms on the iron nail.

Equation:

```text

```

---

## Question 15: Define oxidation and reduction.

Oxidation is the addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen from a substance.

Reduction is the removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen to a substance.

Example:

```text
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
```

Here, CuO is reduced and H₂ is oxidised.

---

```text
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
```

- CuO loses oxygen and is reduced to Cu.
- H₂ gains oxygen and is oxidised to H₂O.

---

## Question 17: Identify oxidised and reduced substances in 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O.

```text
4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O
```

Sodium gains oxygen and is oxidised to sodium oxide.

Oxygen does not undergo reduction in the simple oxygen-addition explanation at this level.

Corrosion is the slow destruction of a metal due to the action of air, moisture, acids, or other substances around it.

Rusting of iron.

---

## Question 19: Why do we paint iron objects?

Iron objects are painted to prevent rusting. Paint prevents iron from coming in contact with air and moisture, so corrosion is reduced.

---

Rancidity is the oxidation of fats and oils in food, which causes unpleasant smell and taste.

Example:

Old fried food or oily food develops a bad smell.

---

## Question 21: Why are oil and fat-containing foods flushed with nitrogen?

Oil and fat-containing foods are flushed with nitrogen because nitrogen is less reactive and prevents oxidation. This reduces rancidity and keeps food fresh for a longer time.

---

The brown shiny element X is copper.

Copper oxide.

# 20. Common Mistakes

## Mistake 1: Changing formulae while balancing

Wrong:

```text
H₂O → H₂O₂
```

Correct:

```text
2H₂O
```

Only coefficients can be changed.

---

## Mistake 2: Confusing combination and decomposition

Combination means many reactants form one product.

Decomposition means one reactant forms many products.

---

## Mistake 3: Confusing oxidation and reduction

Oxidation:

- Oxygen added
- Hydrogen removed

Reduction:

- Oxygen removed
- Hydrogen added

---

## Mistake 4: Forgetting physical states

For full chemical equations, physical states may be written:

```text
(s), (l), (g), (aq)
```

---

## Mistake 5: Calling every double displacement reaction precipitation

Not every double displacement reaction is necessarily precipitation. It is called precipitation only when an insoluble solid is formed.

# 22. Ultra-Short Revision

- Chemical reaction forms new substances.
- Reactants change into products.
- Chemical equations represent reactions using formulae.
- Equations must be balanced to obey conservation of mass.
- Combination reaction: two or more substances form one product.
- Decomposition reaction: one substance breaks into two or more products.
- Displacement reaction: more reactive element displaces less reactive element.
- Double displacement reaction: ions are exchanged between two compounds.
- Precipitation reaction forms insoluble solid.
- Oxidation means addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen.
- Reduction means removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen.
- Corrosion damages metals.
- Rancidity spoils fats and oils.
